movie and another $100 million onmarketing is crazy.</p><p> For the same amount of money, Lucas says he couldmake 50-60 movies (and apparently not market them).</p><p> Volume,volume, volume, he insists.That's great.</p><p> He could indeed make 50 low-budget movies for $4million each and surely some of them would click.</p><p> He'd probably endup like France, where the government bankrolls lots of tiny movies thatnobody wants to see.</p><p> Invariably, a FEW of them would gain a wideraudience.</p><p> But that has nothing to do with making blockbusters.</p><p> Anygood studio should include a mix of smaller films with big stars (called"independent" films) and another label for releasing truly small,independent films (called "award winners").</p><p> Some of them willinvariably break out.</p><p> But they won't fill the role of blockbusters like"Pirates of the Caribbean: Dead Man's Chest" or "Spiderman 2" or even"Superman Returns," which -- good or bad -- pack people in.And how does Lucas conceivably justify the ludicrous claim thatAmericans are abandoning the movie-going habit for good.</p><p> No, moviesdon't dominate the landscape the way they did in the Thirties, but byevery conceivable measure, movies are FAR bigger than they have beenfor at least 70 years.</p><p> Tons of people go to the movies.</p><p> And in the last 10years, everyone has learned the joys of owning a library of movies thesame way they've always had a library of music and books.</p><p> More peopleown movies than ever in history.</p><p> More people have access to moremovies than ever in history.</p><p> More movies are watched than ever inhistory.</p><p> FAR more money is spent on movies than ever before inhistory.Just from 1980 to today, you can see movies exploding from a roughly$10 billion industry to easily a $50 billion industry.</p><p> In the US alone.And we still don't have enough screens in China and India and Russia.Meaning there's a lot of room to grow.</p><p> He says TV is the way to go?Guess what?
