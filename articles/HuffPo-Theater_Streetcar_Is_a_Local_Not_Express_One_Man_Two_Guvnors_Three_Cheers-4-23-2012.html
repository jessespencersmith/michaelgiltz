BROADHURST THEATRE This is not A Streetcar Named Desire simply cast with black actors in the lead roles.</p><p> It is a Streetcar untraditionally cast but in a way that is true to New Orleans, where blacks and whites and Latinos live together in a gumbo of love and desire and conflict.</p><p> It's welcome, if nothing else, for showcasing Nicole Ari Parker, who I know best from her sharp and funnysupporting turn in Boogie Nights and the Showtime series Soul Food .</p><p> Parker almost puts her own stamp on Blanche and proves her Broadway debut is a notable one.</p><p> Unfortunately, it comes in a production that doesn't begin to allow Parker's performance to flourish and deepen.</p><p> Blair Underwood -- also making his Broadway debut -- is thoroughly miscast as Stanley.</p><p> to pay off in act two.</p><p> Harris is less effective in his drunken revenge on Blanche and her aria where Blanche confesses every tawdry detail of her fall doesn'thave the power it should.</p><p> Since there's no explosive sexual tension, Stanley's brutal attack comes out ofnowhere and makes little sense.</p><p> Blanche's final goodbye doesn't have the poignancy and heartbreak itshould.</p><p> She is more betrayed than shattered.</p><p> Directed by Emily Mann, this Streetcar has little sense of place despite all the usual trappings.</p><p> In one of the show's biggest disappointments, the music composed by the terrific Terrence Blanchard is merely a bridge between scenes, squawking in effectively at some key moments but essentially just a mood-setter.It doesn't have the impact of embodying the turmoil underneath the drama, perhaps because the drama isn't providing any.</p><p> Blanchard often does much better.</p><p> Similarly, the world of New Orleans is hardly present.</p><p> A jazz funeral crosses the stage, people go here and there and an unnecessary and unconvincing video projection hints at stores and other businesses, but itnever coheres into a community.</p><p> Stanley and Stella's place is more rundown than raffish and with little ofthe charm that New Orleans has even at its most beat-down. (The serviceable set is by Eugene Lee.)Stanley's buddies who come over for poker barely make an impression; the same could be said for theneighbors who live above them, always fighting and making up and fighting again.</p><p> With no world to setthis story in, no community to surround it and no raw passion and danger to inflame it, the ride on thisStreetcar is all too smooth.</p><p> ONE MAN, TWO GUVNORS *** out of **** THE MUSIC BOX If you want a crash course in what makes the British laugh, don't head to London, head to Broadway.</p><p> See Peter and the Starcatcher for its loving nod to pantomimes (a holiday tradition of taking familiar tales and having a good giggle with them).</p><p> Then head to One Man, Two Guvnors , a charming chef's surprise of a comedy that tosses in music hall, vaudeville, slapstick, farce, Shakespearean clowns, lovers in disguise,and birds and blokes who fancy each other and somehow turns it all into a tasty dish.</p><p> It's a diverting treatrather than a substantial meal, but you won't feel guilty about enjoying yourself.</p><p> James Corden (TV's Gavin & Stacey, The History Boys ) holds court as Francis, a working stiff who soon finds himself in the employ of not one but two bosses.</p><p> He's desperately hungry and tries to keep both of them happy until he can finally score some cash and get a proper meal.</p><p> Dolly (Suzie Toase) is an eye-popping distraction as a curvy working girl he wouldn't mind escorting on a holiday, but first things first.Food!
