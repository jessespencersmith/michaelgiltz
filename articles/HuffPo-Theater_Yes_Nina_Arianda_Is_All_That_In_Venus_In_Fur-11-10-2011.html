seeking an actress who can play the sophisticated and witty young woman in a drama about sex and domination based on the classic German novel Venus InFurs by Leopold von Sacher-Masoch.</p><p> When you realize Sacher-Masoch gave masochism its name, you can imagine what sort of book it might be.</p><p> Vonda bulldozes Thomas into letting her audition and the fun begins.</p><p> The ever-shifting power relationship between the two is dizzying: Vonda wants the part so the director Thomas has control over her.</p><p> But the roles they're reading involve a man who is desperate to become the sex slave of a beautifuland sophisticated woman.</p><p> Thomas is in an unhappy relationship but engaged to be married.</p><p> Vondateases and flirts and drives him to distraction.</p><p> Her performance of the part is astounding yet when she dares to criticize a passage as degrading to women Thomas explodes and puts her back in her place.</p><p> Back and forth, from script and audition to discussion and flirtation, past to present, master or mistress to slave -- it's a delight to watch these actors have such a blast with this conceit.</p><p> The emotional high point is a scene when Thomas is lying on a divan and Vonda is behind him like a therapist, quietly and devastatingly detailing his dull and unrewarding existence.</p><p> This tamped-down man needs shaking up, waking up and we want Vonda to do it.</p><p> But that's not exactly what the show intended, I think.</p><p> Hugh Dancy replaced Wes Bentley, who played Thomas in the CSC production.</p><p> Dancy unquestionably delivers a more nuanced and interesting performance but in an odd way this does the play no good since it highlights some of the work's flaws.</p><p> In Bentley's blunt, direct interpretation, Thomas is...how to putthis?...a dick.</p><p> Thomas is an unregenerate jerk who is dismissive of Vonda and women in general.</p><p> He deserves, indeed demands his comeuppance.</p><p> But with Dancy creating a fuller character, Thomas isn't so cut and dried.</p><p> When Vonda criticizes a scene, Thomas lashes out at her, but this seems more likedefensiveness over a weakness in his play, not the boorish behavior of a lout.</p><p> This leads us to the finale.</p><p> Throughout the show, we've been asking, who is this woman? She comes in off the street squawking and chattering but in the blink of an eye embodies an upper-class woman fromthe late 1800s to perfection.</p><p> She's off book (that is, performing the role without consulting the script) almost the entire time, even though she claims to have just glanced at the script on her way to the audition.</p><p> Plus, she seems to know a disconcerting amount about Thomas and his private life.</p><p> Four or fivelikely scenarios spring to mind but the show lands on by far the least interesting and satisfying one.</p><p> It's a deflating climax that weakens everything that came before.</p><p> But don't let that stop you from going.</p><p> Because what came before is Dancy dueling with Arianda and she is indeed a presence to be reckoned with.</p><p> Arianda dominates the stage with the quicksilver changes called for.</p><p> You want to burst into applause again and again throughout the show as she pulls off one marvelousturn after another.</p><p> Bouncing in a chair across the stage in delight, curling her legs around Dancy, revealing layer and layer of intelligence and ferocity just as Vonda takes off layer and layer of clothing, Arianda is simply delightful.</p><p> Even though you've been told again and again how remarkable she is, herMOST POPULAR ON HUFFPOST 1 of 2 Fox News Viewers Know Less Than People Who Watch No News: Study Loyal Dog Refuses To Leave Owner's Grave, Goes Week Without Food Charlie Angus What if They Declared an Emergency and No One Came?
