But the images are so sharp, so clear and striking it takes your breath away and you feel jealous all over again for what they got to experience.</p><p> All these years, the supplemental footage tells us, we've been watching grainy copies of copies of the same two or three moments.</p><p> The footage on display in For All Mankind was simply never seen before, often even by NASA.</p><p> Stanley Kubrick's 2001: A Space Odyssey came out a year before the moon landing and that film is still revelatory.</p><p> Sometimes you might have to remind yourself that this footage is REAL and it's really happening.</p><p> That's how inured to space missions we've become by both the lack of anything beyond the space station (which dulls our realization of how dangerous traveling beyond the Earth can be) and the constant images of it we see in movies.</p><p> But few movies real or fictional are as gorgeous to behold as this one.</p><p> I can't imagine any science teacher who wouldn't want to show this film to their class.</p><p> I can't imagine any kid who would watch this and not spend at least the next few days saying they want tobe an astronaut.</p><p> I can't imagine any adult not seeing it and appreciating anew the beauty of Earth and the foolhardy, wonderful recklessness of what was accomplished 40 years ago.</p><p> For All Mankind is a classic.</p><p> Other Recent Criterion Releases As I've said before, you can never go wrong purchasing any film released by Criterion.</p><p> They're always substantial films produced with loving care and extras that set the industry standard.</p><p> Every special edition DVD put out by any other studio is just imitating what Criterion pioneered.</p><p> They'vealso been smarter than major studios by pricing BluRays the same as their regular DVDs.</p><p> On saleat Amazon, they're increasingly priced even less since sometimes the extras that need to be on a second regular DVD can fit comfortably onto the much roomier BluRay version.</p><p> Here are some recent titles of theirs now available.</p><p> I always try to talk about DVDs within a week or two of theirrelease but Criterion titles are timeless so you can talk about them anytime.</p><p> Last Year at Marienbad ($39.95 for regular or BluRay from Criterion but the regular on sale at Amazon is $34.99 and the BluRay is only $19.99) -- OK, I'm not a fan of director Alain Resnais but he's a director you simply have to deal with if you're any sort of film buff and the enigmatic puzzle that is Last Year At Marienbad is the principal reason why.</p><p> This 1961 New Wave landmark virtually defined one half of the twin poles of foreign films for American audiences.</p><p> On the onehand you had the earthy humor of Fellini and the tantalizing promise of sensuality (foreign films always promised more skin than they delivered but boy were they sophisticated about sex).</p><p> On the other hand, you had the high art pretensions of Godard and Resnais.</p><p> Audiences delighted in tryingto "figure out" this movie in which guests at a famed exclusive resort may or may not have met there the year before and may or may not have had an affair or be having an affair right now.</p><p> It's a lot more fun if you don't try to make sense of it.</p><p> I've compared both the regular DVD and theBluRay and both look gorgeous.</p><p> Both are loaded with extras, including a new audio interview withResnais himself, who is still producing inexplicable but playful films even today.</p><p> My Dinner with Andre ($39.95; Criterion) -- It never seemed that audacious to me.</p><p> A movie about two guys having dinner -- literally a movie in which one guy gets in a cab, arrives and spends almost two hours talking with his friend? Why not? When the two guys are Wallace Shawn andAndre Gregory, it makes even more sense.</p><p> Gregory spins tale after tale during dinner, fantasticalevents from his life that quietly glide into fantasy (a faun? Did he say a faun?) and usually seem to end with everyone in the story drinking and dancing until dawn.</p><p> Director Louis Malle captures it all effortlessly, though the effort in making two guys sitting at a table not become boring seemsdaunting.</p><p> But actually, it's not and those filmmakers who edit with a frenzy even the action-packedmotion of a concert or car chase should take note.</p><p> In fact, movies are FILLED with lengthy scenes in which two people simply talk: the centerpiece of the IRA film Hunger (which opened earlier this year) involves a lengthy talk between a priest and a prisoner) or the opener of Quentin Tarantino's upcoming Inglorious Basterds in which a farmer talks with a Nazi in his farmhouse for about 20 minutes.</p><p> Both are thoroughly gripping.</p><p> And so is My Dinner With Andre which still seems toflummox people with its premise.</p><p> I remember my brother's incredulity when I suggested we watchit years ago. "Two guys.</p><p> Having dinner.</p><p> That's it?"
