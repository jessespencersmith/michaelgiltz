Theater: McKellen! Stewart! Beckett! Pinter! WAITING FOR GODOT ** 1/2 out of **** NO MAN'S LAND *** out of **** CORT THEATRE It's a rare treat on Broadway to have two groups of actors tackling plays in repertory.</p><p> Mark Rylance and friends from the Globe in London are presenting Shakespeare's TwelfthNight and Richard III, while Ian McKellen and Patrick Stewart are reprising their West End success with two modernist classics, Waiting For Godot and No Man's Land.</p><p> They are joined by Billy Crudup and Shuler Hensley and truly half the fun is getting to see theseaccomplished performers in dramatically different settings one right after the other. (Iknow some make it an all day marathon, with one in the afternoon and one at night, butthat's a bit too much nihilism for me.) We can scorecard the plays (Beckett vs.</p><p> Pinter!), we can scorecard the actors (McKellen vs.</p><p> Stewart! Crudup vs.</p><p> Hensley!), and we can scorecard these particular productions.</p><p> Butwhatever your reactions to any particular element (most everyone agrees Twelfth Night is the triumph for Rylance in their two-fer, while opinion is divided on these two), it's safe tosay these are "events" in the best sense of the word, nights of theater you want to see andjudge for yourself.</p><p> In Godot, McKellen and Stewart play Laurel & Hardy in a blasted landscape, forever holding vigil and waiting -- fruitlessly -- for the appearance of Godot.</p><p> Godot is their onlysalvation, the only thing that gives their lives meaning and Godot (spoiler alert!) will nevercome.</p><p> It's easy to understand -- thanks to numerous references to Christ on the cross -- May 16, 2014 This is the print preview: Back to normal view Â» Posted: 12/06/2013 12:44 amwhy Godot is often seen as a stand-in for God.</p><p> But it's as much a mistake to immediately dissect the show for its meaning as it is to fearfully play it as vaudevillian as everyone seems intent on doing in this production.</p><p> Our two Sirs have the right spirit, but Hensley comes barreling in as Pozzo with Lucky (Crudup) on a leash and subtlety or depth of emotion are out of the question.</p><p> I've neverseen a great Godot, but somehow I always love the play itself.</p><p> It's damned difficult to get right (much like Chekhov) and it's always worth trying.</p><p> The set by Stephen Brimson Lewis was evocative enough, though one hole (or is it a shallow grave?) loomed rather large given its lack of importance.</p><p> He also did the clothes and they were spot-on for our two leads, though Pozzo and Lucky's attire pushed the circus shtick too much for my taste.</p><p> That performance aspect is emphasized by Crudup's Kabuki-like makeup and puppet-like hair designed by Tom Watson.</p><p> But the real problem was the lighting by Peter Kaczorowski and sound design by Rob Milburn and Michael Bodeen.</p><p> Every change in time is intensely indicated, with day so clearly day and night so clearly night that no sense of the passage of time can take hold.</p><p> Worst of all is the moon, presented as a large circular white spotlight filling up much of the stage.</p><p> For whatever reason, I found it so banally literal (even though no moon ever cast such a glow) that it yanked me out of the show.</p><p> It was neither subtly realistic enough nor teasingly metaphoric to suggest depths.</p><p> It was just...there.</p><p> As for the sound, the actors were sometimes miked and sometimes not, sometimes their voices echoed -- again, with heavy-handed literalism -- such as when one bellowed into a ditch.</p><p> It called attention to itself, which is something the sound design should never do.Mind you, they weren't helped at the unfortunate performance I saw, which was plaguedby a traffic jam of some sort taking place right outside the Cort Theatre accompanied bymore horn honking than I have ever heard in 20 years of theater-going.</p><p> All of this falls away when you focus on the actors.</p><p> I realize I've missed all of Hensley's most acclaimed performances, such as his recent work in The Whale, so I don't have the fondness for him that I should.</p><p> Crudup is clearly a creature of the stage and has beguiledme many times.</p><p> They are a good physical team, Hensley large and imposing and Crudupthin and pliable.</p><p> If you're going to go for gusto, Hensley is surely right to go all out,drawling and stomping and bellowing non-stop.</p><p> Crudup has almost no dialogue and isstronger for it, doing great bits of physical business.</p><p> He also has the evening's mostdaunting challenge, an epic monologue when Lucky is asked to "think." It's a tumble ofwords and ideas and subconscious despair made vocal and the rhythm of it unfortunatelyescaped him on the night I attended.</p><p> Far better were McKellen and Stewart.</p><p> One could say McKellen wiped the floor with Stewart (certainly my guest did) and there's no question he was mesmerizing as thequarrelsome Estragon, with his ill-fitting shoes and hunger for carrots and even deeperhunger for an end to this endless waiting.</p><p> But I'd say Stewart provided the straight manas Vladimir.</p><p> Their comic interplay -- such as a trading back and forth of hats that's puresilent movie slapstick -- is delightful to behold.</p><p> But exactly how funny is Godot supposed to be? The ads for any production invariably emphasize the humor quotient (understandably) and clearly comedy is present here.</p><p> You'd be foolish not to mine the humor present in the banter and physical bits of business Beckett has offered.</p><p> But this isn't exactly Boeing Boeing and yet that's practically how every production I've seen has tackled it.</p><p> It's as if they're so worried about the show'sdaunting legend as an existential classic that they can't go a minute without a little pie inthe face.</p><p> Audiences are just as eager; they're always keyed up for laughter and seem to giggle out of fear they won't "get" it.</p><p> Hopefully some day I'll see a Godot that actually embraces the despair and bleakness of the story.</p><p> Let the humor pierce the gloom rather than laugh-track it out of existence.</p><p> Pinter's No Man's Land is just as unsettling, though its setting seems a tad more solid.</p><p> Two mature gentlemen, one well-dressed (Stewart as Hirst) and one down on his luck(McKellen as the threadbare Spooner) are having drinks in an elegant if bare sitting room.</p><p> Spooner holds forth while the dour (drunk? tired?) Hirst offers up a rare grunt of encouragement.</p><p> When we think we know what's going on (they met at a pub or perhapsthe rambles near a pub) and suspect a certain sexual undercurrent, our expectations aredisturbed.</p><p> In burst two men, clearly servants of Hirst, the burly Briggs (Hensley) and thestreet-wise talker Foster (Crudup).</p><p> A battle of wills takes place as the two shady figurestry to maintain their hold on this wealthy patron.</p><p> All of this is upended yet again in act two, where suddenly Hirst and Spooner seem to be old acquaintances, with Hirst blithely telling Spooner he had a torrid affair with the other man's wife.</p><p> Again and again, every time we think we sense the way things are going, our understanding is twisted in a new way.</p><p> It's delightfully disorienting and this time every element of the production is in key.</p><p> Unfortunately, Hensley and Crudup do not convey the crucial menace that would turn this take on Pinter from a strong outing to a stellar one.</p><p> You should fear death when they turn on you; here, at most, you worry they might be rather chagrined.</p><p> Still everyone involved has high points that stay with you.</p><p> Hensley is masterful when telling a story about meeting his partner Foster.</p><p> It involves a section of town bedeviled by one-way streets: you can get in there but you can never get out and some people have wandered them for years with no end in sight.</p><p> Crudup's accent may be a little dodgy but he repeatedly offers subtle bits of business that enliven the proceedings without everupstaging the others, a model of skill and restraint.</p><p> But Stewart and McKellen are remarkable here.</p><p> McKellen continues his excellent work from Godot while Stewart delivers one of his best stage performances I've seen, ranging from helpless old man to powerful figure to blue-blooded indifference without evermissing a beat.</p><p> His befuddled, confused character at the start is a marvel, with McKellenglorying in intense dialogue while Stewart returns serve with the merest raising of aneyebrow.</p><p> The second act monologue where he expounds on dallying with McKellen's wifeand then dismisses it all as so much fluff with a wave of his hand and an indifferentphrase is hilarious.</p><p> So much is embedded in this work, this constant battle between forces: the rich versus the poor, the employed versus the unemployed, the successful versus the failure, the talentedversus the hack, the sober versus the drunk, the sane versus the senile, the straight versusthe gay, the young versus the old, the sexually active versus the dormant (or extinct) andon and on.</p><p> And surely dementia -- so much better understood today by so many -- loomsover this play for us more prominently than it did on its debut.</p><p> McKellen's plea for a job, eloquent and insistent and overflowing, goes on and on and on...and then he adds one more thing, pirouetting into an offer to stage a reading for theapparently esteemed man of letters that is Hirst.</p><p> The castle has not been breeched butMcKellen will not stop looking for a weakness in the walls.</p><p> On and on he talks, leaving usall spellbound.</p><p> And then Stewart says simply, "Let's change the subject," to gales oflaughter.</p><p> But it's a wincing laughter as we see the desperation in McKellen's eyes, not tomention the relief in the two servants who immediately belittle McKellen and work toshore up their own positions.</p><p> Director Sean Mathias hasn't nailed these shows to the wallbut he has at least provided an environment where moments of greatness can be achieved.Crudup is surely taking notes since he'll be handling the leads in years to come.</p><p> Pinter's play is every bit the blasted landscape of Beckett's Godot despite its literal setting (the ceiling is topped by shadows of trees and open to the sky, an evocative touch that gives McKellen a funny bit of business early on).</p><p> Both are set in a no man's land of hopelessness, though how hopeless can things be when art still offers solace? That's the real tug of war between these shows: they see the abyss so clearly, we are fooled intobelieving we can laugh it off or at least skirt around it.</p><p> For a while.</p><p> P.S.</p><p> Stewart hasn't performed his one-man A Christmas Carol in the US in more than a decade.</p><p> He last did it in London eight years ago.</p><p> Surely, 2014 or 2015 will prove long enough to wait?
