But this one strikes a clever balance.</p><p> You get 32 DVDs with all 125 episodes from the eight season run of the Emmy-winning series starring Tony Shalhoub as the man more likely to wipe down a crime scene than preserve it. (Such a mess!) It's packed with the extras and commentaries we expect nowadays.</p><p> And the box itself is an impressive rectangle that can fit sideways on a shelf or nestle on your coffee table if you so choose.</p><p> But there's one more option.</p><p> You can remove the plastic sleeve that protects it and crack the boxed set in half: suddenly you have all eight seasons nicely displayed in a manner that will fit snugly even on shallowshelves built for DVDs.</p><p> Each season is in order and offered in its own DVD set for easy access.</p><p> Finicky fans can heave a sigh of relief.</p><p> It's a very nice job that won't take up too much extra space.</p><p> Or -- as with so many massive boxed sets -- you can just remove the eight seasons and put the boxed set itself in thecloset.</p><p> I'm one of the annoying fans who still mourns the loss of Bitty Schram.</p><p> But Monk himself is a unique and wonderful character.</p><p> And where, I wonder, does he fit into the pantheon of top TV crime solvers? And who ARE the top TV crime solvers? You don't need to be Ellery Queen to know a list iscoming.</p><p> I've avoided just regular old cops and super heroes and procedural shows like Law & Order or CSI, which are more about the cases than the people solving them.</p><p> I can't quite put the distinction into words, but I know a crime solver when I see one.</p><p> TV'S TOP 10 CRIME SOLVERS1.</p><p> Jim Rockford /The Rockford Files -- A childhood favorite.</p><p> Jim Rockford is the Everyman detective, a guy struggling to pay his bills who'd rather talk than fight and rarely carries a gun.</p><p> It's as close as TV ever got to John D.</p><p> MacDonald's Travis McGee.</p><p> Darn close, actually.</p><p> Jim Rockford just felt real, the same way Hill Street Blues seemed to capture (in dramatic fashion) what being a cop on the streets was actually like. 2.</p><p> Sherlock Holmes by Jeremy Brett/ The Adventures of Sherlock Holmes -- TV had tackled Holmes (the greatest detective of them all) many times before and will many times in the future (including a cheeky modern update airing on PBS called Sherlock that begins this Sunday.</p><p> But Jeremy Brett has surely put his definitive stamp on the role that all others will be judged by for generations to come.Quirky, moody, addictive, compelling, intellectually alive, manic -- his Holmes is electric.</p><p> And he isfeatured in a series that was perfectly in tune with the stories as written by Sir Arthur Conan Doyle.</p><p> No fancy trappings or rethinking of the role - just pitch perfect period settings with the intelligence and humanity of real people, rather than cardboard characters. 3.</p><p> Jane Tennison /Prime Suspect -- To be honest, after the first two miniseries installments, the Prime Suspect franchise is quite variable in quality, ranging from solid to okay.</p><p> But no apologies are necessary for DCI Jane Tennison, one of the great characters in TV history.</p><p> Wonderfully flawed, she is above all great at her job.</p><p> And those first two installments in her story -- two complete miniseries that tackle racism, sexism, class and so much more -- are TV at its absolute peak. 4.</p><p> Maddie Hayes and David Addison /Moonlighting -- If we were talking about movie crime solvers, you can be certain The Thin Man series would be right towards the top.</p><p> Crime solving can be a lark, you know.</p><p> And while Hart To Hart and the superior Remington Steele gave it a go, few TV shows ever matched the bubbly, screwball comedy brilliance, fourth wall-bursting inventiveness of Moonlighting .</p><p> Creator Glenn Gordon Caron could have taken six months to deliver a script for all I cared.</p><p> The result was so mind-spinningly funny and smart and silly (not to mention Shakespearean at times), it was alwaysworth the wait.</p><p> He and Bruce Willis and Cybill Shepherd should thank their lucky stars the three of them 1 of 5 Alan Grayson Gabby Giffords: A Few Words Dylan Ratigan A Destructive CrossroadsDON'T MISS HUFFPOST BLOGGERS HOT TRENDS TOP VIDEO PICKS jwoww naked miley cyrus lap dance video natalie portman natalie portman pregnant snooki nude More Celebrity News at People.com More Celebrity News at Popeater.com PHOTOS: Raven-Symone's Dramatic Weight Loss Comments (532) Congresswoman Gabrielle Giffords In 'Very Critical Condition' After Shooting READ MORE Kate Middleton Joins Future In-Laws at Weekend Wedding READ MORE BUZZ: Are Zac Efron & Vanessa Hudgens Back On? Criminals never heard more dangerous words than these, especially when they were delivered with such sly, disarming "I know I'm an idiot but humor me" charm by homicide detective Lieutenant Columbo.</p><p> Peter Falk kept returning to the role again and again and can you blame him? Who wouldn't love getting to pull the rug out from under smarty pants LA criminals time after time and doing it with enough deductive reasoning to make Holmes sit up and pay attention? The mysteries varied all over the map in quality but the appeal of the man himself never wavered. 7.</p><p> Christopher Foyle /Foyle's War -- For me, one of the great charms of a mystery series is getting to know a new place or time, whether it's ancient Rome in the Steven Saylor novels or Laos in the books by Colin Cotterill.</p><p> Foyle's War illuminates the home-front of Britain throughout World War II with this exceptional show.</p><p> The taciturn, hawk-like Foyle is a marvelous man brought to life by Michael Kitchen:the less he says, the more perceptive and penetrating he becomes and people speaking to him positivelysquirm in his presence.</p><p> And throughout the mysteries you get war profiteers, women at work, deserters, shortages and more.</p><p> This may seem like an odd compliment but the show is so vivid and detailed and rich that I'm always gutted (and surprised) when I realize there are no novels to explore about thischaracter and this time.</p><p> I just want to spend more time with him. 8.</p><p> Veronica Mars /Veronica Mars -- Sure, I watched The Hardy Boys and Nancy Drew TV shows when I was a kid and loved other teen detectives.</p><p> But they're light years behind Veronica, the smartest solver of high school mysteries since Judy Blume explained sex and girl stuff to a generation of kids raised on The Brady Bunch.</p><p> If only the show hadn't panicked in its third season, she'd be higher on the list.</p><p> Now get that feature film in the works while Kristen Bell can still play a college student. 9.</p><p> Chief Inspector Morse /Inspector Morse -- Grumpy is the de facto mood for so many crime solvers because they're so much smarter than everyone else in the room (especially their sidekicks) and it can get rather tiresome.</p><p> Why do criminals insist on raping and pillaging and murdering when they should know that Morse is going to sniff them out? A cultured, private man, Morse will tend to fall in love too easilywith women involved in murder.</p><p> And Lewis really is quite dependable and helpful, in his way.</p><p> The British are such deft hands at mysteries they do tend to drag a series out (as if the show itself wasn't stretched to the limit, they even gave Lewis a rather unnecessary spin-off).</p><p> But Morse is one for the ages in all hisornery glory. 10.</p><p> Special Agent Dale Cooper /Twin Peaks -- An un-ironic Boy Scout crossed with visions and a laser-like ability to discern telling clues, Kyle MacLachlan's Cooper is an utterly unique crime solver.</p><p> It helps that he's part of an utterly unique show that used dream sequences to prod Cooper towards the solutions of his crimes.</p><p> But watching this sexily formal and polite man wade through a steamy and fantastical mystery while never losing his decency or genuine oddity is fascinating.</p><p> No wonder peopleimagined crazy ideas like the one that suggested the secretary he dictated notes to didn't exist and wasactually Cooper himself after donning a dress.</p><p> Finding out whodunit was never the point: it was the journey that mattered and what a tour guide in Dale Cooper.</p><p> I know, I know.</p><p> What about Tattinger's, and Peter Gunn and Remington Steele and Tenspeed & Brown Shoe and so many more?
