This morning is the Joel and Ethan Coen movie A Serious Man , the first test of how easy/difficult Toronto can be.</p><p> The theater only holds 582 people.</p><p> But I had a pleasant surprise: my badge has a little "p" on it which gives me a teensy bit of extra priority.</p><p> Hallelujah.</p><p> But my friends in the longer line got in as well, so Toronto really does seem hassle-free.</p><p> A SERIOUS MAN *** 1/2 out of **** -- I don't know if I should call this an unexpected treat after they won the Oscar.</p><p> But A Serious Man is so distinctively a Coen Brothers film -- and so successful -- that it seems like a rebirth.</p><p> No irony here.</p><p> No distancing or sour comic tone.</p><p> No outside source material.</p><p> It feels bred in the bone and deeply felt.</p><p> The story is set in 1967, with our hero a collegeprofessor and Job-like character beset by one trouble after another. (I defy anyone to review thefilm and not reference Job.) His wife wants a divorce because she's in love with an unctuous man who constantly hugs Larry Gopnik (a wonderfully restrained Michael Stuhlbarg) and needs todiscuss everything in a reasoned, calm manner that is utterly infuriating.</p><p> Larry is up for tenure butsomeone is sending anonymous letters denigrating him to the committee.</p><p> His son is having a bar mitzvah but is in hock for $20 to the local juvenile delinquent pot dealer.</p><p> Larry also has to deal with an awkward bribe from a Korean student that escalates in bizarre ways.</p><p> Did I mention hisbrother (Richard Kind) is living on their couch, constantly draining some sort of oozing sore? On the bright side, the sexy neighbor next door bathes in the nude.</p><p> That's a catalog of plot points, but not a good summary of the feel of this film.</p><p> It's the most universal -- and yet the most Jewish --movie the Coens have ever made, even though it has a close kinship with the over the top BartonFink .</p><p> Of course, getting specific can make any experience universal and that's what they achieve here.</p><p> Who hasn't wondered, why me? Their wicked humor is here: Larry is stranded at a local motel and having a crisis at work when his son calls to complain that the TV show F Troop is coming in fuzzy.</p><p> The series of rabbis and lawyers and doctors all proffer advice, but when someone has something truly useful to reveal, of course they drop dead in front of him.</p><p> It's deadpan but real, funny but sad, absolutely true and a fable for our times.</p><p> It's such an odd duck, I thought I might have to start defending it, but the people around me all seemed to be on the film's wavelength.</p><p> And yet what does the opener (in which a peasant woman stabs a dybbuk in the heart) actually imply? The price of not confronting evil head-on? Or the danger of letting fearand mistrust make you act hastily and without reason? One acquaintance called the film a Rosetta Stone for understanding the Coens.</p><p> But they've never spoken in a foreign tongue that needed translating.</p><p> They just cloaked their fears in comedy and heightened drama.</p><p> Here, they have the nerve to stare the trouble right in the eye in this, their most honest film.</p><p> Me, I'm running away but it's fascinating to see them not even blink.</p><p> Can't wait to see it again.</p><p> Another Oscar contender.</p><p> Well, I ran right from A Serious Man to Jason Reitman's Up In The Air with a heavy heart.</p><p> The law of averages says I'm unlikely to see two really good Hollywood movies in a row.</p><p> UP IN THE AIR *** 1/2 out of **** -- Well, I was wrong.</p><p> George Clooney and Vera Farmiga make a sexy couple in this romantic drama with bite.</p><p> Clooney is a man who specializes in firing people -- he's great at coming into a company for a day, calling in people one by one and "letting them go" with a wry touch or brief insight that keeps the people from imploding (and more to the point, from suing).</p><p> But his real passion is for frequent flier miles.</p><p> Clooney loves to fly, loves to stay inhotels, loves to rent cars and loves to...avoid life? Unlike in the novel by Walter Kirn, in the filmClooney's character has never been married, perish the thought.</p><p> When he bumps into fellow road warrior Farmiga, it's a match made in the Executive Lounge waiting area at JFK.</p><p> They whip out hotel cards, compare notes on car companies and click on every level: especially on the level of nocommitment or no fun.</p><p> Single is what they want.</p><p> Not even "single," for that implies the potential for dating, for marriage.</p><p> Uh-uh.</p><p> Seeing them spark and trade details of who is in what city when so they can arrange a play date is contemporary naughtiness at its best.</p><p> Clooney's air-tight,impersonal, first class life is upended when college grad Natalie (Anna Kendrick) convinces his company they can fire people over the internet, potentially grounding him forever.</p><p> She joins Clooney for a trip to Detroit to gain some hands-on experience and it's a delight to see him andFarmiga cluck over and guide Kendrick both personally and professionally (though they may be theones who need the most help).</p><p> The film couldn't be more timely, with its compelling montage of people being fired and then reacting in a thousand different ways.</p><p> A light romantic comedy, it gains more gravitas and then smoothly segues to Clooney reconnecting with his family, getting serious about Farmiga and...well, you can do the rest.</p><p> But you can't.</p><p> An easier film might have stopped there.</p><p> But Up In The Air is bolder and goes on quite a bit longer, frustrating our expectations and avoiding the smooth romantic-comedy landing for an ambiguous crash landingwith Clooney still up in the air about where he wants to go.</p><p> Not Reitman.</p><p> He's taken a major leap forward and Clooney and Farmiga have chemistry to burn. (She's certainly got her eye on a Supporting Actress nod.) This is sophisticated, adult fare and another potential Oscar contender,especially in a year with ten nominees for Best Picture.</p><p> Whew.</p><p> Two in a row, from Hollywood no less.</p><p> Others admire Up In The Air but don't seem quite as bullish as me.</p><p> Still, I'll wait for the reviews instead of my tiny sampling of opinion.</p><p> I see Hollywood Reporter has already raved about the Coens film, which I imagine will be the first of many at the very least respectful reviews. (Respectful is the tone for Variety's take. ) I scramble over to a new venue (the first time I'm leaving the Varsity on Bay St. above the Indigo bookstore) to catchValhalla Rising , which I know nothing about except there are Vikings.</p><p> VALHALLA RISING ** out of **** An arty Viking film? Why not? From the director of the Pusher trilogy, this is a moody and fairly engrossing look at a man captured by other men and forced to fight to the death other prisoners for their amusement.</p><p> Our hero has one eye and a vicious way in the ring (he never loses, never even comes close) and you know it's only a matter oftime before he loosens those chains.</p><p> A young boy that feeds him teams up with the man when he escapes (brutally, bloodily -- this is a slow film with exceptionally vivid violence when it strikes).</p><p> They team up with/are dragooned into a group of Christian Vikings headed for the Holy Land butsideswiped by a fog into a path for Ireland or perhaps America.</p><p> Very little happens here, but it looks very striking and the quirky score -- an organ at one point, at others an anachronistic electric guitar -- keeps your attention.</p><p> But despite its would-be commentary on Christian crusaders andtheir bloody spreading of the faith, it all adds up to little more than a lot of atmosphere and just a little plot.</p><p> The 90 minutes don't drag, but they don't skip by, either.</p><p> The 13th Warrior it ain't.</p><p> Obamas' Anniversary (PICTURES):
