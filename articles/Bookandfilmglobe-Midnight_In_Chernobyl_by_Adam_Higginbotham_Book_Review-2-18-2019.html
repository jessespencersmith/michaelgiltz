Saturday, September 7, 2019 NON-FICTION ﬂIt’s the Final Countdown The Black Comedy of ‘Midnight In Chernobyl’  February 18, 2019 Michael Giltz I kept laughing out loud while reading this marvelous, remarkably-detailed look at the Chernobyl nuclear meltdown.</p><p> That made it a little awkward when someone asked, “What are you reading?” and I had to say “anonfiction book about the worst nuclear disaster in history (so far).” I wasn’t ha-ha laughing.</p><p> It was a bleak,“Dr.</p><p> Strangelove” sort of laugh.</p><p> I couldn’t do anything else in face of the idiocy and incompetence and sheer, frightening, dear-god-did-they-really detail I found on every page of Adam Higginbotham’s book Midnight InChernobyl .</p><p> The tragedy begins with the very design of the nuclear reactors built at Chernobyl and throughout the SovietUnion.</p><p> People knew they were deeply flawed.</p><p> But that totalitarian state didn’t like admitting error.</p><p> Disastrous accidents happened throughout the USSR, but they deemed each one a state secret.</p><p> The state didn’t even tell the nuclear scientists and engineers at other power plants about them.</p><p> And they certainly didn’t learn from theaccidents,improve designs, or share what they learned with others.</p><p> They made roofs of asbestos when othermaterial wasn’t available.</p><p> They used subpar building material.</p><p> And on and on and on.</p><p> Thanks to the fall of that empire, and a mountain of declassified documents, Higginbotham gives a blow-by- blow account of the tragedy.</p><p> He recounts the shame and bravery of those involved (sometimes the shame andthen bravery of the same person).</p><p> Then he continues the story through the cover-up and the blowback fromMikhail Gorbachev’s attempt at perestroika.</p><p> Most importantly, he details the ongoing environmental andpersonal disaster impacting the earth and the people involved.← The Eyes Don’t Have It Prepping for a Post-Roe America →Higginbotham captures the voices and personalities of so many vivid characters, from high-ranking Party officials to the first responder who was luckily drunk the night he responded to the fire at the plant.</p><p> This gained him some all-important protection from radiation.</p><p> Most memorably, he profiles Maria Protsenko, a diminutive architect, formidable, talented and no-nonsense.</p><p> She was born in China to Sino-Russian parents and probably had two strikes against her from the start in the USSR.Her grandfather disappeared during a Stalinist purge.</p><p> Two siblings died unnecessarily because of a curfew thatkept her family from taking them to a doctor.</p><p> Her father descended into opium addiction.</p><p> And still Protsenko became a model Communist.</p><p> She planned the city where workers at Chernobyl lived.</p><p> During the sudden evacuation, she proved a model of efficiency.</p><p> Then she worked with soldiers trying to deal with the crisis and ultimately wall off the devastatedarea.</p><p> Towards the end of the book, the author interviews her.</p><p> When he can’t discern the smell of a book shebrought out of Cherbobyl, Protsenko puckishly blows dust from it into his face.</p><p> When he’s understandablyhorrified, she laughs off his concern.</p><p> Who couldn’t love her? Whether you read it for the drama, for the careful and fascinating science around this tragedy, as a warning for the future or simply as a horror story to share with unsuspecting brunch companions, Midnight In Chernobyl isfirst-rate, funny and ultimately so very, very sad.
