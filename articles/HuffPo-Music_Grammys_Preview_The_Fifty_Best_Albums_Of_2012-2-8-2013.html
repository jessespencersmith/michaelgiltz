Quintet -- Live In Europe 1969: The Bootleg Series Volume Two THE BEST ALBUMS OF 2012 -- THE EXTENDED VERSION 1.</p><p> Rumer -- Seasons Of My Soul/Boys Don't Cry (70s pop perfection) I've been waiting two years to say this: Seasons Of My Soul is the best album of the year.</p><p> She's from the UK but Rumer isn't a soul sister like Adele.</p><p> She's got a Karen Carpenter vibe, a deceptively mellow voice that proves addictive.</p><p> Still waters run deep, though; her debut is an emotionally rich collection that deepens with every listen.</p><p> Rumer also nods to Burt Bacharach and her demos were so striking, it's no surprise to discover that Bacharach himself invited her to meet with him and asked her to sing some of his new songs.</p><p> That's like starting a rock band and having Paul McCartney call you up and say he's got a tune you might fancy.</p><p> I can speak very confidently about this debut and Rumer's potential.</p><p> Seasons Of My Soul came out in the UK in 2010 and I fell in love with it immediately.</p><p> I've lived with it a long time and seen her in concert, where Rumer was even more impressive than she is on CD.</p><p> You can really spot an artist that's in it for the long haul when they wow you in concert.</p><p> I waited and waited for the album to come out in theUS but it was delayed again and again as success took her all over the world and her label waited for the right time to launch her in the US.</p><p> Her second album came out before that happened; Boys Don't Cry is a striking covers album showing her great taste and interpretive ability.</p><p> Still, I suggest you start with the debut.</p><p> That CD finally came out in January of 2012 and I interviewed her to mark the occasion.</p><p> She got some good notices but didn't quite break out the way she did back in the UK.</p><p> Don't worry; she's not goinganywhere and the US will catch on at some point.</p><p> Here's her wonderful debut single,"Aretha." 2.</p><p> Branford Marsalis -- Four MF's Playin' Their Tunes (hard-driving jazz; faultless) Did somebody say jazz was dead? Ha.</p><p> Not when talent like Branford Marsalis can deliverstraight no chaser jazz that's swinging, melodically strong and bursting with vigor.Experimentation is fine but there's no need for the trappings of other genres when themusic is this good.</p><p> The title is my least favorite part; it's too obvious and blunt for analbum this good.</p><p> Here's an in-studio look at the recording of the album closer "Treat ItGentle." Jazz didn't die with Miles Davis and it's not just for the museum.</p><p> Here's proof. 3.</p><p> The Tallest Man On Earth -- There's No Leaving Now (inspiringly good Dylanesque rock) The Tallest Man On Earth is one of the most exciting talents to emerge in recent years.</p><p> He comes from Sweden, sings like he's from Woody Guthrie's part of the country and writesgreat folk-ish songs that would tag him as "the next Dylan" if anyone was still dumbenough to curse an artist with such a label.</p><p> Best of all, he's reached the wide world thanksto the reach of digital music; great albums can indeed burst out from anywhere withoutthe help of a major label.</p><p> I somehow missed his debut, but a rave in a UK magazine sentme to the web where I quickly sampled two or three songs and then immediatelydownloaded the entire album from iTunes because I couldn't bear to wait for a CD. (Ibought one later, anyway.) That was The Wild Hunt, my favorite album of 2010.</p><p> Now he's released his third full length album and There's No Leaving Now is a confident extensionof everything that's come before.</p><p> TTMOE has subtly expanded his musical palette but theemphasis is still on his guitar and his songs, most of the time, with the occasional pianoand other instrumentation thrown in.</p><p> If anything, it might be his strongest yet.</p><p> So in fouryears we've had three albums and two EPs, and each one has steadily improved on what came before.</p><p> Plus, like Rumer, I've seen him in concert and was blown away by his skill, charisma, humor and stage presence.</p><p> He is the real deal.</p><p> When it comes to discovering acts all over the world in this post-record label, post-MTV, post-radio world, I've seen the future of rock and roll and it's The Tallest Man On Earth.</p><p> That's why it's so easy to spot him, I suppose.</p><p> Here's the lead single, the infectious "1904."4.</p><p> Frank Ocean -- Channel Orange (state of the art soul) Frank Ocean's mixtape Nostalgia, Ultra scraped onto my list of the best albums of 2011.</p><p> I have to admit, I wasn't expecting the full-blown r and b mastery of Channel Orange, which ranges from the mind-blowingly smart "Pyramids" to the cleverly emotional "Thinkin Bout You." Marvin Gaye would be taking notes. (Okay, to be accurate Ocean has been taking notes from Gaye.</p><p> But you get my point: he's good.) The matter-of-fact personal backstory is just a no-fuss bonus.</p><p> It's like Jackie Robinson (re)breaking the color code of baseball.</p><p> When you're this talented, people will pay attention. 5.</p><p> Paul Buchanan -- Mid Air (emotionally intense, late night confessions) Bill Fay -- Life Is People (age+wisdom+piano+God) Do you know the Blue Nile? They're a Scottish band of legendary impact in the UK, with albums coming sparingly over the years and then not at all.</p><p> Their live performances havebeen even rarer.</p><p> But like, perhaps, Nick Drake, their work has an intensity and power yousimply can't get anywhere else, matched with mature lyrics and melodies to die for.</p><p> Theirdebut Walk Across The Rooftops became a cult classic while Hats became their big seller,a landmark work that never quite moved beyond critical acclaim in the US.</p><p> Nine yearsafter their last album, leader Paul Buchanan has delivered this heartbreaker, a solo albumpared down almost solely to his voice and a piano that as always focuses on the needs andconcerns of adults like few other artists.</p><p> The title track (below) stops me dead in mytracks.</p><p> You listen to this music late at night, with the lights off, your heart almost stoppingin sympathy with the beauty on display.</p><p> Their debut album contained one of my favoriteromantic lines, a favorite because it's so mature.</p><p> Buchanan pleaded with his lover to stay,stay, promising her, "I will understand you." Really, who could ask for anything more?Now if you don't find this performance entrancing, fair enough -- move on.</p><p> I'm suresomething else on this list will appeal.</p><p> But if you're intrigued like I was, trust me, the restof the album is just as good.</p><p> Mid Air is the rare pop album that delves into family, a longmarriage, raising children and other concerns with the same passion the Stones brought to drinking and drugging.</p><p> Just a lot quieter.Bill Fay is a genuine cult figure from the UK.</p><p> But I shouldn't pretend I've ever heard of him.</p><p> Life Is People is his first album of all new songs in 41 years and it came with wordsof praise from all and sundry, many of whom claimed him as a touchstone in their lives.</p><p> What? How come I've never heard of him? That's the sort of praise -- when coming from artists and critics you like -- that sends me running to the music.</p><p> I haven't lived with it long enough yet, but this album is growing on me steadily.</p><p> Like Mid Air, it's mostly Fay's voice and a piano (with a strong band backing him up at times) and years of distilledwisdom.</p><p> I won't be surprised if a year from now it's grown in my estimation but it'salready a unique and noteworthy album.</p><p> Plus, it's deeply religious in its way, with nods toGod threaded throughout the album quietly but persistently.</p><p> In a year where I didn'tdiscover much pure gospel music, this was welcome.</p><p> It's not gospel of the "Thank You,Lord" variety (though one track is called "Thank You Lord").</p><p> It's more a wide-eyed wonderover the beauty of creation, which happily encompasses this gentle reverie of an album. 6.</p><p> Johann Johannsson -- The Miners' Hymns (the film score of the year) Technically, this score for the film of the same name came out in 2011.</p><p> But I justdiscovered it and it's too good to ignore on a technicality.</p><p> The film is by Bill Morrison,who has made a career out of crafting movies from found footage.</p><p> His landmark workDecasia turned decaying film stock into a beautifully sad thing of beauty and had a killer score to match.</p><p> Now comes this film, a genuine masterpiece as well.</p><p> It is certainly one ofthe two or three best films released in the US in 2012.</p><p> It uses footage from annualgathering of miners who were celebrating their union's strength.</p><p> Cut together from variousevents over the years, it becomes an unwitting funeral for that very way of life.</p><p> You needn't own a copy of The Ragged-Trousered Philanthropists to appreciate the moving,beautiful way Morrison tells a story without a single word of narration or dialogue.</p><p> It's all held together by Johannsson's masterful score, a complex and stirring elegy indeed. 7.</p><p> Fiona Apple -- The Idler Wheel Is Wiser Than The Driver Of The Screw And Whipping Cords Will Serve You More Than Ropes Will Ever Do (pop artist at peak of her powers) I don't care if Fiona Apple's next album has a title as long as a novel, not when the pop music she's creating is this good.</p><p> I'll type out every word and say, if you've been a fan ofhers in the past, don't hesitate.</p><p> She's at the top of her formidable game here.</p><p> She mayindulge herself on the titles, but everything else here is wonderfully eccentric, catchy ashell and right to the emotional point.</p><p> She does what she does and it's wonderful. 8.</p><p> Jamey Johnson -- Living For A Dream: A Tribute To Hank Cochran (all-star country hoedown) He co-wrote the Patsy Cline classic "I Fall To Pieces." You could put that on your gravestone but Nashville legend Hank Cochran wrote plenty of other gems in his longcareer.</p><p> This is nominally a Jamey Johnson album but it's filled to the bursting with guests,making this a de facto all-star tribute to a great talent.</p><p> Johnson, never in better form,serving as ringleader for artists like Emmylou Harris, Elvis Costello, Willie Nelson, GeorgeStrait, Allison Krauss, Vince Gill and more.</p><p> It's better than most tribute albums.</p><p> I thinkJohnson's presence throughout gives this a cohesiveness so many other like-mindedprojects lack.</p><p> Here's Johnson and Krauss performing the first track, "Make The World Go Away" on Letterman. 9.</p><p> Dr.</p><p> John -- Locked Down (triumphant New Orleans music) Jon Cleary Occapella! (tip of the hat to Allen Toussaint) Sometimes, with iconic artists like Dr.</p><p> John that have reached the twilight of their careers, every time they release a new album it's automatically touted as a career capper, a returnto glory, the best in years and so on and so forth.</p><p> Well, I hope this isn't a career capperbecause that would mean we might not get anything this good again.</p><p> But this isn't avictory lap, it's funky and locked in New Orleans jazz of the highest order.</p><p> Does"Revolution" sound like a man fading away gracefully? Heck no.</p><p> And when he says in anamusingly droll voice, "Let's all jut pray on it right now," that's not followed by a momentof silence, it's followed by a stirring solo that let's you know Dr.</p><p> John is going to lead therevolution, given half a chance.</p><p> Righteous stuff.</p><p> Jon Cleary doesn't have the nationalprofile of Dr.</p><p> John but those in the know know he's a New Orleans mainstay.</p><p> His new album is a tribute to the great Allen Toussaint and is as vibrant and loose and funky and free as one would hope.</p><p> Sure, there's "Southern Nights" (how couldn't there be?) but he also digs up some lesser known gems.</p><p> Much like Cleary himself.</p><p> He plays most of the instruments most of the time, though Dr.</p><p> John, Bonnie Raitt and others stop by.</p><p> Irresistible.</p><p> The fan video I've included is raw as can be.</p><p> But the sound is great and it'sfun to peek over shoulders at his piano work.10.</p><p> Mumford And Sons -- Babel (flowering of Americana) The Lumineers -- The Lumineers Pigpen Theatre Co. -- Bremen The Avett Brothers -- The Carpenter As I get older, I'm drawn instinctively more and more towards "porch music," songs thatare delivered acoustically and with a folksy directness.</p><p> I can still get stirred by hip-hopand that front porch can include an electric guitar (just run the cord through the window so it can be plugged into an outlet in the kitchen).</p><p> But the feeling of a band, the feeling of people in a room creating music strikes a chord with me more and more.</p><p> So the rise of Americana, which of course has always been with us in one form or another, is welcome indeed.</p><p> Mumford And Sons have been at the commercial forefront.</p><p> And they've flourished in the glow of commercial success.</p><p> Their follow-up Babel is stronger in every respect than their debut; these guys are embracing a wider audience without ignoring the strumming heart of what they do.</p><p> They're stadium ready with this widescreen album but they're taking the stage with banjo and acoustic guitars in hand.</p><p> The Avett Brothers on the other hand have shied away from the spotlight a bit.</p><p> Their last album I And Love And You had a thunderous sound (and was my favorite album of 2009).</p><p> But the acclaim has spookedthem a bit I think.</p><p> The Carpenter is a little more tentative and certainly quieter. (Andwhile the religious beliefs of certain Mumfords gets attention, The Carpenter has morenotable religious overtones).</p><p> Mumford wins this round.</p><p> Following in the wake of these twoexcellent bands are acts like The Lumineers, who deliver a great debut in the same vein.Sure, they deserve their own attention.</p><p> But if you lvoe those two bands, this is right inyour wheel house.</p><p> Coming from a more offbeat angle is Pigpen, a theater troupe that hascreated marvelous plays filled with humor, puppetry, storytelling and excellent songs.Many of those can be found on this album mixed with some originals and they have aconfidence and distinctive lyricism that should win this band/troupe a much wideraudience.</p><p> See their theatrical productions; see them perform as a band on tour; or forheaven's sake buy their album.</p><p> Mumford And Sons "I Will Wait"The Avett Brothers "Live And Die" The Lumineers "Stubborn Love" Pigpen Theatre Co. "Bremen" (Watch this for a glimpse of their theatrical magic as well)11.</p><p> Kasey Chambers And Shane Nicholson -- Wreck And Ruin (world class folk- country-blues) Both acts have terrific solo work, but this husband and wife team from Australia is one of the best male/female country duos in a long, long time.</p><p> Their first album together -- Rattlin' Bones -- just blew me away.</p><p> This follow-up is just as good, filled with vivid numbers drawing on folk and country and blues for a...well, a bone-rattlin' hoedown thatis infectious and moving and just plain fun.</p><p> Did you buy O Brother, Where Art Thou? Buy this. 12.</p><p> Nas -- Life Is Good (rap for grown ups) Nas has always understood that honesty is more powerful and strong than boasting,though he can do his share of boasting too.</p><p> You won't find Killer Mike on my list; gangstarap that can't see beyond braggadocio doesn't interest me anymore.</p><p> Here Nas raps with allthe fervent skill he's always possessed, ripping apart the competition while making it coolto worry about his teenage daughter.</p><p> This is just about as good as Nas at his best andthat's as good as rap gets.</p><p> Okay, I'm not street but I'm not a farmer either.</p><p> That doesn'tstop me from loving good rap or good country. "Daughters" is a classic, a perfect intro forthose who don't usually delve into this genre. just try not to nod your head while listening.13.</p><p> Leonard Cohen -- Old Ideas (magisterial pop) What a great second wind the great Leonard Cohen is enjoying.</p><p> Financial travails brought him down from the mountain and out onto the road where this cool figure embraced the massive audience that had been waiting for him all these years.</p><p> His craggy, Grand Canyon of a voice had deepened even more, but Cohen delivered his portfolio of classic tunes withmajesty and appealing, witty reserve.</p><p> Now we get this great, autumnal gem.</p><p> He's paredaway the unessential.</p><p> But the result isn't austere and cold; it's as tuneful and rich as ever.</p><p> Just older and wiser, a second flowering.</p><p> Maybe it's been a while since you've purchased a Leonard Cohen album.</p><p> It shouldn't be. 14.</p><p> Josephine -- Portrait (two English lasses with talent to burn) Lianna La Havas -- Is Your Love Big Enough The marvelous Corinne Bailey Rae threw down the gauntlet, embracing pop and soul and rock with aplomb on her brilliant debut.</p><p> That gauntlet has been snapped up by a handfulof talented artists, led by Josephine (who leans towards the full-bodied soul) and Lianna(who is a bit more folksy).</p><p> Both feel like genuine artists right off the mark.</p><p> These are thesort of debuts that leave you tapping your toes, waiting for them to come to town inconcert or release a new album right away.</p><p> NIna Simone, Maxwell, Erykah Badu, PhoebeSnow, Bill Withers, Joni Mitchell - take your pick of excellent influences you can spothere.</p><p> Can't wait for more.15.</p><p> Grant-Lee Phillips -- Walking In The Green Corn (acoustic gem) What a pleasure.</p><p> After fronting the excellent band Grant Lee Buffalo, Phillips finally disbanded if after label support never matched the critical acclaim of the group.</p><p> As a soloartist, he turned to a more acoustic sound, one that's grown richer and more assured witheach release.</p><p> His latest was funded in part by fans, the sort of communal support you readabout but don't always see happening in practice for the acts that deserve it most.</p><p> It's alovely work.</p><p> If you've lost track of him over the years, now is as good a time as any tolisten again.</p><p> He hasn't been waiting for you; he's gone ahead.</p><p> Here's hoping you catch up. 16.</p><p> Antibalas -- Antibalas (Fela Kuti lives!)Honestly, I get so much music handed to me by labels or make impulsive buys based on a rave review that half the time I forget why I've bought an album.</p><p> But they all go on myphone (yep, it's 2013) and every once in a while I'll start to listen to an artist without thefoggiest idea of who they are.</p><p> That was certainly the case with Antibalas.</p><p> The first trackbegan and I was immediately intrigued.</p><p> Was this some archival album, a compilation ofsome world music act I'd never heard of before? You can't help but have an open mindwhen you don't know the first damn thing about an album.</p><p> All I knew was that I loved it.</p><p> Turns out Antibalas is a new act from Brooklyn of all places, a group devoted in part to the music and spirit of Fela Kuti.</p><p> Mission accomplished.</p><p> This album was recorded for Daptone Records, the home of the great Sharon Jones and a host of other acts that love classic music genres but play them with the ferocity and passion of the new, not as some musty work of revivalism.</p><p> It's a perfect fit for this band.</p><p> To top it off, the music video for "Dirty Money" is clever enough to make me enjoy music videos again. 17.</p><p> Iris Dement -- Sing The Delta (comeback of the year) Boo Hanks with Don Flemons -- Buffalo Junction What the heck happened to Iris Dement? I'd been wondering that for years.</p><p> This little gal with a huge, rustic voice that could shake mountains and sounded like it was living back nthe 1920s blew me and everyone else away when she broke through with My Life and itsinstant classic of a tune "Sweet Is The Melody." (I still sing that one, poorly, all the time.)Her next album was political in nature and proved, unfortunately, how few artists outsideof Dylan can create enduring tunes that are overtly political.</p><p> And then...nothing.</p><p> Turns outshe made an album of gospel covers I missed.</p><p> But essentially it's been 16 years betweenalbums of new music for her.</p><p> Why? She just wasn't ready, or to be exact she felt the songsweren't ready.</p><p> They are now because Sings The Delta picks up right where she left off,delivering pure, coal country, mountain music of a variety so pure it makes most Nashvilleproduct sound like Carly Rae Jepsen.</p><p> Remarkably, it's been worth the wait.</p><p> I haven't beenwaiting for a new album from Boo Hanks because frankly I'd never heard of thisbluesman.</p><p> Heck, his debut album came out when he was 79 years old, which has to beclose to a record.</p><p> Now he's collaborating with Dom Flemons of the terrific group CarolinaChocolate Drops and Hanks' second album is a low-key, witty and tuneful delight.Thirteen songs under 30 minutes and it's both just right and over way too soon.</p><p> His video(a fan's bootleg, I think) starts just as the song does and it's pretty raw but gives you asense of his playful style.</p><p> Here's a fine No Depression feature telling his story.18.</p><p> Vijay Iyer Trio -- Accelerando (jazz piano) Brad Mehldau -- Where Do You Start/Ode I suppose jazz long ago became the realm of the wealthy and obsessive aficionados.</p><p> The clubs that show the top acts are very expensive and usually offer short sets at high prices.Or you head to even more expensive venues like Lincoln Center.</p><p> It's a pity since jazz at itsbest is popular music.</p><p> But it won't rustify as long as artists like Vijay Iyer and BradMehldau are around to deliver.</p><p> Iyer's album is a probing affair that takes a few listens tosink in.</p><p> Mehldau shows off both sides of his talent with two albums.</p><p> The first contains hisusual wide-ranging covers while the second is all originals.</p><p> You wouldn't want to give upeither, but here I'll give the edge to the originals.</p><p> The Branford Marsalis is for theunconverted; these are for the believers.19.</p><p> The Hives -- Lex Hives (rock, by god) Japandroid -- Celebration Rock The Doughboys -- Shakin' Our Souls The Hives are so good live and bursting with such confidence that it confuses me sometime.</p><p> Do these guys fill stadiums? Have they sold millions of copies of their albums?They sure look like they have and their music sure as hell deserves to do so.</p><p> It's been fiveyears since their last album and they don't miss a trick, delivering a collection of songsthat builds catchy songs out of the barest of essentials (a hand clap, a brief phrase like"Come on!" repeated over and over, etc.) and making it uproariously fun.</p><p> They knowthey're the biggest band of the world, even if we don't know it yet.</p><p> Japandroids fromCanada also know how to rock and their second album shows them growing by leaps and bounds.</p><p> Sure, the Ramones would havereleased seven albums in the last four years, but at least they're making sure they get itright.</p><p> Maybe both groups took notes on The Doughboys.</p><p> These garage rockers from Jerseytook their own sweet time.</p><p> They flirted with fame in the 1960s, broke up and reunited for a laugh in 2000.</p><p> This time it stuck and they've finally recorded
