I read all three books within a few weeks so perhaps that helped me appreciate the arc even more.</p><p> Anyway, Isuppose you could read the third book alone, but why would you? It's a trilogy and meant to be read that way.</p><p> InBook One, Max lives in a quaint sort of turn of the century kingdom with his parents who run a theatrical troupe.</p><p> Maxloves them and the theater but it's not quite his thing.</p><p> Exactly what his thing will be, this 12 year old boy isn't quitesure about.</p><p> His parents are lured to a far-away country to establish a new national theater company and Max isaccidentally left behind with his librarian grandmother.</p><p> They realize the parents have been kidnapped and worry andworry and worry.</p><p> To maintain his independence, Max becomes sort of a detective, though he insists on callinghimself The Solutioneer. (Why the books aren't called The Solutioneer remains a mystery to me.) There's also a girlthat annoys Max with all her questions and a boarder in Max's home who has a mysterious past and other danglingthreads.</p><p> So there's the set up.</p><p> At first, Max solves rather mundane or obvious problems like finding a lost dog.</p><p> InBook Two, however, Voigt takes a quantum leap forward: Max is now solving problems of the human heart andshowing a great deal of insight.</p><p> In Book Three, Max and his friends head off to rescue the boy's parents in the LatinAmerican dictatorship they're trapped in.</p><p> The final volume is especially good, with remarkably complex passagesdepicting that awkward moment when a child sees their parents as people, flaws and all, not to mention somecompletely earned but still bravura changes that make clear the pattern Voigt was creating from the very start.</p><p> ReadBook One alone and -- like me -- you might consider it a pleasant diversion, with so-so mysteries but appealingcharacters.</p><p> Book Two is much better and Book Three deepens the entire work.</p><p> It's like a magic trick the way shesuddenly reveals the life Max was intended for all along.</p><p> I plan to read more Voigt right away.</p><p> Highly recommended. -- Michael Giltz P.S.</p><p> I'm convinced we'll see more of Mister Max and his eyes, which are an odd sort of grey, perhapsthe grey of a computer screen when you've just powered it down and the energy seems to be draining away orperhaps the grey of a London fog when you don't quite know where you're going and there's no other light aroundexcept the dull glow of street lamps that haven't been cleaned in a while, a murky sort of menacing but perhaps notreally dangerous grey.</p><p> Something like that.LessWhat You SayFilter byNo Reviews Found .....
