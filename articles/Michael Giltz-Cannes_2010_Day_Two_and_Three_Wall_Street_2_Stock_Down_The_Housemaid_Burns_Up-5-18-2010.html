If you'd asked me about the buzz, I would have said, oh, everyone hated it.</p><p> But then standing in line I'd overhear Europeans and especially the French say they liked it, even that they loved it.</p><p> At first, I thought it was just an exception.</p><p> But it happened again and again.</p><p> Then I noticedthe roundup of top European critics in Le Film Francaise had given it strong support, with almost half giving it three stars (out of four) and two even the top award of four stars.</p><p> Plus, I remembered that Tim Burton is the head of the jury and he surely loves the New Burlesque.</p><p> So Tournee has morphed from a forgettable Day Two flop into a contender for perhaps a top prize like Runner Up or Best Actor.</p><p> It's a good reminder that critics from Europe vs the US (and the UK) often have wildly different takes on films.</p><p> MOST POPULAR ON HUFFPOSTBIG NEWS: Celebrity Skin | Jersey Shore | Ellen Degeneres | Celebrity Body | Energy Debates | More...</p><p> LOG IN | SIGN UP SHARE THIS STORY Get Entertainment Alerts Email Comments More Dead Birds, Fish Found Across The World Camille Grammer's Porn Past Comes Back To Haunt Her Elizabeth Edwards' Will Revealed Intestinal Parasites May Be Causing Your Energy Slump Rudeness Is A Neurotoxin Should The Leader Of The Free World Dress Like THIS? Sure it was fun to talk about but was it fun to listen to?In fact, their album Tres Tres Forte (Very Very Strong ) was well-reviewed.</p><p> It didn't quite make my list of the best of the year, but it was a solid work.</p><p> Someone had made a documentary film about them and itwas the opening night film of the Directors' Fortnight.</p><p> Plus of course the band would presumably be there, so this was on my don't-miss list.</p><p> Imagine my surprised when I realized the filmmakers had been following the men for years, long before they'd recorded their album.</p><p> In short, they focused on somestreet musicians with every conceivable roadblock to success, only to see them performing around theworld.</p><p> It wasn't just dumb luck.</p><p> In a gesture that goes against traditional documentary film distance(don't get involved with the people you're filming) but goes for every sign of decency, the filmmakers actually believed in the band so much they helped them record the album.</p><p> They also stumbled across a boy who became the spine of the film.</p><p> Just a kid with a tin can and some string for his instrument, butquite accomplished.</p><p> He'd never met Benda Bilili but the producers brought the boy to them and the film follows the kid's maturity as we watch to see if he can become a member of the band and stick with his practicing and rehearsals and staying out of trouble for literally years until their big chance strikes.</p><p> It's acrowd-pleasing, heart-warming sort of film and one that actually should play commercially around the world.</p><p> You'll come out of it smiling and then head online to buy their music.</p><p> CHATROOM * out of **** -- Chatroom began as a short play, one of three commissioned for teen audiences.</p><p> I almost saw it in London (though reviews were mixed) and regret missing it ever since because the very talented Andrew Garfield ( Boy A, The Red Riding Trilogy ) was in the cast of the stage production.</p><p> So that's what drew me to the film.</p><p> For most others, it was the film's director Hideo Nakataof horror film Ring fame.</p><p> I at least hoped he'd add some visual flair to the story of teens who meet in a chat room, only to have one of them urge another to commit suicide.</p><p> I can buy the idea of chatrooms ashotel rooms off a dingy hallway crammed with people, escape pods where you can be alone with a chosenfew and others must knock to get in.</p><p> But the entire film had a cheap, unappealing look to me.</p><p> On stage, it must have been dead easy: to show the kids talking in a chatroom, they just sat them in a circle on stage.</p><p> Done.</p><p> In film, we get lots of cutting back and forth between the chats and the kids in their own lives,usually seen in washed out grays with tiresome parents in the background.</p><p> One or two of the kids is good (notably Aaron Johnson of Kick-Ass ) but for the most part they can't overcome cliched writing and the old John Hughes trope of blaming the parents.</p><p> For all the aura of sex and violence, it's a pretty dull affair.</p><p> CANNES DAY THREEA typical day at Cannes: Everyone not being lazy is up and at the Lumiere by about 8 am for the first screening of the day, grabbing a croissant and the trade papers along the way.</p><p> That first film is alwaysone of the major films in Competition (this morning it's Wall Street: Money Never Sleeps ) and this crack of dawn screening is your best chance to see it.</p><p> Then you scurry to the next movie, especially if it's an in- demand film that the 1,500 other journalists will also want to see.</p><p> Assuming you get in, that takes you to 1 or 2 p.m.</p><p> Maybe you've got time to grab a panini on the Croisette and stare at the gigantic yachts dottingthe bay before getting back in line for a movie at 3 or 4.</p><p> Then another major film usually screens at 7, another at 10 and sometimes a midnight movie on the weekend can take you to 2 am.</p><p> Head back to your place, file something, grab four hours of sleep.</p><p> Rinse. repeat.</p><p> It's not just money that never sleeps.</p><p> AttCannes, I suppose it's possible this might have happened at some point onWall Street, but it's so unlikely -- however much they might have bonded -- that it threw me off.</p><p> Plus, forwhatever reason the music by David Byrne and Brian Eno used throughout (from their terrific collaborations) sounded out of place.</p><p> It didn't help that the lyrics about wanting a home were repeated over and over. When Gekko delivers a speech at Fordham, it has the caustic, insider-y take on the (coming) meltdown we long to hear, but instead of being corrosive, it plays too much to the gallery and is interrupted with lame laugh lines and poor edits.</p><p> On the personal side, LaBeouf and Mulligan'slovers are unhappy from the first minute we see them, so there's not much to root for. (Plus, he behaveslike a schmuck with her repeatedly.) And then there's the godawful, timidly optimistic finale that is not as rosy as some think (Gekko is still a slime ball and we're not supposed to think differently) but is far from earned.</p><p> This is exactly what studios fear about bringing a fall film to Cannes.</p><p> All you get is bad buzzmonths before the movie is released.</p><p> On the other hand, many American critics thought the movie wascommercial and would play well in the US, so don't sell it short yet.</p><p> At least not at the box office.</p><p> AURORA * 1/2 out of **** -- Oh dear.</p><p> The first few days can be very rough, especially when a film is as slow and obtuse as Aurora . it's from Cristi Puiu, the director of The Death Of Mr.</p><p> Lazarescu, one of the major films in the new wave of Romanian cinema that has swept critics along.</p><p> That movie had a driving narrative and mordant black humor.</p><p> This film features all the daunting aspects of the Romanian style(long takes, unexplained characters, plot and details that take literally hours to emerge) without any of the rewards for your effort.</p><p> The director stars as an emotionally stunted man who goes about his day rather furtively.</p><p> Even when he's standing in plain sight, I felt somehow this man was...lurking.</p><p> Threehours long, it's quite a while before we begin to piece together his life.</p><p> This isn't unusual in festival fare.</p><p> The Dumont brothers make brilliant movies where the viewer can be at sea for quite a while.</p><p> But there's usually a driving momentum or a magnetic character to keep you engaged.</p><p> Not so here.</p><p> Our protagonistbuys a shotgun in the first hour, plots and slaughters two people at about the 90 minute mark and it'swell into the second hour before I even had a clue who he had killed and why.</p><p> That's not suspense; that's in fact, the opposite of suspense, as Hitchcock would have gladly pointed out.</p><p> A mildly amusing coda (very mild) at a police station sends you off on a note of humor but it's far too little too late.</p><p> So little iseven said in the film that for minutes at a time you feel nothing but impatience.</p><p> Contrast that with a scene (about two-thirds of the way in, I think) in which the protagonist is forced to actually talk to people.</p><p> He's trying to find a woman who he thought worked at a clothing store.</p><p> The women there let himin before they've opened, tell him she's not there and then get increasingly amused/annoyed/frightenedby his accusatory, paranoid comments.</p><p> He believes no one and imagines they are hiding something when a casual reference to the woman not having worked there in weeks turns out to be months (or vice versa).</p><p> The slightest discrepancy sets him off.</p><p> This is the scene where we realize how unhinged he actually is,though they just find him creepy.</p><p> See what dialogue and interaction can actually do? It can reveal character! I will say this: despite the movie's tiresome obliqueness, in the lead role, Puiu creates a genuinely unnerving fellow.</p><p> He has a habit of staring, turning away and then staring again that makesyour skin crawl.</p><p> And not just people.</p><p> He even eyes his car suspiciously after he locks it and begins to walk away.</p><p> Does he think it might move on him? And when he's at a school picking up his little girl and stares and re-stares at another student the same way, it's as unsettling as anything I'll likely see at Cannes.</p><p> He'sa contender for Best Actor, but it's not worth slogging your way through this three hour film to see it.</p><p> UN POISON VIOLENT/LOVE LIKE POISON ** 1/2 out of **** -- Hundreds if not thousands of films screen at Cannes.</p><p> Besides the roughly 20 films in Competition, there are other curated categories like the Un Certain Regard, Out Of Competition, Seance Speciales, Cannes Classics, the market, movies on the beach and two officially unrelated events: the Directors' Fortnight and Critics Week.</p><p> Since the best first film award (the Camera d'Or) can come from them and because they always contain a gem or twothat becomes the talk of the fest (like Control and XXY from previous years), I go to them as much as possible.</p><p> I've already seen the fun music documentary Benda Bilili and now I'm two for two at DF with this fine debut by Katell Quillevere.</p><p> I was immediately struck by it in comparison to Wall Street: MoneyNever Sleeps.</p><p> Whereas I felt Oliver Stone's music cues were distracting and pulled me out of the film, Baby Bump Bristol Palin's New Radio Gig?
