But perhaps even worse than a really bad show is a show that leaves you indifferent.Someone asks what you think... and you shrug.</p><p> It's a sad situation, since no show is written or staged lightly.</p><p> Italmost always represents at the very least a year (and usually years) of work beginning with the playwright.</p><p> Youstruggle to latch onto some praiseworthy element, anything David Auburn, soexpectations are naturally high for any new work by him.</p><p> Grand Concourse is from Heidi Schreck, the Obie-winning talent who also writes for TV's Nurse Jackie.</p><p> Neither quickens the pulse, though each play is blessedwith a fine cast.First, Lost Lake.</p><p> I was intrigued to see actor John Hawkes on stage.</p><p> He's been a favorite since Deadwood (canwe give that stage, despite seemingto waver a bit on one late monologue.</p><p> He plays a hapless loser, a guy cut off from his ex-wife and his daughter,barely tolerated by his family while he squats at the family cabin.</p><p> Hogan (Hawkes) is so desperate for cash herents out the cabin to a nurse (a nurse practitioner, she corrects him!) named Veronica (the appealing TracieThoms) looking to give her kids a week away from New York City.</p><p> While she's in his home, he sleeps rough inhis truck.In a series of short scenes, these two cross swords, reveal personal traumas and bond in a modest, semi-personal fashion.</p><p> It all takes place in the cabin he's renting out (behind his family's back).</p><p> She checks the placeout, he comes over to do repairs he promised would be done before they moved in, she sidesteps his requestsfor the final payment and so on.</p><p> Truths are revealed, jokes are made, the possibility of romance gently rises onlyto be just as quickly dismissed and it's over.Nothing about this modest work is terribly interesting.</p><p> With Auburn and two fine actors, you might come in withstrong expectations.</p><p> But those are quickly dialed down to Lost Lake proving a minor character study.</p><p> Not everyplay needs to be the Great American Play to satisfy and entertain.</p><p> Sometimes a less lofty goal is in fact exactlywhat one wants.</p><p> But soon even this minor goal proves elusive.</p><p> A child almost drowns, suicide is considered, anexplosion of anger frightens and yet most of that happens off stage and isn't very interesting
