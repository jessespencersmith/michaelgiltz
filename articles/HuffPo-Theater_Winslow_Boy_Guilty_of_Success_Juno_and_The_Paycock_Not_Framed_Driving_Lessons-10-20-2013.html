WINSLOW BOY *** out of **** ROUNDABOUT THEATRE AT AMERICAN AIRLINES This is exactly what one expects of The Roundabout Theatre Company: a strong revival of a classic, traditional play.</p><p> That's not as easy to do as it sounds, so let'scelebrate when they succeed so well.</p><p> In this case, it's almost shocking to realize that Terence Rattigan's durable drama The Winslow Boy hasn't appeared on Broadway since it debuted back in 1947.</p><p> Thanks to the hoopla surrounding Rattigan's 100thbirthday in 2011, the UK was flooded with good to great productions of this playwright once dismissed as rather middle of the road.</p><p> People soon realized he was richer in talent than that.</p><p> Here in the US, most of those shows didn't travel (though we did get to see a wonderful cinematic version of The Deep Blue Sea by the great film director Terence Davies).</p><p> Happily, a UK production of The Winslow Boy inspired the Roundabout and we have this entertaining tale capped by an excellent Roger Rees in full fettle as thedetermined but ailing Arthur Winslow.</p><p> It's a performance and a show sure to beremembered come Tony time.</p><p> My previous experience of the play was limited to David Mamet's excellent film version.</p><p> I must admit, a two hour and 45 minute version of that fairly straightforwardstory seemed rather daunting.</p><p> But not to fear: the play does not feel padded or drawnout in the least when done well.</p><p> The set up is simple.</p><p> Young Ronnie Winslow (Spencer Davis Milford) is home unexpectedly from military school.</p><p> He has been kicked out for stealing but stoutly insists he didn't do it.</p><p> Ronnie's stern father (Rees) simply and directly asks the boy ifhe did it, making clear that lying about it would be far worse than some impulsive actof thievery.</p><p> But no, Ronnie says, he did not do it and that's enough for father.</p><p> The repercussions are enormous.</p><p> The engagement of Ronnie's sister Catherine (Charlotte Parry) is endangered and his brother Dickie (an enjoyably silly ZacharyBooth) must leave university when money becomes tight.</p><p> The case becomes anational obsession complete with debate in the House of Commons.</p><p> Worst of all it'sMay 14, 2014 artsThis is the print preview: Back to normal view Â» Posted: 10/20/2013 11:11 pmtaxing the strength of Ronnie's ailing father who goes from elderly to ancient in front of our eyes: the case is literally killing him.</p><p> Director Lindsay Posner presents this drama about the drive for justice and the price to be paid with a blessed lack of fuss.</p><p> The Winslow Boy is not re-imagined or reframed in any way; Posner simply trusts the work will reward a sturdy, thoughtfulpresentation.</p><p> The set and costumes by Peter McKintosh follow suit, displaying theWinslow drawing room with an old-fashioned completeness that grounds us in theirworld and station in life.</p><p> All other tech elements follow suit.</p><p> Mary Elizabeth Mastrantonio plays Ronnie's mother with unaffected pleasure.</p><p> Grace is allowed to be a little more preoccupied with feminine things without being foolish, nothing more and nothing less than a woman of her time.</p><p> Dickie clearly takes after her as does Booth's performance, which doesn't push the comedy too strongly inherent in this silly son.</p><p> Alessandro Nivola is very good as the lawyer Sir Robert Morton.</p><p> Frankly I've never seem him better on stage as he too makes a virtue of restraint while sparring with the feminist Catherine.</p><p> It's tempting to be almost abashed at how minor the role is that Michael Cumpsty assays.</p><p> Yet what a delight he is in turning the hang-dog DesmondCurry into such a complete and affecting character with just a few brief appearances.</p><p> But the play rises and falls on the strength of the father and daughter.</p><p> Parry is very good indeed as Catherine.</p><p> Smart, penetrating, stubborn in her convictions: she isclearly her father's daughter.</p><p> If there's a tiny complaint I'd make, it's that she andNivola aren't allowed the delicious final lines that Mamet used in his film version.Nonetheless, their budding romance comes through wonderfully well.</p><p> And what a triumph for Rees, who has been floating on air I trust with his directorial triumph of Peter and the Starcatcher .</p><p> Here he reminds us what an excellent actor he is, almost disintegrating into old age before our eyes and yet never pandering to oursentiment.</p><p> When the servant Violet (a fine Henny Russell) comes in and gives her bubbling report on the verdict, you can't take your eyes off the silent Rees.</p><p> He trembles with emotion while absorbing the news of his fate, the outcome of his struggles with the legal system and it's hard not to ache and tear up as realization sinks in for him.</p><p> The Winslow Boy has not been revealed as some coruscating classic.</p><p> It's just a very durable entertainment given a first class revival that deserves to run as long as Rees wants to savor his best role on Broadway in more than a decade.</p><p> JUNO AND THE PAYCOCK ** 1/2 out of **** IRISH REPERTORY THEATRE The Irish Repertory Theatre recently triumphed with a pitch perfect revival of Conor McPherson's The Weir that deserved to transfer to Broadway and become a substantial hit; it was that good.</p><p> Perhaps any follow-up would pale in comparison.More generally, even the Irish Rep shouldn't tackled Sean O'Casey's epic, tricky masterpiece Juno And The Paycock without a massive commitment to scaling the heights of this work.</p><p> The play is a deflating look at vainglorious attitudes towards the Irish Civil War. "Captain" Jack Boyle (Ciaran O'Reilly) is a drunken sod, railing at the world and fairweather friends like Joxer Daly (John Keating) when not avoiding work and sneakingoff for a drink down at the pub with those self-same pals.</p><p> His son Johnny (Ed Malone)was wounded in the fighting and sits at home all day, moaning his fate and panickyover the dreaded knock on the door that might mean discovery by one side orpresumably conscription for more danger by the other.</p><p> Like women everywhere, Juno (a no-nonsense J.</p><p> Smith-Cameron) must actually get on with the work of making money, keeping their shambles of an apartment clean andtrying to knock some sense into her feckless husband.</p><p> She's helped by her daughterMary (Mary Mallen), who seems sensible enough though we're vaguely astonished tosee her refusing the intentions of Jerry Devine (David O'Hara) in favor of a ratherfastidious Charlie Bentham (James Russell).</p><p> The lives of the Boyles are slowly disintegrating but that's up-ended when it appears Captain Jack will be coming into a major inheritance.</p><p> Even the promise of an influx ofmoney manages to turn their slow descent into a speedy collapse, as most anyonewho's won the lottery can surely attest.Funny, painful, sprawling, ambitious, merciless -- these are the attributes of O'Casey's play as written.</p><p> But this production by director Charlotte Moore never comes close tocapturing that complexity.</p><p> The set by James Noone and costumes by David Toser are the highlights here: they have a genuine specificity.</p><p> You can see the decay in their lives mirrored withoutemphasis by the sagging wallpaper. (Though even here the placement of the doorseems awkward; actors entering the set seem to be in the middle of the apartmentbefore they've barely arrived.) Context and specificity is precisely what the rest of the show lacks.</p><p> The civil war, the paranoia, the danger, the changing allegiances and desperate desire to just survive,false patriotism and foolish pride -- all of this would bring the story of the Boyles to lifeand all of it is missing.</p><p> Without understanding the world they live in and the issues atstake, the tragedy never has a chance to take hold.</p><p> Malone's Johnny is simply a bore, moaning and whining about everyone around him, when of course we should realize before the rest of the family that he is harboring some terrible secret or at least be astonished when it is revealed.</p><p> O'Hara doesn'tmake much of an impression but he's very appealing nonetheless, which confuses usinto thinking Mary must be calculating what's best for the family when rejecting him forthe unappealing Bentham.</p><p> When she insists it's Bentham she loves, we can't help butwonder why when O'Hara is playing the rival.</p><p> O'Reilly never creates the larger thanlife, Falstaffian figure that Jack Boyle can be.</p><p> So his fall echoes far less loudly.</p><p> On the plus side, Smith-Cameron is a solid Juno, grounding herself (if not the show) in the business of getting on with things.</p><p> Mallen is quite good as her daughter untiltheir final scene where her tears struggle to fall.</p><p> That's no surprise since ours do too.Only Keating (who was so good in that marvelous Weir ) captures the mercurial Joxer to perfection.</p><p> His head pops into the home like a snapping turtle and his body slowlyfollows; he's ready to bolt at the first sign of danger, with danger generally being thearrival of Juno.</p><p> He coaxes and wheedles and glad-hands until he senses weaknessor changing fortunes and then turns on you like a jackal.</p><p> If you wonder how ficklesome people can be in a time of crisis, how ready to jump one way or another if it's to their advantage, if you want see how good O'Casey's play can be, just watch Joxer.</p><p> HOW I LEARNED TO DRIVE ** out of **** TONGUE IN CHEEK THEATRE Tongue In Cheek is an Off Off Broadway company celebrating its eighth season, a major achievement in and of itself.</p><p> They caught my eye with a funny productionduring the Fringe Festival a few years ago and now I've checked back in with theirrevival of the award-winning Paula Vogel play How I Learned To Drive.</p><p> It's a tricky work that requires a delicate balancing act this production has yet to pull off.</p><p> But keep in mind with the short runs such companies can provide I was reviewingonly its second or so actual performance.</p><p> I'm sure the cast will be tighter and sharpersoon...probably just as their run comes to an end on November 2.</p><p> Such is the life of atiny theater company with little room for luxuries like extended rehearsals or previews.Still, it's enough to reveal some talent in the cast.</p><p> The story is straightforward but presented in a complex flashback structure that wins over your...empathy (certainly sympathy is not the right word) only to slap you withthe realization of what a violation you have been witnessing all along.</p><p> L'il Bit (JakeLipman, who also directed) is a winning, smart young girl with a family that wants nothing more than to keep her down.</p><p> She's burdened by a boorish dad (Michael Edmund), a mom (Shelley Little) who doesn't quite know how to want more for herdaughter and breasts that are big enough to exhaust L'il Bit and fascinate every boyin sight.</p><p> Thank god for Uncle Peck ( an excellent Lynn Berg ).</p><p> He's smart and funny andactually listens to L'il Bit and takes her seriously.</p><p> He encourages her ambition to go to college, takes her out to nice restaurants on big occasions and even teaches her to drive.</p><p> He's also molesting her. (photo by Maeghan Donoghue) Vogel's play is wonderfully sneaky as it seems to presents Uncle Peck in the best possible light, for apedophile that is.</p><p> He really has L'il Bit's best interests atheart, right? When we first flashback from 1988, we seePeck and L'il Bit in a car parked off in the middle of nowhere in the backroads of Maryland.</p><p> He's polite and thoughtful and repeatedly tells the teenage girl he won't do anything she doesn't want.</p><p> She's in charge and if shedraws a line, Uncle Peck won't cross it.</p><p> Of course, by the end of the scene we see Uncle Peck unbuttoning her blouse (with permission!) and gently stroking his niece'snipples and then kissing them.</p><p> Of course, it's the very illusion of power for a teenager that is so seductive.</p><p> L'il Bit can never truly be in control,because Uncle Peck has been preying on hervulnerability since she was 11 years old.</p><p> She'll be incollege before L'il Bit can sort of find the courage tobreak away from his grip and even then we'll see herrepeating the cycle of abuse by sleeping with a high school boy when she's a teacher.</p><p> The play itself is excellent and Berg captures the self-deluding emotions of a molester like Uncle Peck very well.</p><p> Less successful is Lipman's own journey as L'il Bit.</p><p> Shestrikes mostly the same note throughout the show, not capturing as well the girl'sjourney from friendly niece to confused but flattered teen to angry adult.</p><p> It's a verysubtle, challenging part she might well nail down as the run progresses.</p><p> The others are even less successful, though Edmund has his best moment as the high school boy L'il Bit sleeps with while Holland Hamilton makes the most of her big scene when she acknowledges to the audience at least some understanding of Peck's proclivities.</p><p> Having the voice-over be done by a cast member and the awkward presence of apicnic bench dominating the tiny space throughout were also not the best choices.</p><p> It's Berg I'll remember best and the show is worth seeing to savor his talent.</p><p> Uncle Peck is charming and pathetic, needy and controlling, sad and sadistic -- he's so goodat engaging your emotions you almost feel bizarrely betrayed for L'il Bit when wediscover he's also molested at least one little boy.</p><p> Sure, he's screwed up, but youalmost want to believe he's monstrously screwed up specifically and only for L'il Bit,as if that would make the betrayal less monstrous.</p><p> It wouldn't and that's the power ofHow I Learned To Drive -- forcing you to deal afresh with the horror of pedophilia without the easy dismissal of pedophiles as "monsters." They're people and that's far,far worse.</p><p> THE THEATER OF 2013 (on a four star scale) The Other Place ** 1/2 Picnic * 1/2 Opus No. 7 ** 1/2 Deceit * 1/2 Life And Times Episodes 1-4 ** Cat On A
