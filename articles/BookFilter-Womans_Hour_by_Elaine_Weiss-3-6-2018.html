Amendment.</p><p> This is a ticking clock scenario: 35 states have ratified itand the rest (mostly former slave states) have rejected it or won't even bother considering the idea of women voting.</p><p> Thatleaves Tennessee.</p><p> If women don't achieve victory there, they must start from scratch and wait years or perhaps a decade or longer to garner their basic civil rights.</p><p> Weiss captures the urgency.</p><p> Better still, this is often a battle between women, not women pleading with men.</p><p> Major figures include the head of the traditional, more socially respectable suffragettemovement founded by Susan B.</p><p> Anthony and others; the fighters in the more radical wing unafraid of pickets and ACT-Upstyle stunts; and the formidable women campaigning against suffrage.</p><p> It's all here, including Bribery, heart attacks,mysterious trouble-makers, false charges, petty and un-admirable abandonment of racial justice (no one is a saint) to achieve a (partial) victory.</p><p> Without obscuring the main narrative, Weiss offers deft histories of the movement, its racial politics, biographies of key players and more.</p><p> If you don't know your
