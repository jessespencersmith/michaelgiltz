after the 1994 genocide.</p><p> Aghion has released a series ofacclaimed documentaries that have been seen inRwanda and around the world, focusing especially on the gacaca trials.</p><p> This film was set to debut in Rwanda as part of the 15th anniversary ceremonies marking the genocide.</p><p> Then Cannes made its offer with the usual proviso that the film could not debut elsewhere.</p><p> Aghion deliberated and regretfully chose to declinethe honor of the initial Rwanda premiere in exchange for the chance the film had to be seen by the worldwide media and get its story out to a wider audience.</p><p> And all this was done with the knowledge that -- Michael Moore notwithstanding -- documentaries, especially ones not inCompetition, rarely get any attention at the festival.</p><p> That risk paid off.</p><p> The film has received a lot of notice and strong across the board reviews, with Screen International calling it "remarkable," Hollywood Reporter describing it as "excellent," Newsweek saying it is "worth your time" (high praise at a festival loaded with hundreds if not thousands of movies), and the Los Angeles Times labeling it "quietly devastating." The gacaca trials are an extraordinary event: people who watched their families get slaughtered are asked to confront the killers in the hope of some sort of reconciliation.</p><p> The killers, in exchange foran admission of guilt, get a reduced sentence. (The accused invariably try to admit to as little aspossible while the survivors fight despair and hopelessness just to find the strength to take part.)
