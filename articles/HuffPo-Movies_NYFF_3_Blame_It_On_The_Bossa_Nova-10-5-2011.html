diversions.</p><p> It's moving, strange, weird, hypnotic and quite wonderful.</p><p> Patience (After Sebald) is a tribute to all that.</p><p> It's not a film like London and it doesn't try to adapt the novel, which would seem as impossible as turning The Unbearable Lightness of Being into a movie (and yet, that film was a classic in its own right).</p><p> Instead it's an awkward, murky hybrid.</p><p> The film travels to some of the spots Sebald's book ventures to in its walking tour, but then immediately assures us that trying to trace that path is fruitless and frustrating.</p><p> Voice-overs offer chunks of the book at random.</p><p> Much of the time is spent talking to artists who love the book and venture tentatively but with deeprespect to explain why.</p><p> Their grappling with it is amusing and bizarre.</p><p> The sanest person made a "lit- map," using GoogleEarth to pinpoint all the locations the book hits upon and including text from the novel itself in this art piece.</p><p> Others are far, far more convoluted and confusing.</p><p> If you haven't read Sebald, I imagine you'd leave the film wondering exactly what the heck this Sebald guy had written.</p><p> The film muddies the issue even though it spends most of its time trying to describe it,or rather explaining how difficult it is to describe it.</p><p> I fear anyone not already a fan Stefano Savona was on the scene for the Egyptian uprising very early on.</p><p> He won the trust of people risking their lives (and the lives of their families and friends) to overthrow a brutal dictator and filmed throughout the entire remarkable series of triumphs, setbacks and ultimate victory. (Or rather, the signal victory of deposing Hosni Mubarak; establishing a vital democracy is a work in progress.) It's an exciting prospect that the film ultimately doesn't fulfill.</p><p> They're in the midst of many of the major moments, even getting on stage or near top officials for key speeches.</p><p> But we know very little about theyoung people we're watching argue and debate and hold fast while events spiral out of control.</p><p> At the end of the movie, they're almost as much a mystery as they were at the beginning.</p><p> Also, the filmmaker clearly found a warm welcome among those pushing for a secular democracy.</p><p> The splintering MuslimBrotherhood and its offshoots are shadowy presences that we gain no clear understanding of at any point.</p><p> MOST POPULAR ON HUFFPOST 1 of 2 Fox News Viewers Know Less Than People Who Watch No News: Study Loyal Dog Refuses To Leave Owner's Grave, Goes Week Without Food Charlie Angus What if They Declared an Emergency and No One Came? Let's just gather as many film and concert and video clips that we can of the best artists in the world singing Jobim's music and cut them together into one glorious film.</p><p> Wonderful! The movie gets off to a strong start, combining concert footage with scenes of life in Brazil to show the world Jobim came from.</p><p> Soon we glimpse scenes of carnival and Black Orpheus, the movie that sent bossa nova into the stratosphere for many people around the world.</p><p> Soon enough there was "The Girlfrom Ipanema" -- one of the most recorded songs of all time -- and the rest is history.</p><p> We see artists like Gal Costa and Ella Fitzgerald and Elis Regina and Judy Garland and Dizzy Gillespie and Caetano Veloso and on and on and on all delivering his many classics with verve and style.</p><p> We also hear bits of hissymphonies and art song ventures, though the movie always returns to the songs that have become standards.</p><p> So what's the problem? First, almost no songs are heard in their entirety, or to be more exact we almost never get to hear one artist cover a song from beginning to end. (It does happen once or maybe twice but this is clearly the exception.) That works great for "The Girl From Ipanema." At the center of the film thatsong is turned into a mega-medley with a fast version dissolving into a slow version into a version sung inEnglish into a jazz instrumental into a (happily brief) contemporary dance pop version) and on.</p><p> That showcase deftly demonstrates what a popular song it is and how a standard can be adapted brilliantly into so many styles.</p><p> Unfortunately, to a lesser degree that's how every single song is treated.</p><p> Judy Garland may be breaking your heart but you can be certain we'll cut away from her to another version.</p><p> Great performance afterperformance is cut short so they can move onto another one.</p><p> But a third of three great performances most assuredly does not add up to one great musical moment.</p><p> All three are ultimately lessened.</p><p> Music According to Jobim becomes 90 minutes of teasing.</p><p> By the end, the movie reaches for a grand finale by drawing from performances at all-star tributeconcerts.</p><p> Seeing a glittering array of stars on stage certainly makes clear how cherished Jobim hadbecome by the end of his life.</p><p> But group sing-alongs are rarely interesting musically so the movie climaxes with some of the least interesting clips.</p><p> I may not be right, but I also think Stan Getz wasn't included; maybe no good film footage was available but that's a shame as well, though of course manyother acts couldn't be fit in either.</p><p> A movie that let the songs breathe, that chose certain performances and let them play out would have been much, much more satisfying.</p><p> It can't help but send filmgoers out to their local record stores tocheck out some of the marvelous music heard in the movie.</p><p> But it should have been a satisfying
