It's roughly chronological but often many chapters feel likestand-alone features that might have appeared in The New Yorker itself: here's a chapter on excessive drinking,there's a chapter on romantic and marital woes, yet another on the war and the inevitable final chapter as they filetheir final stories once and for all.</p><p> Indeed, it would play better as a serialized offering over a year, say one a month.Read all at once, it is exhaustive and exhausting.</p><p> Gibbs played a central role in the magazine as both editor andreviewer and writer, but much is explained when Vinciguerra vertebra, osteoporosis, akidney infection, and congestive heart failure." The accretion of detail is admirable at first but eventually frustrating.Still, Vinciguerra usually keeps all their foibles and brilliance in perspective.</p><p> He's a little too kind at times. More problematic is that this truly is about a cast of characters.</p><p> Each vivid, eccentric character getsour full attention, one after the other.</p><p> The New Yorker itself is a little lost in all this, with little sense of forwardmomentum allowed to develop.</p><p> Sure, highlights like the takedown of Time magazine and John Hersey's Hiroshimapiece are captured.</p><p> But this is a series of portraits more than the journey of the magazine itself.</p><p> Still, it's intelligent,well-grounded, and moment-to-moment entertaining.</p><p> With a cast of characters like this, how could it fail?
