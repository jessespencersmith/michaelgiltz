To my shock, it didn't even appear on RollingStone's list of the 50 Best Albums of 2015, when of course it should have been #1.</p><p> At least it was in the Top 10 and the Village Voice's vaunted Pazz And Jop poll. (If you're wondering, I've heard most everything on theRS list and much in the Top 100 of the Pazz poll.) Maybe you're notfamiliar with Stevens? He delivered what is widely considered one of the best albums of the 2000s: Come On Feel The Illinoise, a sprawing, everything-but-the-kitchen-sink masterpiece as ambitious and exciting and diverse in style and form as one could ask.</p><p> This is it's polar opposite.I first heard it under ideal conditions: driving in a car, late at night on a darkened highway with no idea of what it was like other than "the new Sufjan Stevens." Turns out it's a 3 in the morning, Nick Drake sort of album, fragile and quiet and when it was finished, I thought, "Well, somebody died!" Lyrically, musically, emotionally, the Angel of Deathhovers over this one and blesses it with the beat of its wings.</p><p> Turns outthe mother of Stevens did in fact die and this album grapples with that.</p><p> Becoming an orphan (or a semi-orphan if your other parent is still alive) is always notable.</p><p> But Stevens reportedly had a complicated relationshipwith her, to say the least.</p><p> It's a testament to the work that I didn't knowany of this but felt churned up emotionally, almost holding my breath at certain songs, afraid to break the spell.</p><p> It's no less sonically ambitious than Illinoise but that boldness is far more subtle here.</p><p> Stevens and the people recording with him do remarkable things here with analog, the hiss of audio, the textures of recording that others can appreciate and break down better than me.</p><p> All I know is listening to the album is a transfixing experience.</p><p> Is it depressing? Not at all.</p><p> Any art that tacklesdeep, dark issues and can produce a lasting work is to me a joyful act, however sad the story at hand.</p><p> Illinoise was hailed with hosannas the moment it arrived.</p><p> This one will grow in stature and endure.</p><p> It's a thing of rare beauty. 2.</p><p> JILL SCOTT - Woman I think I love Jill Scott.</p><p> She's smart and sexy and frank and funny and her new album is just the latest to declare her brilliance at picking up where Prince left off. "Closure" makes me laugh every time I listen to it but the pleasures to be had are many: gospel, blues, pop, soul, spoken word, the personal and the political.</p><p> It's all hereand all good.</p><p> How many more excellent albums does she have to create before people wake up to her talent? 3.</p><p> KENDRICK LAMAR - To Pimp A Butterfly This is it, the album anointed the best of the year by the critics and - who knows - maybe even the Grammys on Monday.</p><p> It's a stellar follow-up to Good Kid, M.A.A.D.</p><p> City.Lamar took the success of that album and ran with it, working in livemusicians, jazz, soul, pop and pretty much everything else you can think of into his "state of the union" masterpiece.</p><p> No, let's not call it his masterpiece because I think he's just getting started? You've beendigging the cast album to Hamilton, marveling at how hip-hop canilluminate the complexity of America back in 1776? Check this out to hear how it illuminates America in 2016. 4.</p><p> CHRIS STAPLETON -- Traveller -- What are they drinking in Nashville.</p><p> Every year seems to bring up a new winner for Sturgill Simpson in 2014 to this absolute killer album in 2015.</p><p> Unlike Simpson, the wheel remains the same -- no reinvention here.</p><p> Just great songs, great musicianship, great singing.</p><p> Except for the maudlin "Daddy Doesn't Pray Anymore" -- a traditional country tune in the bad sense, right down to the "surprise"twist at the end -- this is a sheer pleasure. 5.</p><p> THE TALLEST MAN ON EARTH - Dark Bird Is Home It's been a decade now that I've been digging this artist, a guy from Sweden whosings in English, plays the guitar with flair, digs Dylan and Guthrie, has a folkie vibe and yet from the start has followed his own melodic, rock and roll heart.</p><p> I can't say he keeps getting better because he started out sensationally good.</p><p> But he has been slowly, gently expanding his musicalrange.</p><p> First it seemed like it was just him and a guitar.</p><p> An EP followed by an album and then another EP and then another album, each one opening up to a fuller sound without ever sacrificing the pure lyricism thathas struck a chord with fans worldwide.</p><p> This growth has been so gradualthat when his new album includes a full band and the first track opens up wider and wider with sound it feels like a movie that suddenly expands into Panavision. (See Xavier Dolan's Mommy for the cinematic idea I'm driving at.) Really, it's not that radical: if you're new to him, you'll just heara terrific, lovely, actually rather restrained work of folkish rock and roll bursting with great tunes, incisive lyrics and winning vocals, always anchored by his sterling guitar work.</p><p> For long time fans like myself, it'slike being launched into space.</p><p> Catch him before he goes into orbit. 6.</p><p> COURTNEY BARNETT - Sometimes I Just Sit And Think And Sometimes I Just Sit As good as it is, I'm a little wary of this.</p><p> The poor woman had barely released an EP when she was being dubbed the new Dylan.</p><p> This debut album was immediately clobbered with hype that saidwe could now expect decades of brilliant work from this amazing artist.</p><p> Leave her alone! Can't we just let her actually produce some music first before shoving Joni Mitchell and Neil Young aside in the pantheon ofgreats? Truth be told, humor has never been a valued commodity in rockand roll and Barnett is hilarious.</p><p> So her immediate acceptance is welcome.</p><p> She's got a deadpan delivery and some really amusing lyrics and a beguiling guitar style that's so direct you'd think it was indirect, ifthat makes any sense.</p><p> I'm not sure how much this music will bear repeating but it sure was a hoot on first blush.</p><p> It's compelling, strange, wonderfully weird and all that.</p><p> I'm just worried the next album will becompelling, strange and wonderfully weird in all the same ways, a la a one-trick pony.</p><p> There's no denying it's hugely effective but I'd like to delay the coronation for a while, please.</p><p> Now let her tour and develop and let's see what she comes up with next. 7.</p><p> TONY BENNETT AND BILL CHARLAP - The Silver Lining One of the greats, Tony Bennett has sold more albums, won more Grammys and made more new fans as a senior citizen than he ever did before.</p><p> The pityis that most of his recent albums can't hold a candle to his best.</p><p> Even though I was tickled as much as anyone to see him on MTV Unplugged, that live album was not the artistic peak of his career.</p><p> And I shudder tothink many fans own Duets and nothing else.</p><p> Still, his album with kd lang back in 2002 was a gem.</p><p> And now we've got another one to celebrate.This collaboration with pianist Bill Charlap of course harkens back to oneof the absolute peaks in his recording career: the two albums Bennett didwith the great Bill Evans.</p><p> This delight is damn near as good as those and those are about as good as Bennett gets.</p><p> They're paying tribute to Jerome Kern and while his voice may be rough with age, he can zero inon a lyric like nobody's business.</p><p> Charlap (who has done the same on two great albums with his mom, singer Sandy Stewart) is an accompanist who can swing and provides an ideally sympathetic partner for Bennett.The irony is that Bennett has been so lauded in recent years that nowwhen he really does deserve the Grammy for Best Jazz Vocal Album, he may get overlooked.</p><p> Don't you do the same.8.</p><p> THE FRATELLIS - Not quite.</p><p> Yet with those pipes and the intense glare of the media, maybe I'm reacting to the noise of what surrounds her rather than the music itself.</p><p> And hell, I was there for "Chasing Pavements" and neverexpected all of this so I'm not ready to stop listening yet. 37.</p><p> THE MAVERICKS - Mono The reunion of this group has been a joy and not just because it meant I got to see them live in concert again. I was so happy to hear 2013's In Time that I couldn't quite be sure how good it was.</p><p> The first time around, they'd gone wackier and wackier and frankly I loved the country-politan sound of Music For All Occasions and the bold eccentricity of Trampoline .</p><p> Their "final" album seemed a retreat and now their return seemed rootedperhaps in familiar territory.</p><p> But each album has boasted a handful oftunes that just kill in performance and they are solid, as if the band knowswho they are now and has nothing to prove other than that they can still deliver.</p><p> Comfortable in their own skin. 38.</p><p> ANDERSON EAST - Delilah Speaking of Boz Scaggs and Van Morrison, here's a debut album from a kid who wants to keep the soul flame burning.</p><p> Anyone listening to Leon Bridges should put that down and check this out.</p><p> Occasionally stunning, always solid, this is a veryappealing opener.</p><p> Can't wait to see him live. 39.</p><p> DON HENLEY - Cass County Yet another veteran on my list.</p><p> I don't believe I've ever had the Eagles or Don Henley solo on my list for the best of the year.</p><p> Mind you, the Eagles' Greatest Hits is an essential and I'vealways loved Henley's voice. Well, those elicit anger and confusion and sadness and derision.</p><p> When Dylanreleased this album of standards, a tribute to Frank Sinatra recorded not with strings or a jazz combo but his touring band, the general reaction was WTF? (In contrast, his recent album of Christmas music got a shrug.)Dylan's been here before.</p><p> Anger and confusion and villagers armed with pitchforks and torches greeted Dylan when he went electric, when he went country, when he found Jesus (and then lost Him again).</p><p> Heck, themost lambasted album of his career - Self Portrait - came from recording sessions that have now produced music that has become widely acknowledged as pretty fricking amazing.</p><p> Those sessions, not to mention his cover albums from the 1990s and his constant references lyrically andmusically to classic folk tunes and poems of the past as well as the wide musical palette he's employed drawing in everything from Stephen Foster to swamp music have made one thing abundantly clear: Bob Dylan drawsupon popular music.</p><p> All of it.</p><p> Of course the natural comparison is WillieNelson's Stardust , an album greeted I believe with confusion and a little indifference until it steamrolled into a remarkable commercial hit andbecame recognized as a classic.</p><p> Nelson has always wandered far andwide into different genres (reggae and blues and so on) while Dylan hastaken up various strands and woven them into his own unique and distinctive style.</p><p> So here he is, one of the most influential singers of all time, tackling some standards.</p><p> At first listen, I was intrigued if not floored.At second listen, I thought, let me take a break from this for a while.</p><p> I'mjust not sure what I think.</p><p> Obviously, this wasn't one of his tossed off albums; he was singing with care and attention to the lyrics.</p><p> But was it good? Well, it's definitely good; but how good I can't quite say yet.</p><p> Butover the course of months of returning to it when the mood struck,Shadows In The Night has deepened for me, become stronger, sturdier, more moving.</p><p> Why this rush to judgement? Sometimes, it takes a while toappreciate an album's strengths (and its flaws, for that matter).</p><p> Peoplemake up their minds about an artist and just close their ears.</p><p> DavidBowie's death was mourned to a remarkable degree.</p><p> But the truth is that until 2013's The Next Day , the world had spent 30 years pretty much dismissing everything he recorded from Tin Machine to solo work.</p><p> The albums were listened to once, if at all, and then tossed aside. (I include myself here.</p><p> A good friend pointed out for years that the people who ignored Bowie's post Let's Dance music hadn't really listened to Bowie's post Let's Dance music, not really.</p><p> Don't great artists deserve more careful attention? A year from now, I'll bet Shadows In The Night climbs up higher on this list.</p><p> Five years from now? Ten? We'll see.</p><p> He's recorded his share of clunkers over the years.</p><p> How could any real artist not do so?But it's never smart to bet against Dylan.</p><p> Like Carrie & Lowell, this has a fragile beauty, with Dylan allowing his voice to waver and sigh, sounding as vulnerable as ever, whether tackling "Some Enchanted Evening" or pondering "What'll I Do?" It may be his most emotional, open-hearted album.</p><p> Why this? Why now? What can I say? All I know is that I keep coming back to it, puzzling it over or just letting its quiet tenderness washover me. "Who can explain it? Who can tell you why?" he declaims at onepoint. "Fools give you reasons.</p><p> Wise men never try." FADE OUT Looking back at my list, I'm surprised by how many veterans popped up on it.</p><p> I certainly didn't hear the year that way, at least not until I actually looked at what I listened to and rated highly.</p><p> It's a good year for some oldfriends! And yet, I've never listened to more new albums than ever before.Maybe that's why I assumed this list would be dominated by newer acts.</p><p> Instead, I discovered Los Lobos and the like made a good showing in 2015.</p><p> Why's this? I think it's the tidal wave of music available.</p><p> It used to be only critics who got swamped by a flood of CDs (or cassettes or LPs or whatever).</p><p> But even Robert Christgau never had quite as many actualalbums in his office as any casual fan can access on Pandora right now.And when the options grow and grow, it's no shocker to realize you gravitate to familiar territory, checking out the latest from familiar acts.</p><p> I miss the gatekeepers: it's fun to stumble upon an album that's barely been released anywhere in the world like Humble Orange Band.</p><p> But finding that meant I also plowed through a LOT of albums that never would have made it on my radar in the past since they would have had to make it to a label and then the publicist would have to decide to forward it to me.</p><p> That system had its minuses, but stemming the rising waterswas one of the positives.</p><p> This seems to extend to genres as well: I've lost track of a lot of world music and jazz and other, less-beaten paths I've known and loved in thepast.</p><p> Plus, I'm getting older and find myself increasingly drawn to what for lack of a better word I'll call Americana, all those strains of music that draw upon folk and blues and the like.</p><p> Or maybe it's the hard times I see all around me, from the homeless people I pass sleeping on the sidewalk every cold night or the smallbusiness owners who welcome me haunting their diners late at night justso they have someone to talk to and people walking by know they're still open.</p><p> In hard times, sometimes the truth rings louder strummed on a guitar.</p><p> In 2016, I'll try to be more discerning AND more broad-minded in terms of style.</p><p> Now tell me what albums I foolishly left off!
