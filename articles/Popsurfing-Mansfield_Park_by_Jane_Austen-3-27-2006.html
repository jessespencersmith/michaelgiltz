M O N D AY, M A R C H 2 7 , 2 0 0 6"Mansfield Park" by Jane Austen -- The PopsurfingReviewMansfield Park by Jane Austen ** 1/2 (out of 4)I've been working my way through the collected works of Jane Austen.If you're in NYC, a small bookstore just two blocks uptown of FilmForum has a great little boxed set containing all her works in tiny littlevolumes with good-sized type. (Most novels are offered in two books,ie.</p><p> Pride & Prejudice Vol. 1 and 2.) At $15 for the whole set, it's muchcheaper than buying paperbacks and the little volumes are so pleasingand so perfect for carrying on the subway, if I were filthy rich I wouldhave every new book I wanted to read custom-published this very sameway.</p><p> Pride & Prejudice was nigh on perfect and Sense and Sensibilitywas only slightly less so.</p><p> Both were a delight to read.</p><p> So I'm all thesorrier to report MP is a bit of a let-down.</p><p> In her first two books,Austen created spirited, wittily charming heroines.</p><p> The center of thisbook, Fanny Price, is a bit of a pill.</p><p> She's timid in thought and deed, soher musings are far less satisfying.</p><p> Fanny is so self-less that whenrelatives take her in and then treat her poorly, she's ashamed to letsuch ignoble thoughts cross her mind.</p><p> If she's shivering in her dayroombecause a fire is forbidden to her even in winter, Fanny immediatelydenounces herself for wishing for one instead of realizing how luckyshe is.</p><p> The "bad" girl of the book is Mary Crawford, who perks thingsup whenever she's around.</p><p> Mary and her brother Henry wreak havocon Fanny's relatives.</p><p> Mary sets her sights on Fanny's secret belovedEdmund (also quite a prig) and Henry toys with two other youngladies, getting them both to fall in love with him before moving blithelyon.</p><p> The decision of the young people to mount an amateur theatrical--such scandalous behavior shocks Fanny, of course -- is the subject ofendless discussion and reverberates tiresomely throughout the book.By the finale, Austen's usual canny insights into human behavior arereduced to moralistic scolding.</p><p> I imagine Austen didn't want to repeatherself with another vivacious heroine.</p><p> Too bad.</p><p> Mary would have beenmuch more fun to spend time with.</p><p> At one point, I had the delicioushope my expectations would be undermined.</p><p> It seemed quite possiblethat Mary and Henry, by being determined to get Edmund and Fannyto love them, would in fact become reformed and thus genuinelyworthy of their love.</p><p> It would have been a most welcome development,but unfortunately Austen let
