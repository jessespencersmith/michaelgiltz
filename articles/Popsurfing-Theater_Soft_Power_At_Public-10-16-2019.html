Will healways be too Chinese for some Americans and too American for someChinese? Where does he belong? Where does anyone belong? And why do musicals starring Asians appear on Broadway only once every decade or so? And why is it almost always a revival of The King and I? Like any real artist, Hwang turned his pain and probing into art.</p><p> Soft Power is an awkward, ugly duckling of a play that yearns to transform into a swan of a musical.</p><p> It's a mess but boy is his heart in the rightplace: on his sleeve.</p><p> Actually, I've just described the set-up of the show.</p><p> In it, the character DHH (a too-earnest Francis Jue) is meeting with a producer from Shanghai named Xūe Xíng (Conrad Ricamora).</p><p> Xūe company wants to turn Shanghai into the Broadway of Asia (though it kind of already is),starting with a big, fat, American-style musical with a Chineseperspective.</p><p> And they want DHH to write it!MICHAEL GILTZ AT WORK Michael Giltz is a freelance writer based in NYC and can be reached atmgiltz@pipeline.com FAVORITE LINKS Americablog Five O'Clock Lightning baseball blogDeep Pop -- Lori Lakin's Blog The Back Page -- Jason Page on ESPN Radio Cine-Blog -- George Robinson's Blog Documents On Art & Cinema - Daryl Chin's Blog Brucie G's Wondrous Blog Of Adventure and Mystery -- Bruce Greenspan's Blog BLOG ARCHIVE ▼ 2019 (81) ► November (1) ▼ October (21)The movie they want him to adapt into this show? You might imagine Hwang delivering a scathing take-down of the US or a sly skewering of China's imagined fears about America anddemocracy.</p><p> But you'll get neither.</p><p> Soft Power is indeed soft in every way -- it's gentle, earnest, polite and really not out to offend much of anyone.</p><p> Are we meant to be amused by China's distorted idea of America or perhaps laugh at ourselves when seeing the US throughthe eyes of outsiders? Neither happens.</p><p> McDonald's as a symbol of USconsumerism? Well, sure. (And hey, they've got one in Beijing.) Anobsession with guns? Duh.</p><p> If that's how China sees us, well, they'repretty spot on there too, aren't they? So what's the point? It's a treat to see an almost all-Asian cast in a musical.</p><p> But that's not enough.</p><p> Soft Power is too timid to do more.</p><p> If this is the flip-side of The King and I, why is Xūe talking to Hillary? She lost, Hwang! Hillary is not the leader of the country.</p><p> Xūe (or really, a Chinesewoman) should be civilizing Trump .</p><p> I can't blame Hwang for not wanting to grapple with Agent Orange, even in his imagination.</p><p> But ifhe's going to update The King and I, that's what he needed to do.</p><p> Since they kept Clinton, why cast a white woman? Alyse Alann Louis has a lot of fun in dual roles, especially when Hillary is chowing down on ice cream and pizza while singing the blues and surely her casting isthere to clarify the gap between East and West.</p><p> But since Broadway(and Hollywood) has such a long history of casting white people in yellow face, surely it would have been more on point to include, say, the sole Japanese actor in a sea of Chinese faces to play the whitewoman. (Or at least a woman of color.) This certainly isn't a criticismof Louis, one of the show's bright points.</p><p> The cast is game for this grab-bag of a goof, even though Saturday Night Live tries to be more pointed on a weekly basis. (And South Park actually succeeds.) By far the show's best element is Conrad Ricamora as Xūe.</p><p> He's sexy, charming, magnetic and all on his ownrescues the evening.</p><p> Ricamora was terrific in David Byrne's Here Lies Love.</p><p> You can have fun going through the Playbill and spotting how many cast members appeared in that show.</p><p> It's a testament to the top-notch talent here,but also a comment on how few roles written for Asian actors.</p><p> Ricamora's Broadway debut after that breakthrough? is terrific in the serious drama, the romance and the song-and-dance, not to mention smoothly delivering two different accents.</p><p> Thank goodness his hit TVshow How To Get Away With Murder has just begun its sixth and final season.</p><p> Ricamora should be available for a lot more theater andfilm work soon.</p><p> But this is mostly a musical and I fear the melodies by Jeanine Tesori are akin to her work on Caroline, Or Change and Fun Home.</p><p> Sometimes her score can be beautiful and the 22-piece orchestra certainly does it justice.</p><p> Yet she has no interest in melody and it shows.</p><p> I don't need Jerry Herman, but I need something and Tesori offers it up rarely and grudgingly.</p><p> You'll find no equivalent to Fun Home's"Ring Of Keys" here, though in its way "Happy Enough" comes close.</p><p> That song is also the show's best effort at exploring cultural differences between the US and China.</p><p> Stereotypically, the US champions theindividual, while Chinese culture celebrates the greater good.</p><p> The songoffers a nuanced moment and is worthy of Hwang's best intentions --here, he's not spoofing or making a point, merely revealing.</p><p> Otherwise,he can't bring himself to question much about the US any more than he can acknowledge that Trump won and is the real King right now.</p><p> If this show were the mirror-image of that Rodgers & Hammerstein show, it would end with the the King being "civilized" by the Chineseinterloper who offers a new way of ruling.</p><p> Instead, Hillary firmly rejects Xūe's idea of a one-party state or anything less than full democracy.</p><p> If there's anything much to learn from Chinese culture (asopposed to the current Chinese government, of course), Soft Powerdoesn't grapple with it.</p><p> No mistake."
