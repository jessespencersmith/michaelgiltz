Theater: The 'Big Fish' That Got Away; Poor Time Killer BIG FISH ** out of **** A TIME TO KILL * 1/2 out of **** BIG FISH ** out of **** NEIL SIMON THEATRE One holiday season years ago, my siblings were all back home.</p><p> I would be there in a few days but they called me and put me on speakerphone with everyone listening. "We'rehaving an argument," said my brother Chris, "and we need you to settle it.</p><p> Just say thefirst thing that comes to mind.</p><p> What did dad do during the war?" I answeredimmediately, "He was a nurse during the war, right down to the big hat with wings thatjutted out on the side, like the nuns wore." Everyone burst out laughing while I heard mydad good-naturedly exclaiming, "I never said that!" But of course he had, along with a million other stories, some true, many with a grain of truth and more than a few completely untrue but fun and why let facts get in the way of agood story? My father dined and drank with everyone from the Pointer Sisters (probablytrue, since he barely knew who they were) to Jackie Gleason.</p><p> He studied acting under LeeStrasberg. (Maybe, since he says it amounted to a few lessons, Strasberg told him he wasterrible and that was it.) He dated actresses, including Constance Towers and Jean Peters(who starred in Pickup On South Street but is best known for having married Howard Hughes).</p><p> A Texas husband and wife -- millionaires in oil, I assume but definitelymillionaires -- wanted to adopt him when they met my dad while he worked at theCommodore Hotel in New York City.</p><p> His boss showered dad with tickets to every sportingevent and show in town though I can't imagine my dad at a Broadway musical, much lessthe opera.</p><p> It was the hotel for athletes, so my dad met every sports star under the sun atthat time...and then went out drinking with them of course.</p><p> He could make up songs about anything on the spot, thrilling children who begged him to write them down because they were so good.</p><p> If an adult was listening, they would understand why he didn't bother to write down the lyrics and melodies to originals like "Singin' In The Rain." He hated music but was constantly humming songs.</p><p> His family owned the rights to land inChicago where the stockyards are built and if only we could find the original deed (placedin a family Bible since lost) we'd be rich.</p><p> That's what he gets for not reading the Bibleregularly... or at all.</p><p> He traveled the world and you can name any city, state or country and he'd immediately insist he knew someone who lived there, a great friend who would love to put you up if you were traveling through.</p><p> Many years ago, before most of the people he met starteddying off, it might even have been true.</p><p> He lived all over Asia, was friends with drugsmugglers in the Virgin Islands, worked for big corporations, launched small businessesand somewhere in Hong Kong there might be a safety deposit box containing...gold?Jewels? Deeds or other valuables? Or perhaps just information on his career as a spy.</p><p> Last May 14, 2014 This is the print preview: Back to normal view Â» Posted: 10/24/2013 6:05 pmmonth, one of my siblings said he hinted for the first time at involvement in the Kennedy assassination.</p><p> But he lived in Bermuda at the time, I said.</p><p> Well, then the planning of the Kennedy assassination.</p><p> If I take my dad to dinner, he'll never stop talking.</p><p> But he would be just as likely -- make that more likely -- to be talking to the waiter, the bartender, thepeople at the table two seats away than to actually talk to me.</p><p> He's 86 years old and sofrail that this morning he fell down on the sidewalk trying to walk from his car to arestaurant.</p><p> In short, if you're making a Broadway musical about a son whose father is dying and has spent his entire life spinning tall tales rather than opening up and sharing the truth, byGod, I'm your audience.</p><p> The musical Big Fish is filled with talent on stage and off but it doesn't remotely begin to tell the frustrating, bitter, sweet tale delivered in the novel by Daniel Wallace.</p><p> Like the film version by Tim Burton, this show thinks the real magic is in the tall tales that Edward Bloom (Norbert Leo Butz) tells to his wife Sandra (Kate Baldwin), his son Will (Bobby Steggert) or anyone that will listen.</p><p> But in fact the few brief moments of emotion and charm occur in the quiet real world scenes where Will is comforting his dying father orSandra expresses her devotion in "I Don't Need A Roof." The fantasy scenes, the tall tales,the too-good-to-be-true stories? They're only fun if you haven't heard them a hundred times before.</p><p> Unlike the film, this take on the story clearly shows the frustrating part of such a charming father.</p><p> Edward takes over his son's wedding, interrupts their first dance andeven jumps the gun by announcing his new daughter in law (Krystal Joy Brown) ispregnant.</p><p> Will is furious and wishes for once -- just for once -- his dad would stopspinning tales and just tell the truth.</p><p> The rest of the show is filled with the dad's fancifulstories and the son's desire to learn the truth about his dad's mysterious past.</p><p> It's telling that every time we cut to one of those magical tales that the show stops cold: dad meets a witch, befriends a giant, joins a circus, saves a town and none of it isengaging or interesting.</p><p> That's the point of the book.</p><p> Those tales are fun to outsiders butin fact they're simply a way of opening up and being honest, of engaging with reality.That's precisely why they appeal to strangers and drive family members crazy.</p><p> There's aflip side: the tales can inspire imagination and creativity; they can open a mind thepossibilities of the wide world.</p><p> It takes Will the entire show to realize his dad really didlove him and was proud of his son.</p><p> That's good, but it doesn't change the fact that his dadhas to make every moment about himself, steal the spotlight and avoid anything remotelyapproaching a heartfelt conversation when it matters.</p><p> The book is by John August, who made similar mistakes on the screenplay.</p><p> The music and lyrics are by Andrew Lippa but despite the best cast one could hope for, they rarely soar.Perhaps the most unfortunate element is the scenic design by Julian Crouch that combines with the projection design of Benjamin Pearcy for 59 Productions.</p><p> The basic framework are wooden slats, which are fine for the small town country home where webegin.</p><p> But they're terribly distracting and unattractive when it comes to the cave of thegiant or New York City (even Central Park looks more urban than that) and pretty muchevery other setting for the story."The Witch," where Edward meets a witch and sees the future, looks like an outtake from Into The Woods and is murky and ineffective.</p><p> The Calloway Circus and its performers seem to have wandered over from Pippin (the costumes are by the esteemed William Ivey Long).</p><p> And the act one finale "Daffodils" includes a field of daffodils that isunderwhelming if modestly pretty as they pop up awkwardly out of the stage floor.</p><p> It's a dreadful shame Kate Baldwin and Bobby Steggert aren't starring on Broadway in Giant , the excellent musical they starred in at the Public but which has yet to transfer. instead, they have fairly uninteresting parts.</p><p> Baldwin is especially woeful as the long- suffering wife of Edward.</p><p> The first time she meets Edward is one of the most effective numbers in the show because it's actually about something.</p><p> But by and large she has nothing to do.</p><p> Steggert, one of the best talents in theater today, brings immediate empathy to a role that can sometimes seem like a pill. (Everyone gets annoyed when Will can't realize what great fun his dad is!) But you immediately trust Steggert's heart is in the right place and he sings and acts beautifully.</p><p> What little emotion is present is due in no small part to him.</p><p> Zachary Unger is quite good as the young Will, too.</p><p> Susan Stroman directs with her usual fluidity but the heart of the story was never there to begin with.</p><p> Her choreography, usually so dependable, is not called on much here.</p><p> Anopening bit called "The Alabama Stomp" (Edward insists it makes fish jump right out ofthe river) is diverting but turns out to be the dancing highlight.</p><p> A flashy number at the topof act two -- a U.S.O. number called "Red, White And True" (performed to the hilt byBaldwin) is the very definition of a time-waster both in song and dance.</p><p> Like so many ofthe other songs, it just stalls the story and fills the stage with one of Edward's tales thatdoesn't really advance the story or our understanding of Edward.</p><p> Then there's Butz, who sings and dances and does everything he can to land this Big Fish.</p><p> But pointless songs that go nowhere and fantasy sequences with no purpose can't come tolife even with his formidable skills.</p><p> He's such an opaque character that in a way hebecomes less interesting as the show goes on.</p><p> The fact that Edward is given a real secret -- one that seems utterly pointless, by the way (is he really afraid his wife might find out that he didn't cheat on her once?) -- cheapens the whole musical by the way.</p><p> The truth is not that Edward and men like him arespinning stories for very good reasons.</p><p> This constant distancing between him and realityis a flaw, not a noble pursuit.</p><p> But they can't change, not really.</p><p> The truth is not thatthey're more lovable and noble than you realize; it's that you have to learn to accept andlove them anyway.</p><p> A TIME TO KILL * 1/2 out of **** GOLDEN THEATREI enjoy a good old-fashioned courtroom drama as much as the next fellow.</p><p> And I prepared for this show by reading John Grisham's debut novel -- it's clearly a young man's workfilled with every possible plot twist and stakes-raising event you could ask for.</p><p> But it's alsoricher and more nuanced than most of his legal thrillers and you can see why it's the favorite of Grisham and his fans.</p><p> The feature film helped launch the career of Matthew McConaughey but barely scratched the service of the vivid cast of characters in this tale of rape and Southern justice.</p><p> So i was eagerly prepared to say if you wanted to kill some time that this new play might be a fun way to do it.</p><p> Unfortunately, despite some clever touches by playwright RupertHolmes towards the finale, this Broadway production also fails to capture the best parts ofthe book, not to mention fails in casting, production design, score and even a decentSouthern accent.</p><p> The time is the early 1980s and our story begins with an act of horrific violence: two drunken, hateful white men brutally beat and rape a ten-year-old black girl.</p><p> They areimmediately arrested by the town's black sheriff (Chike Johnson in a part that mainly callsfor him to change his clothes a lot) and one of them confesses to the crime theycommitted.</p><p> But the child's father Carl Lee Hailey (John Douglas Thompson) is mad withrage.</p><p> While his daughter is in the hospital recovering from the nightmarish assault, he gets a gun and kills the two men.</p><p> Enter our young, idealistic attorney Jake Brigance (Sebastian Arcelus) who must somehow get this decent family man set free.</p><p> Everyone agrees if the father were white and therapists black that he'd probably get a medal instead of facing capital punishment.Nonetheless, that's exactly what waits him if Jake can't pull some magic.</p><p> Adding to thedrama is district attorney Rufus R.</p><p> Buckley (Patrick Page) who has his eye on thegovernor's mansion, a gruff judge (Fred Dalton Thompson), an eager beaver assistant (Ashley Williams) who's smart as a whip and quite a temptation while Jake's wife and daughter are way, and a drunken mentor (Tom Skerritt) with an even drunker expert witness in tow (John Procaccino).</p><p> That doesn't even cover the KKK, civil rights protestors, death threats, burning crosses and much more.</p><p> No one would accuse Grisham or Holmes of being skimpy on plot.</p><p> The problems are apparent from the start: the show begins with frantic video of a country road and the voice of a little girl calling for her daddy.</p><p> The handsome courtroom by JamesNoone has wood paneling stretching up to the roof and a rotating stage so we can getslightly different perspectives of the rather static set up (you can't mess too much withtables for the attorneys, a witness stand and so on).</p><p> Unfortunately, it's dominated by videofootage that covers the top half of the stage during transitions.</p><p> Designed by Jeff Sugg, these videos are thoroughly unnecessary attempts to create a feel for the setting and hint at the massive turmoil outside the courtroom where a trickle ofprotestors turns into an NAACP-driven flood and the arrival of KKKers sparks a riot.</p><p> Ontop of that, they're muddy and unattractive.</p><p> This mostly courtroom-based drama fails atrecreating the media frenzy and huge crowds that dominate the imagination of the novel.Flashing country homes and vague imagery of burning buildings only emphasizes how cutoff from this drama we are.</p><p> The score and sound design by Lindsay Jones leans heavily on slide guitar for a Southern feel.</p><p> Fair enough, but this is a tale about the rape of a little girl and a man facing execution: the score is at times so jaunty from scene to scene you'd think we're headed to a juke joint on Saturday night.</p><p> Poor Arcelus starred in the musical Elf , where he had the thankless task of being in the shadow of Will Ferrell's career-best turn as a naive man who thinks he's one of Santa's little helpers.</p><p> Now he's asked to take on a role given life by the sexy charm and guile of McConaughey.</p><p> He barely handles the accent (few on stage do) and is capable but littlemore.</p><p> Tom Skerritt is an excellent actor and in the novel the role of Jake's mentor is a richone.</p><p> It's far less interesting here and the night I caught the show Skerritt notablystumbled over his lines at numerous points (playing a drunk is no cover).</p><p> Fred Dalton Thompson handles the no-nonsense Judge Noose (hey, blame Grisham for the name) well though with no notable Southern twinkle.</p><p> It's not his fault that in the bookthe judge is much more interesting, thanks to a cowardly fear of being second-guessed anda constant supply of drink (hinted at in the opening scene).</p><p> As the father and mother ofthe poor little girl, John Douglas Thompson and Tonya Pinkins add immeasurably to the show.</p><p> They have just a few very brief scenes together but those are the only moments when the import of what has happened actually sink in.</p><p> It's almost cruel to cast the greatPinkins in such a minor role; at least Thompson (an excellent actor in a thin part) hasmore stage time.</p><p> I've saved the worst and best for last.</p><p> Ashley Williams is the eager assistant to Jake.</p><p> She has a string of TV credits to her name, but the stage is clearly not her forte.</p><p> She can'tproject her voice, move across the stage or deliver a single line with the slightestconviction.</p><p> It's hard to understand how she got the part.</p><p> It's impossible to understandhow she kept it.</p><p> The sole pleasure in the show is Patrick Page, who burst into prominence for the public by playing the villain in the woebegone musical Spiderman: Turn Off The Dark.</p><p> Here he is wonderful as the politically savvy lawyer Rufus R.</p><p> Buckley.</p><p> Everyone who acts across fromhim is instantly better and more interesting.</p><p> His asides to the jury, his opening andclosing arguments, his play-acting when he provokes the father into a moment of angerare criminally fun.</p><p> Page is good enough to be remembered come Tony time, though bythen I fear the show will be long gone.</p><p> I appreciated some of the clever telescoping accomplished by Rupert Holmes, who has had one fo the more curious, entertaining careers around, ranging from "Escape (The PinaColada Song)" to the musical The Mystery Of Edwin Drood and the TV series Remember WENN.</p><p> A sentimental low-point in the novel is a reported scene in the jury room that sways the verdict.</p><p> In the film, it was put into the mouth of Jake, which was even morerisible.</p><p> Here, Holmes has cleverly worked it into the closing arguments in a way that proves our hero outsmarting Buckley.</p><p> Other similar tweaks were wise as well. (Though when I read the book and saw this play I had the same impulse: why not put the expert witness back on the stand to defend himself? It's ripe for drama and would turn the jury against Buckley if the story came from a tearful old man rather than Jake.) But the main mistake I think was not to commit to one vision.</p><p> The novel is epic in its way and certainly episodic.</p><p> It's also a young man's work filled with so much incident it could only be captured in a miniseries which would also do justice to the wide cast of characters (like the showdown between Jake, the family and the local preachers that is a high point of the book).</p><p> But on stage they try to have it both ways: sticking mainly to the courtroomand hinting at the massive public outcry roiling their small town.</p><p> Better to stick even morefirmly to the courtroom.</p><p> Better to have shown the judge firm in court and craven andfearful in chambers.</p><p> Better to have milked the drama there for all its worth rather thanstepping outside for brief climaxes like a man trying to blow up Jake's home or the laterscene where it's set on fire and is shown rather tackily in the shape of a burning cross incase we missed the point about racism.</p><p> For most theatergoers, they'll be better off reading the novel and tackling Sycamore Row, the sequel he's written after all these years.</p><p> If the Broadway production of A Time To Killdid nothing
