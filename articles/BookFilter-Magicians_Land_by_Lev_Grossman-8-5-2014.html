Thefirst book had teens cursing and doing drugs and having sex and all the things teens might do when they're not infamily friendly adventures.</p><p> It was an amusing conceit but for me didn't go farther.</p><p> But in his second book and now thefinale, Grossman has deepened the psychology and plumbed his idea of taking these tales seriously.</p><p> I must admitthat the others who raved were right (or at least righter) all along.</p><p> Think about it: what if you were a kid allowed toenter a Narnia-like world where you were royalty with magical powers...and then it was taken away? That doeshappen quite a bit in tales, especially when children hit adolescence.</p><p> But really, wouldn't that mess up your mindimmeasurably? That's the focus of this third and final book, where Quentin Coldwater is the kid who has been castout of the magical land of Fillory for no particular fault of his own.</p><p> The land needs saving so guess what? Quentinmight just be able to force his way back in again.</p><p> Even here, I feel the book spells out its intentions too baldlytowards the end.</p><p> But it's a very satisfying exploration of the characters Grossman created and how fantasy can reallyF you up, as they might say in more salty terms.</p><p> And do say!
