"I started as a boy soprano; that's how I got into acting," explains Norton, chatting in his dressing room at Finian's Rainbow before a recent performance. "I had quite a good voice as a kid.</p><p> The first radio play came because I won this competition.</p><p> They had a program on Irish radio called Children at theMicrophone.</p><p> So I sang my songs, I sang "Who Is Sylvia" or whatever. "I saw these people behind the glass watching me.</p><p> They asked if I acted.</p><p> I said sure, school plays and thesuch. 'Would you want to audition for 'The Foley Family?,' which was huge.</p><p> The guy who played [the teen on the show], his voice had broken.</p><p> Puberty struck.</p><p> That opened that door [for me] and I never thought of doing anything else after that." Though he continued to study singing throughout his teens, from the age of ten on, Norton found a wonderful playground and the only schooling that would ever matter to him on the radio. "I was in Brendan Behan's first play, first two plays because they were done for radio," remembers Norton. "I was the kid among this wonderful group.</p><p> I met all these people and being a kid I wasn'toverawed.</p><p> But I was learning all the time.</p><p> It was all live radio.</p><p> We would do the Sunday play.</p><p> I would read the short story.</p><p> I would do the sports report.</p><p> I would introduce the symphony orchestra.</p><p> It was a wonderful training ground because in radio if you can't be seen you can play anything." Norton's father was proud of his son, but it was his mother who really reveled in the glitter and glamour of it all -- or at least whatever glamour she could manufacture out of it. "My father was a grocer," said Norton. "He opened one of the first delicatessens in Dublin.</p><p> He wasn't at all [artistic]...he had a great voice.</p><p> I've got his voice.</p><p> I see myself in the mirror now and though he's amuch bigger man than me, I see him.</p><p> My mother was very theatrically inclined.</p><p> Did she act or does Norton mean in life?"Oh, she was a bit of a drama queen," he smiles. "She played violin very well.</p><p> She was very...artistic, I think is the word.</p><p> We were sent to drama classes and elocution." Her pride was a very public sort of pride. "It was a bit embarrassing.</p><p> She took me into a big store in Dublin around Christmas time to buy me a suit.</p><p> She said to the man, 'Do you know who this boy is?' I blushed from my toes to the top of my head.</p><p> I said, 'Please don't do that.' No, she was very proud.</p><p> As indeed was my dad.</p><p> Interestingly, he never came to see me in anything.</p><p> He had the store to run and he took that very seriously.</p><p> But whenever I tried tofind out why, he'd say, 'Well you know something might go wrong.' I'd say, 'But, it's live theater.</p><p> Things go wrong all the time.'" "Um, I don't think I really knew myself.</p><p> I just felt more comfortable being other people.</p><p> It's only in more recent years when you're called upon to play who you are in age that I found that I can do that.</p><p> But up to that point I was always hiding, hiding behind the disguise.</p><p> Hiding from what?"I don't know," he said. "That was really bad.</p><p> And it was tough being a kid who was in the public eye being taught - for want of a better word - by that organization, which was full of very strange, bitter, vicious, sadistic people.</p><p> So I had a tough time there, got beaten a lot.</p><p> I guess I retreated into being other people.</p><p> I retreated into being theschool clown just to avoid that confrontation. "When I finally left school, they said what are you going to do? I said, 'I'm going to be an actor.</p><p> I am an actor.' They said that thing of who do you think you are? Someone from South Circle Road in Dublin and you're going to be an actor? I wasn't encouraged, shall we say.</p><p> It wasn't respected." But with his family's support -- at this point, Norton's sister was running a drama school in Dublin that was quite notable, only adding to his mother's delight -- Norton continued to work steadily in radio, TV and on the stage. "I guess in terms of the big break, Peter O'Toole did Juno & The Paycock in Dublin in the 60s," says Norton. "I played Johnny Boyle, which is a lovely part and that really got me noticed.</p><p> Then an agent from London came.</p><p> I did a play called Zoo Story the next year.</p><p> They said, 'We'd like to represent you.' So like most Irish actors do, I got on the boat and went to London seeking fame and fortune. "I was very lucky that the first job I was up for was at the Royal Court.</p><p> Lindsay Anderson was directing a play called The Contractor.</p><p> David Story - wonderful play where they build a tent on stage every night.</p><p> That ran for a year.</p><p> Then I did The Changing Room after that and then I was asked to join the National Theatre.</p><p> Suddenly, it was all happening.</p><p> I've been fortunate although I think luck is the residue of design.You have to be ready.</p><p> When Norton mentions shows like Playboy of the Western World, I wondered if he was becoming the go-to guy for Irish roles. "There was a danger of that so then for a while I didn't play Irish parts," says Norton. "I played Laertes to Albert Finney's Hamlet.</p><p> I did Emigres with Brian Cox and I did St.</p><p> Joan and I did a whole pile of things.</p><p> I wanted to be an actor rather than just an Irish actor.</p><p> It was only really when The Weir happened that people said, 'Gosh, he's Irish.' I'd been playing English and Germans and Americans and all kinds ofstrange psychopaths." The Weir raised Norton's profile in the US dramatically, but he'd had an unusually steady career on both sides of the Atlantic.</p><p> Thanks to his talented wife Mary's work that got her a contract with Disney, Norton was based in Los Angeles for a while and suddenly started working on TV shows like LA Law for people who certainly didn't know his stage work and sometimes didn't even realize he was Irish.</p><p> And his turn as Bishop Brennan in the iconic Irish sitcom Father Ted made him recognizable throughout the UK and even Australia and New Zealand.</p><p> But The Weir turned Norton from an actor into a name.</p><p> He also embraced his Irishness without reservation once and for all."When I did Laertes, [director] Peter Hall came up to me one day and said, 'You're really good in Norton also famously never touched drink until he was 27 years old. (His mother was a strict tee-totaler and drink was never in their home and as far as Norton knew, his father never drank either.) Now he enjoys wine but still doesn't drink spirits.</p><p> But just maintaining the physical state of a wrecked old drunk was demanding, not to mention a tumble down the stairs during a crucial scene. "Some nights I could hardly walk home," admits Norton. "My legs hurt, my back hurt because you know coming down that stair every night, sometimes you land badly.</p><p> My hip began to hurt.</p><p> In the winter it was terrible.</p><p> But it's what we do.</p><p> It's what we love to do.</p><p> It was a huge challenge to keep it going to the very end." And when it was over, what could be next? "What part could I possible play?" said Norton.</p><p> Naturally, another Conor McPherson play.</p><p> He ended The Seafarer, took a week off and then dove right into the limited run of a revival of Port Authority with Brian D'Arcy James and John Gallagher Jr. of Spring Awakening.</p><p> And now Finian's Rainbow.</p><p> So after a lifetime of avoiding typecasting, Norton has gone from a bitter Irish drunk in one drama to the twinkling Irish rogue who loves a spot of drink, spouts blarney and tussles with a leprechaun over a pot of gold. "I said, 'I'd love to do it but I do want to make him real.</p><p> I want to make him organic.</p><p> I don't want him to be a diddly idle Irish man or a Disney character.'
