Will & Grace: The Complete Series Collection ($249.98; Lionsgate) is a great chance to see this acclaimed sitcom in a new light.</p><p> Theelaborate boxed set includes all eight seasons and those who've been buying the individual seasons all along can stick with Season Eight to round out their set.</p><p> The show began with NBC bracing for controversy that simply never came.</p><p> The show was too well written, too conventional in the best sense of the word, too...familiar to raise a ruckus.</p><p> Will was a single guy in NYC with his best friend Grace, his wacky neighbor Jack and Grace's assistant who swilled martinis when she wasn't snapping off one liners.</p><p> And it wasn't a show where Will just happened to be gay -- his gayness and the show's queer attitude was present at everymoment, in every scene, in every way.</p><p> But people complained that Will was neutered and Jack wasa gay minstrel show.</p><p> Apparently, they never saw Kramer on Seinfeld or the million other sitcomswith over the top secondary characters.</p><p> Will never got married (until he officially settled downwith his boyfriend on the final episode) and neither did Mary Tyler Moore.</p><p> Heck, even Sex and theCity waited until the last episode to give Carrie a happy-ever-after.</p><p> So Will didn't get married because that's not how sitcoms usually work.</p><p> They're variations on a theme, not dramas whereupheaval and change are essential for growth.</p><p> Will COULD have been married (though Grace'smarriage was pretty disastrous comically speaking) and people WANTED Will to get married andthat was all the success they ever wanted.</p><p> Is "Gigi" the greatest movie musical? I'm talking here about musicals created directly for the big screen.</p><p> Most of the great movie musicals began on Broadway or even in dance.</p><p> But Gigi ($20.98; Warner Bros.) was created for the movies by Lerner & Lowe when they were at the heightof their powers, just as Rodgers & Hammerstein may have created the greatest musical written forTV when they crafted Cinderella around Julie Andrews.</p><p> Me, I've always been lukewarm about this one.</p><p> I think it has something to do with prostitution.</p><p> I'm far from prudish, but somehow I get Be the First to Submit This Story to Digg Get Breaking News Alerts Share Print Comments never spam Popular Stories on HuffPost Fashion Face-Off! Fox has released the final boxed set of Charlie Chan movies ($49.98; Fox), including the seven final Sidney Toler movies, including a few pretty good ones.</p><p> First the good news: the five boxed sets include 25 movies in total, most of them starring the definitive Chan, Warner Oland and the not as definitive but still good Chan, Sidney Toler.</p><p> The prints are very good indeed, especially for B movies and often include extras.</p><p> These movies were kept out of circulation for years because a Swede playing an Asian was hard to swallow and Chan's aphorisms and slightly formal English seemed quaint and condescending to people who never considered that Chan's English is much better than their Mandarin or Cantonese.</p><p> Watch the movies -- like the final box's Charlie Chan at the Wax Museum -- and the charge of racism (beyond who actually played Chan) is patently absurd.</p><p> Chan is always the smartest person in the room and is widely admired by police forces and public officials all over the world.</p><p> He is easily the most positive portrayal in Hollywood that Asians would receive for many, many years.</p><p> His children -- including Number One son -- are invariably shown as happily assimilated, All American kids.</p><p> It's just a shame these movies have been packaged in such bulky boxed sets.</p><p> The 12 Oland movies should have been sold all together in one neat package with two movies to a disc and the 11 Toler flicks in another set.</p><p> They should take up less than half of the shelf space and cost half as much.</p><p> But what's here is great to have.</p><p> Should you watch Max Ophuls for the first time on DVD?
