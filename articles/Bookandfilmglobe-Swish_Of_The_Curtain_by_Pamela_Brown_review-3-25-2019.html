Saturday, April 6, 2019  FICTION Young Adult Swish Swish We’ve Got an Abandoned Meeting Hall, Let’s Put on a Show!  March 25, 2019 Michael Giltz Long ago, the British perfected a certain type of children’s book.</p><p> Think of the 1930’s Swallows and Amazons by Arthur Ransome.</p><p> In that delightful classic, the children of two families meet during vacation and have modest adventures of the sort children have.</p><p> The stakes are typically quite low and thus–to the children involved–immeasurably high.</p><p> Invariably the kids display diverse and vivid personalities, clash a little, learn a little, andhave a jolly good time.</p><p> When done decently, a book in this genre can be as satisfying as a cozy mystery to Agatha Christie fans.</p><p> When done very well, they can be corkers.</p><p> Pamela Brown did it quite well indeed with The Swish Of The Curtain,first published in 1941 (when she was just 16!).</p><p> The Swish Of The Curtain involves best friends from two families who befriend the kids in a third family just arrived at their small town.</p><p> Bored and restless, they stumble across an abandoned meeting hall perfect forputting on theatricals.</p><p> In the blink of an eye, the industrious children clean it up, dub themselves the Blue DoorTheatrical Company, create their own evening of entertainment from scratch (including original songs, somedancing, skits, a melodrama and so on) and mount it to roaring approval.Roll credits? Not quite.</p><p> In the many pale imitations of strong books like this, the evening of the big show would be the finale.</p><p> But this triumph occurs only a third of the way into the book.</p><p> Author Brown then depicts how outof sorts the kids are in the days and weeks after their great success.</p><p> Reading about the high of the big gamefeels familiar.</p><p> But reading about the low that can follow feels like a refreshing dose of life as it’s really lived.</p><p> That honest practicality gives this book and its four sequels a unique appeal.</p><p> The Swish Of The Curtain details the hard work involved in actually doing what they do, even in a small way in a small town.</p><p> Our troupeperforms other pieces, so they might refashion a cape from one skit into a skirt for a show months later.</p><p> Brownpresents this idea of recycling costumes and props (a mainstay of theatrical companies) without fuss, but it’smerely one element in the novel’s realism.</p><p> The kids fight and bicker, with an unspoken attraction between two of them simmering in the background.</p><p> They never act on or even speak of it, which is almost shocking.</p><p> More radically, the kids become obsessed withgreasepaint and the spotlight.</p><p> Some coolly calculate which courses at school they can backslide.</p><p> Another← This is ‘Us’ ‘Brooklyn Nine-Nine’: a Cop Show for Kids a musical conservatory.</p><p> Two years pass, with the kids performing at everything from outdoor fetes to fundraisers to entertaining little kids with a Christmas pageant.</p><p> All the while, their families wait for this silly fad to pass or start putting theirfeet down. “Now enough of this nonsense!” they say.</p><p> The Swish Of The Curtain doesn’t suggest following your heart is easy or delightful or destined to pay off, not by any means.</p><p> But it does make clear in a simple, straightforward manner that nothing should stop you fromtrying.</p><p> Substitute any passion for that of the theater and you can imagine how appealing this book is to kids…and how frightening to sensible parents.</p><p> Pushkin Press is reprinting all five books in the series this year.</p><p> If you
