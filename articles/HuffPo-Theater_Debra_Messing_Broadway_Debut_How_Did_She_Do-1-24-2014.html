(O'Byrne) and his father Tony (the wonderful PeterMaloney) come home to their farm after the funeral of their next-door neighbor.</p><p> Anthony is straightening upbecause he's invited the widow (Dearbhla Molloy, pitch perfect) and daughter Rosemary (Messing) over for acup.</p><p> It's the first of six deceptively simple scenes taking place over a few years.Anthony is astonished to hear his father doesn't plan to leave the farm to him, even though Anthony has bled forthe place and has nothing to show for it other than a broken heart from his teenage years and the bleak future ofa lonely bachelor existence.</p><p> He's fine with that, but to not get the farm? Even though he's always felt it to be amillstone around his neck? Unthinkable.</p><p> The two families spar with the ease of old acquaintances, both elderlyfolk readily admitting they're not long for this world and both young (okay, middle-aged) children insisting they'refine with their limited prospects.It's clear Tony should leave the farm to his son.</p><p> It's clear Anthony and Rosemary are...well, not made for eachother.</p><p> They're too ornery and set in their ways and just plain odd for all that.</p><p> But they're certainly not made foranyone else, not as far as they're both secretly concerned.</p><p> And it just makes sense, them two together.</p><p> Maybe.Two obstacles remain: one of them a disputed bit of land and the other a Secret of Anthony's.</p><p> Happily, OutsideMullingar keeps that Secret just a modest secret, so it doesn't build up anticipation for a truly offbeat revelationthat takes an actor of O'Byrne's immense skill to put over.As the two parental figures, Maloney and Molloy are very good.</p><p> Molloy especially might have been forgiven forpumping up her part by stealing more laughs than necessary.</p><p> But she is wisely content to let her presenceremain exactly as it should.</p><p> Indeed, the play is filled with humor, though only the final scene has the romanticloopiness of Moonstruck.</p><p> The first few scenes have more substance, more drama, than one might expect andTony's deathbed confession is quietly moving.Playing the two lonely souls so set in their ways they are in danger of remaining so forever, O'Byrne and12/7/2015Theater: Debra Messing's Broadway Debut -- How'd She Do?
