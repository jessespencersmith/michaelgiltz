Capote should enlighten them.</p><p> Unquestionably one of the best movies of the year, Capote details the signal triumph of Capote's long career-the reporting and writing of In Cold Blood, a remarkable best seller about a multiple homicide in Middle America and the men who committed it.</p><p> The book exploded concep­ tions of what was possible in nonfiction, and the openly gay Capote influenced writers and re­ porters for generations to come.</p><p> At the center ofthe film is Philip Seymour Hoffman as Capote.</p><p> Hoffman has wowed us repeat­ edly in movies like Boogie Nights and on Broadway in such productions as lllng Day's Journey Into Night so we expect brilliance .</p><p> We get it.</p><p> Capote could easily be portrayed as a vain, silly figure-­ played for laughs, as gay men, especially "sissies," so often are.</p><p> But Hoffman exhibits the keen intelligence and seductive empathy that helped this exotic creature win the trust of informants as disparate as high school girls, the head of the investigation, and, especially, killer Perry Smith (Clifton Collins Jr.), a miserable outcast with dreams of artistry.</p><p> By Hoffman's side is Catherine Keener as Capote's childhood friend, writer Harper Lee (To Kill a Mockingbi rd).</p><p> Ke.ener is having a career year, thanks to her warm and sexy turn in the sweet comedy The 40-Year-Old Virgin and now Hoffman's risk-taking grounded in reality.</p><p> It's hard to imagine Capote succeeding as well without Lee, and it's hard to imagine Hoffman succeeding as welt without Keener.</p><p> So much of the film works beautifully: the production design; the sharp black-and­ white photography; the subtle score; and the delicate dance between Capote and Smith, the killer Capote identifies with, wins over,
