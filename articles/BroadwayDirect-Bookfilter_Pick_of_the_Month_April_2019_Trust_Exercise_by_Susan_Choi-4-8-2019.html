Every month, BookFilter picks the best new theater book, exclusively for Broadway Direct readers.</p><p> Trust Exercise By Susan Choi$27, Henry Holt and Co.Published April 9 Novels are like a reverse trust exercise.</p><p> Instead of falling backward and believing your friends will catch you, a reader falls into a story, trusting the writer to play fair and not waste your time.An author may immediately reassure you with a familiar plotline or fool you into a false sense ofsecurity before taking a hard left turn.</p><p> You think you’ve been caught, only to realize the net isfrayed and there’s another story underneath the one you thought you knew.</p><p> Will that net hold, oris there another rude surprise waiting? That’s the effect of Pulitzer Prize nominee Susan Choi’s latest literary novel, Trust Exercise.</p><p> It’s set in the hothouse of a performing arts school, where students Sarah and David fall under the spell of each other and their magnetic teacher, Mr.</p><p> BOOKFILTER’S APRIL PICK OF THE MONTH MICHAEL GILTZ | APRIL 8, 2019 SHARE Kingsley.</p><p> It’s the Reagan era, and Sarah is the Molly Ringwald character, a poor-ish student with a white-knight boyfriend in David, who is almost embarrassingly wealthy.</p><p> That difference hardly matters when Mr.</p><p> Kingsley turns the lights off in the classroom and students crawl over andaround each other with uncontained sensual glee.</p><p> Sarah and David outpace the rest, not byhaving sex (no great marker in this world) but by the intensity of their unstableattraction/repulsion.</p><p> Mixed signals aren’t the half of it.</p><p> Each acting lesson — such as sittingknee-to-knee in front of the class while repeating lines to one another — is agonizing for them,but each one serves as delicious entertainment for their oohing and aahing classmates, andirresistible fodder for the quietly manipulative Mr.</p><p> Kingsley.</p><p> Despite the young-adult subjects,this isn’t the TV show Fame, where lessons will be gently learned.</p><p> It’s more intense anddamaging than that.</p><p> The story stays close to the raw, unforgiving, often-crying Sarah … untilhalfway through the novel, when the net frays and we fall into an entirely different understandingof what is going on.</p><p> You don’t need a new perspective to know that the trust of the students isbeing abused, not in some dramatic scandal (though that happens too), but in a simpler,perhaps crueler way.</p><p> Choi toys with our trust but it pays off in dividends.</p><p> Certainly fans oftheater and serious fiction will relish how frustrating and rewarding and life-changing a dramaclass can be for adolescents, even if the closest they get to the
