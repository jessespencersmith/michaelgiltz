The New York Times (and Everyone Else) Is Lying to You.</p><p> But Here Are A Few Titles We Liked Anyway.  December 12, 2018 Michael Giltz When the New York Times published its list of the Ten Best Books of 2018, I jumped on it like any avid reader would.</p><p> Which books made the list? How many had I read? This year, I’d read NONE of them.</p><p> But I didn’t feelshamed by that fact.</p><p> Why? Because I knew the New York Times was lying.</p><p> To me.</p><p> To you.</p><p> To themselves.</p><p> They can’t possibly know what the ten best books of 2018 might be because no one knows what the ten best books of 2018 truly are.</p><p> Heck, no one can honestly tell you the ten best mysteries or ten best sports books or even the ten best picture books of the year.</p><p> Why? Too many damn books.</p><p> In North America, literally hundreds of thousands of books are published in English every year.</p><p> Toss in self- published books and we may be talking about more than a million new books.</p><p> True, 17% of them are written byJames Patterson and his legion of co-authors.</p><p> Still, that’s a lot of books.</p><p> If they’re being honest, most critics will acknowledge they haven’t even scratched the surface.The world of TV criticism has been the most forthright lately, since the explosion in TV content has been so massive and so recent and so good.Again and again, TV critics will explain and apologize and justify andqualify any and every list they make.</p><p> Then they make a second list of thethree dozen shows they’ve going to watch next.</p><p> Music critics are less honest; they still kid themselves that they’ve heard everything that matters.</p><p> Now if you’re a film critic, you actually can see every single movie that gets a one week theatrical run in New York Cityeach year.</p><p> You just have to watch about three movies a day.</p><p> Broadway?Piece of cake! Toss in Off Broadway and you’re already out of luck.</p><p> Itcan’t be done.</p><p> So be honest, NYT–those ten books are a list of the best books of the year…out of all the books (some of) youactually read.</p><p> Still, the list is life and in this over-saturated world of content, a good list is like a good friend,someone who can direct your attention and save you time.</p><p> If I can direct your attention, one of my favorite books of the year that isn’t popping up on a lot of other lists is The Sea Queen by Linnea Hartsuyker.</p><p> It’s book two in a brutal, compelling Viking saga that features schemers as thrillingly strategic as ThomasCromwell in Wolf Hall or anyone in Game of Thrones, but with less accessto dragons.</p><p> Toss in some of the best female characters of the year (womenwho hold their own with the men have center stage here) and you get agreat work of historical fiction.</p><p> Book three in the trilogy can’t come soonenough.</p><p> But you’ll have to start with book one: The Half-Drowned King.</p><p> The National Book Awards sent me to the young adult novel The Poet X by Elizabeth Acevedo.</p><p> It ticks so many boxes that YA novels tend to tickthese days: person of color, non-privileged world, faith (questioning, butstill respectful), sexual confusion and mixed-race dating, which doesn’teven count anymore as a box to tick for young people.</p><p> As a bonus,Acevedo wrote the novel in free verse, with each chapter its own stand-alone vivid piece of writing.</p><p> All of itcombines into a rich story that ends in a very happy moment, not a given by any stretch in YA, but one that’swell-earned.</p><p> The New York Times sent me to the “urban Indian” novel of Tommy Orange, a rudely reductionist label for a rich work called There There.</p><p> Orange’s remarkable debut is smart enough to put the quote by Gertrude Stein (referred to in the title) into context while telling the painful, honest, angry, funny, wrenching and toss-in-your-own-adjective story of people who converge at a powwow in California.</p><p> It’s multi-layered, unsparing andhumane.</p><p> I’m very glad I read it.</p><p> That’s three more books down
