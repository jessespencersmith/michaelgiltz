At his new home, Giorgio meets a boisterous friendly group of soldiers making the most of their isolation, a quiet existence punctuated by the painful cries of the cousin of hiscommanding officer Colonel Ricci (Stephen Bogardus).</p><p> That cousin is Fosca (Judy Kuhn),a terminally or perhaps perennially ill patient of Dr.</p><p> Tambourri (Tom Nelis).</p><p> Fosca quitenaturally is drawn to this strikingly good looking and yet sensitive man (he reads!) andher polite interest soon turns to passion, a passion encouraged by the well-meaning doctorwho places Giorgio in her path at key moments.</p><p> Fosca is grey-ish in pallor but strong in a way, tempered by her life of suffering.</p><p> Her desire, no her need for Giorgio is embarrassing, clinging, demanding, unyielding, unceasing and he is driven almost mad by her servile manner.</p><p> She would surely sit at his feet like a dog if it would please Giorgio because she loves him and only wants to makehim happy.</p><p> Her love is so complete that Fosca never expects him to love her in return.Her? How could he, she wonders.</p><p> It is enough to love him Fosca dominates Giorgio's thoughts and even the letters to his lover Clara, who is increasingly distraught about the hold this creature has on his affections.</p><p> Giorgio tries toget away; Fosca follows.</p><p> He is repulsed by her humiliating abasement but he can't stopthinking about her until finally, somehow, his intense involvement moves ever so slowlyfrom annoyance to... admiration.</p><p> And then, somehow, impossibly, to love.May 6, 2014 This is the print preview: Back to normal view Â» Posted: 03/01/2013 3:55 pmThis is a chamber opera, with all the heightened emotions and quiet intensity that implies.</p><p> The book by James Lapine is air-tight but complex and the melodies and lyrics bySondheim are as slippery and dangerous as mercury. "Loving you is not a choice/ It's whoI am" is perhaps the most quoted line.</p><p> The first time I saw the show, that line was aplaintive plea by Donna Murphy in a coruscating performance that dominated everyonearound her on opening night.</p><p> Fosca was a force of nature, tragic and dark but pitiablywonderful as well.</p><p> Everyone stood in her shadow.</p><p> When I was lucky enough to see the show again shortly before it closed, the cast had gelled remarkably.</p><p> Murphy was as memorable as ever, but the other performers hadfound their characters and the show had a better balance.</p><p> It wasn't just Fosca and Giorgiowith the distracting bauble of Clara off to the side.</p><p> That second time the Doctor loomedlarger and Clara wasn't so easily dismissed.</p><p> But it was still a tragedy.</p><p> Now with this new production, Passion seems to me more beautiful than sad.</p><p> Errico humanizes the sometimes flippant Clara; her desire to stay in her marriage until her son is grown seems quite reasonable.</p><p> Can't Giorgio wait? But Giorgio has now seen a lovewhere no obstacles are allowed, where no convention or societal demands could stand achance.</p><p> Suddenly this isn't the tragic story of a sickly woman clinging to a man and rousing his pity/love.</p><p> It's the story of a woman pure of desire who intentionally but not cruelly wins the affections of another for the first time in her life (even her cousinconsiders her utterly undesirable).</p><p> And in doing so, she reveals to Giorgio that he iscapable of being loved completely (just like her) and that allows him to love purely, gently,passionately even though doing so may cost the physically fragile Fosca her life.</p><p> It's not adark tale, it's a romance.</p><p> Of course, it's also wish fulfillment of a sort: what lonely person longing for beauty wouldn't love to believe that they could win the prince simply with the unrelenting forceof their desire? That perhaps is why a subtle mist descends on the stage at the magicalmoment when Fosca and Giorgio discover mutual love.</p><p> It's just one small touch in abreath-taking production.</p><p> The costumes by Ann Hould-Ward are not costumes, butperfectly apt clothing, from the beautiful but not vain clothes of Clara to the simple, austere material of Fosca.</p><p> Kudos also to the makeup design of Angelina Avallone which marks Fosca as the walking dead without romanticizing or overdoing it.</p><p> The lighting byJane Cox and sound design by Dan Moses Schreier mesh seamlessly to set the tone andsetting for everything from a ruined castle to the dining hall of soldiers, from a rattlingtrain to a private boudoir.</p><p> The musicians located on a balcony above the audience are led beautifully by Rob Berman, performing the orchestrations by Jonathan Tunick.</p><p> Doyle's direction, needless to say, iselegant and precise.</p><p> And the cast is in strong voice throughout.</p><p> Bogardus makes thecousin Colonel Ricci come alive.</p><p> And Nelis finds just the right note for the Doctor.</p><p> Theyare not afterthoughts in a love triangle but vital players.</p><p> A flashback "explaining" Fosca'snature by revealing an early betrayal by a cad seems unnecessary here, though it's quite well played.</p><p> Surely being homely and an invalid is all the explanation we desire for her neediness? Errico, as I've said, makes Clara a worthy attention of her lover.</p><p> Silverman I feared might not be worthy of her.</p><p> He's handsome but it seemed at first a rather mannequin-likehandsomeness.</p><p> But Silverman reveals depths of Giorgio.</p><p> He's almost afraid of intimacywith anyone, not just Fosca, though her almost mythic desire would scare anyone at first.Mostly it's embarrassment and a fear of being known that drives Giorgio.</p><p> At one or two moments, his revulsion at Fosca was perhaps not quite right, brief flashes that felt overdone in this intimate space.</p><p> I wouldn't even call them wrong notes since they flitted byso quickly.</p><p> I was more convinced that this Giorgio might in fact not think himself asterribly lovable or interesting either, a vulnerability that was winning.</p><p> Overall, Silvermanshines in the part, showing his equal role in the seduction that Fosca enacts.</p><p> It's the roleof a lifetime for this actor and he makes the most of it.</p><p> Indeed, the marvelous Judy Kuhn as Fosca is not some spider laying a web for Giorgio.</p><p> She is knowing and implacable, but genuine.</p><p> The humor that courses through the show isvery welcome.</p><p> When Giorgio admits his lover is married, Kuhn garners knowing laughswith a simple but loaded, "Oh" in response.</p><p> It's a pleasure to see her wear down Giorgiowhen he comes to her bedchamber.</p><p> Please, sit next to me.</p><p> Please, rest your legs on thebed.</p><p> Please, write me a letter of devotion.</p><p> Please, do you have sisters? Do you kiss themgoodnight? It's a dance she is initiating but a dance takes a partner and Giorgio knows aswell as Fosca what is happening.</p><p> That makes their final coming together all the more beautiful.</p><p> He hasn't been trapped or worn down; he has been lifted up to experience real love for the first time.</p><p> Thecombination of book and song is so seamless, I wouldn't be surprised if you told me theentire show was sung through or if the music was only in my head.</p><p> Repeatedly, Classic Stage Company delivers shows that deserve to run much longer and transfer to bigger houses.</p><p> I'm still devastated I didn't get a second chance to see TheSchool For Lies and Three Sisters and other recent triumphs.</p><p> It's impractical but I could only wish this production would stay right where it is in this ideal location.</p><p> Perhaps CircleIn The Square might be the small, unusual Broadway house that could serve this Passionwell.</p><p> But when a jewel finds the perfect setting, you know it.</p><p> CAROUSEL AT LINCOLN CENTER *** out of **** NEW YORK PHILHARMONIC AT AVERY FISHER HALL It's been almost 20 years, but the 1994 revival of Carousel at Lincoln Center still looms large in the imagination.</p><p> It was revelatory enough to convince Time magazine to name Carousel the greatest musical of the 20th century.</p><p> Maybe 200 yards from that landmark production, the New York Philharmonic is celebrating the show with a charming concertperformance of Carousel that proves its enduring worth.</p><p> Sometimes, concert performances prove an excuse for stunt casting that would never fly ina full-blown production.</p><p> But here the Philharmonic and director John Rando (with BinderCasting) have done exactly what you should do: use the opportunity of a concertperformance to cast ideal people who otherwise would never be available.</p><p> So we get twostars from the world of opera, two principal dancers from the New York City Ballet, majortalent like John Cullum and Kate Burton and Shuler Hensley in smaller roles and best of all the Philharmonic at full force.</p><p> No Broadway house could dream of an orchestra so big and talented; the music they create led by conductor and music director Rob Fisher is a joy.</p><p> Though Carousel famously did not begin with an overture, that doesn't stop them here from delivering the goods with"Carousel Waltz." For the music alone, this night was a pleasure.</p><p> Kelli O"Hara was on loan from Nice Work If You Can Get It and it will surprise no one that she was excellent as Julie Jordan.</p><p> Her voice opened up to operatic heights when paired with Nathan Gunn as Billy Bigelow and he -- unlike most opera stars of the past --found the right voice for delivering the show tunes Carousel is filled with.</p><p> Their scene at night as they fell in love while insisting they weren't in "If I Loved You" is of course one of musical theater's most disarmingly complex moments and they were ideal.</p><p> While O'Hara captured the strong Jordan with ease, it has to be said that Gunn didn't remotely begin to plumb the dark soul of Billy Bigelow.</p><p> That wasn't a problem when falling in love or voicing the concerns of a father. (His "Soliloquy" was a highlight of thenight and Gunn's best moment.) But the sense of danger and perhaps menace that should envelope Billy was never there.</p><p> This wasn't a fatal problem: Billy as played by Gunn is anessentially nice lug who doesn't realize he has other options.</p><p> You can't imagine him actually beating Julie; at most, he seems to be pushing her away.</p><p> That makes the brief scene in which Julie explains why she stayed with a man who beat her also a tad morepalatable.</p><p> Overall, Gunn was good, with the missing dark colors balanced by his immenseappeal.</p><p> Not good casting for a full revival but he worked in this context.</p><p> In contrast, mezzo-soprano Stephanie Blythe was marvelous as Nettie, the force of nature at the center of this New England town where an orphaned girl like Julie could fall for acarnival barker like Billy and see her life tinged with tragedy for doing so.</p><p> Blythe beltedout "June Is Bustin' Out All Over" with aplomb, joined by the rousing ensemble that wascast well down to the smallest part.</p><p> She was equally at home with the humor of "A RealNice Clambake" and of course the stirring "You'll Never Walk Alone." I still think of Carrie as the Audra McDonald part and always will but Jessie Mueller proved anew why it's such a scene-stealing role. (It helps to be paired with JasonDanieley's witty Mr.</p><p> Snow.) The other big moment is the ballet in act two that sobrilliantly shows history repeating itself with the romance of Julie and Billy's daughterwith another carny.</p><p> Tiler Peck was breathtaking as Louise, flying across the stage in suchrapture you wished briefly that the entire orchestra could be whisked away for a momentso she'd have more room to soar.</p><p> Robert Fairchild was unfortunately not the right choicefor chemistry.</p><p> His costume including a leather vest was ill-fitting and more dancer-ishthan sexy (the only off note in the otherwise excellent work of David C.</p><p> Woolard).Fairchild seemed a little too stiff, a little too formal and that kept the sparks from flyingbetween these two.</p><p> He was more convincing at shrugging her off at the end.</p><p> Don't get mewrong: this was merely the difference between a duet being good and being great butthere's no question he was outshone here.</p><p> Of the Big Five of Rodgers & Hammerstein -- Oklahoma (probably the proper choice for the most influential musical of the 20th century), Carousel, South Pacific, The King & Iand The Sound Of Music - Carousel had the shortest initial run.</p><p> It's hard to imagine given the show's riches, not to mention a winning high school graduation scene and one of themore moving moments in musical theater: all Billy needs to do is wish more for hisdaughter, wish her to be free of his anger and sad past and live her life and be happy for this troubled man to be redeemed in the eyes of heaven.</p><p> Gunn is open-hearted here as well to great effect.</p><p> And the reprise of "You'll Never Walk Alone" is staged with wonderfulsimplicity by having the cast members slowly rise up out of their seats as the chorusbuilds.</p><p> It's a touching finale to a delightful evening rich in musical pleasures.</p><p> THE REVISIONIST ** out of ****CHERRY LANE THEATRE I'll do my best in writing this review.</p><p> But no matter what level of quality I reach -- okay, pretty good or good (for me) -- you can be certain that it would sound much, much betterif read aloud by actress Vanessa Redgrave.</p><p> That's surely how Jesse Eisenberg (and indeedany playwright) would feel when she agreed to star in his new play The Revisionist.Hurrah! My play just got better! It's curious that the talented Eisenberg would write a part for himself so similar to the screen persona he's had for years but that's exactly what he's done, albeit with lesssuccess.</p><p> Eisenberg plays David, a young author who is struggling to revise his secondnovel, the follow-up to a young adult novel that got a fair amount of attention...and a badreview from the New York Times.</p><p> Now in desperation to overcome distractions andperhaps doubting himself, David has invited himself to spend a week with a very distantrelative living in a port city in Poland whom he's never met.</p><p> Maria (Redgrave) is thrilled; she leads a lonely existence with no close relations and her entire emotional life seems to revolve around the American branch of her family, peopleshe's never met except for one happy day when she had coffee with one of them who waspassing through.</p><p> Having David spend an entire week with her is clearly a signal event.</p><p> Unfortunately, David is tiresomely self-absorbed and can't be bothered to offer up even the basic social niceties.</p><p> You'd have to be pretty dim (and David is clearly intelligent) to think it's ok to show up on someone's doorstep and immediately turn your nose up attheir food and insist on being left alone without even the formality of visiting and gettingto know -- even for a few minutes -- this person who has agreed to open their home toyou.</p><p> I've no problem with David being a jerk or just indifferent.</p><p> But he quickly becomesboringly rude and that's unforgivable.</p><p> I didn't catch Eisenberg's first play so I can't make any comparisons as far as growth.</p><p> He has created two distinctive characters and scene to scene there is fun to be had inwatching David try and grab a smoke while Maria ploddingly insists on his attention now and then.</p><p> A late night drink fest that turns into a dark confession about her experiences during the war offers some brief drama.</p><p> But essentially Eisenberg has developed two characters without finding a story for them to tell, revelations or not revelations.</p><p> Redgrave is especially strong at the start.</p><p> But as themotivations for Maria become dimmer and dimmer, she struggles a bit to make sense ofthis opaque person.</p><p> Eisenberg's screen charm translates easily to the stage but his David iseven more inert than Maria.</p><p> Daniel Oreskes is solid as a taxi cab driver (and Maria's lover)but he's not enough of a catalyst to drive the show.</p><p> We learn more about Maria and sense David's visit will be enshrined without irony among all her other mementoes of Maria's American relations.</p><p> But information does not equal story.</p><p> Neither character changes and I think we know just about everything we need in the first five minutes.</p><p> Without the exceptional skill of Regrave (who is a pleasure towatch), it's doubtful the show would have even the modest hold that it does on ourattention.</p><p> One could joke and say The Revisionist needs more revisions.</p><p> But Eisenberg has attempted a serious play and deserves more than flippancy.</p><p> I look forward to his next
