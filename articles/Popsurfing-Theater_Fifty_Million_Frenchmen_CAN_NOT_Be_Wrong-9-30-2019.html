MONDAY, SEPTEMBER 30, 2019 THEATER: "Fifty Million Frenchmen" CAN'T Be Wrong FIFTY MILLION FRENCHMEN: MUSICALS IN MUFTI ** 1/2 out of **** YORK THEATRE COMPANY Cole Porter's breakthrough musical Fifty Million Frenchmen is the sort of nonsense Broadway audiences won't tolerate anymore.</p><p> The plotis frivolous (rich Boy makes a bet he can woo the Girl while pretending to be poor), the characters are cardboard and the songs are only loosely connected to the story.</p><p> Sure, once in a blue moon Broadwaywill smile and accept a throwback like Crazy For You.</p><p> But most of the time, even when it revives an old fashioned charmer like Oklahoma!,it prefers to dirty them up a bit.</p><p> So our only chance to catch a "lost" Broadway landmark like Fifty Million Frenchmen are ventures like Encores! and the York Theatre Company's Musicals In Mufti series. (Those are musicals done in"street clothes," i.e. casually and quickly so artists can jump in and audiences can get a sense of the show.) In this case, director Hans Friedrichs and his game cast had all of seven days to do a read-through, learn their lines, learn the songs, learn some dance movesand learn the staging and then before they've even had time for acoffee break they must get up onstage and perform it in front of a paying audience.</p><p> To be honest, they got a head start on this one.</p><p> York's Executive Director Evans Haile (who handles Music Direction, first piano and isa friend) restored Fifty Million Frenchmen in 1991 for the 100th anniversary celebration of Cole Porter's birth.</p><p> You can listen on Spotifyto the marvelous all-star recording he made that year, named by USA Today as the best theater album of 1991.</p><p> I agreed. Even casual fans will perk up at the Cole Porter standard that starts things off. "You Do Something To Me" (tackled by leading man Andy Kelso) is not as flashy or lyrically playful as other Cole Porter gemsand it is not as tuneful as his best.</p><p> Yet its casual charm (echoed aminute later by the tongue-tied "You've Got That Thing") wins you over.</p><p> You're not supposed to start a show with your best song since late arrivals might miss it.</p><p> But that's exactly what Porter does here.</p><p> Ardent cabaret-goers might recognize the humorous story song "The Tale Of The Oyster," put over nicely here by Kristy Cates.</p><p> And yet the rest of the show is bursting with treats too.</p><p> As a would-be chanteuse, Ashley Blanchet steamrolls over the dated bits of the silly "Find Me APrimitive Man" and positively lights up "I'm Unlucky At Gambling."The only shocker here is that she must capture her man, rather thanhaving every man in the show chasing after her.</p><p> And Karen Murphy nails the social climbing frustrations of her character Gladys with "The Queen Of Terra Haute." In a show with little on its mind, Murphyconjures a moment of poignancy and humor almost out of thin air.</p><p> Indeed, the women beat out the men from start to finish.</p><p> Men are all well and good for paying the bills and wrapping around a finger, but the women were in charge at the York.</p><p> They all certainly had goodsongs to work with.</p><p> In truth, you won't find a weak number here --even the scene-changing filler was sharp, funny and pretty darnwinning.</p><p> Porter on the rise is a lot more fun than most other composers at their peak.</p><p> Unsurprisingly, the casual nature of the evening calls for understanding from the audience when it comes to this or thatFrenchmen" CAN'T Be Wrong The Movies, Books, Theater, Concerts, CDs I've See...</p><p> SHOWBIZ SANDBOX #463: Why Is A TV Special Celebrat...</p><p> SHOWBIZ SANDBOX #462: Fall Film Festival Roundup MOVIES: The Scarily Good Combination Of Hitchcock ...</p><p> MOVIES: A "Close Encounter" With A Steven Spielber...</p><p> THEATER: "Betrayal" And The (Permanent?)
