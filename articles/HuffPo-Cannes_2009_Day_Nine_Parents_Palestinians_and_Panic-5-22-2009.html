(His dad lives in the same town but is utterly indifferent to the boy, called Hubert.) Hubert hates how his mom eats, hates how she puts onmakeup while driving, hates what station she turns to on the radio, hates her clothes and hateseverything else about her and lets her know this in a stream of invective and criticism that is helplessly funny for the first half hour and then calls our sympathy for him into the question as the film goes on.</p><p> Interspersed with their wrangling are black and white excerpts from his video diary.These are quite different in tone from the initially brutal comic nature of the film and either reflect a teenager's hyper-self-importance or just indicate the more serious problems at the heart of the film.</p><p> Hubert really is horrible at times and his mom isn't so awful but you can't help laughing.</p><p> It'slike hearing all the meanest fleeting thoughts you might have felt about your parents voiced withlacerating precision.</p><p> In the car, he tries to change the station and she complains that she wants to listen to the news, so he angrily puts on headphones and she responds by asking why they can't talk -- she's not his chauffeur.</p><p> So he retorts, "I was letting you savor your news program," getting alaugh out of his use of the word "savor" and her repetition of it.</p><p> Their battles are endless.</p><p> Is she horrible? Funny, caustic and with charm to spare, Dolan will be fascinating to watch in the next few years.This isn't a "promising" debut; he's already delivering.</p><p> Some visual signatures are evident here.</p><p> When two people are together, he often sits them side by side.</p><p> A close-up of the person on the left, will show them on the far right of the screen.</p><p> A close-up of the person on the right frames them onthe far left of the screen.</p><p> It's the sort of internal consistency that shows an artist at work.</p><p> He has ashaky camera shot on a bus of our hero that could have been intentional all along -- or maybe hecouldn't get the shot he wanted due to a lack of equipment and realized it captured Hubert's jangled state of mind.</p><p> Dolan is so in control, you can believe happy accidents were part of his master plan.</p><p> He dresses his mom in her tackiest sweater when she's going to be at her best inprotecting her boy.</p><p> When he lashes out and says what would she do if he died today, Dolan waits until he's out of frame and lets her say softly "I'd die tomorrow" and it doesn't feel sentimental or false; it feels completely earned and moving.</p><p> As does the rest of this strong film.</p><p> THE TIME THAT REMAINS *** 1/2 -- Elia Suleiman is a stone-faced heir to Buster Keaton who makes absurdist comedies about life in the Palestinian territories and Israel.</p><p> His last film,Divine Interventions, was the first of his I saw and it was frustrating.</p><p> The first wonderful hour of the film was virtually a silent comedy a la Jacques Tati, with comic bits of business in a smallcommunity building and expanding and circling each other with increasing hilarity...until politics stepped in and ground the movie to a halt.</p><p> It could have been a classic without the heavy-handed message.</p><p> But Suleiman established a personal comic style that was wonderful -- quite often he'llplace the camera in a certain point (say a bench on a street or outside a window looking into aroom) and have people criss-cross back and forth in front of our view, telling mini-stories of frustration or annoyance or tenderness that delight in their simplicity.</p><p> Everything he promised is fulfilled in The Time That Remains.</p><p> It succeeds not by abandoning its message but by subsuming it effortlessly in the story as a whole, which charts his family history from 1948 to the present inelegant, moving fashion.</p><p> Showing his father as a young man, then middle-aged and finally old, with Suleiman himself growing from a little kid into the director of today, it's a deeply humane movie that encourages all sides in the conflict by managing to see the humanity of the Jews even as his sympathies are clear.</p><p> Yes, a tiny Israeli soldier who has to step on a rock just to tie a blindfold on a prisoner is funny.</p><p> But I can't help thinking it was a humanizing touch rather than a cruel one.Wonderful moments abound, like the guy who blithely takes out the garbage as the muzzle of agigantic Israeli tank on his block swings around to follow his every step.</p><p> When he gets a cell phone call, the man wanders back and forth with the tank's gun cranking back and forth again and again.</p><p> It makes the tank look silly and shows how life goes on under extreme circumstances and yet alsotakes the fear out of the Israelis: look how sad they look, scared of a man taking out his garbage or from another point of view look how the Israelis are making themselves foolish.</p><p> Two soldiers pull up to a late night dance club and say over their loudspeaker that a curfew is in effect.</p><p> They keeprepeating it, until their speech sounds like part of the pounding disco tune and soon the soldiersare bobbing their heads in time to the music while they say again and again that there's a curfew.</p><p> Surely this can only be seen as revealing the soldiers as just like the Palestinians.</p><p> And yet, in a lone fantasy sequence when Suleiman takes a pole vault and flies over the wall, there's no doubtingwhere he sees the problem lying.</p><p> But it's a rich, funny, touching film, far better than any message or political analysis might reveal.</p><p> I have no idea how audiences in the Middle east would react.</p><p> I'd like to thank Jews and Palestinians could watch it together and see
