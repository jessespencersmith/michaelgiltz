distinctive and funny dialogue, smart direction and an excellent cast can enliven any story.</p><p> Kirsten Greenidge's play begins with a bang as three high school girls strut onto the stage with all the attitude and self-possession of pop stars.</p><p> It's the birthday of Annie (Angela Lewis) and they're waiting outside a tattoo parlor, with Talisha (a very funny and focused Cherise Boothe) and Margie (a wittily dopey Nikiya Mathis) bantering over what sort of design Annie should get.</p><p> Margie is pregnant but rather than worrying about the stress and responsibility as a single mom, she's rhapsodizing over cool baby carriages, dope baby clothes and the like.</p><p> Talisha -- who insists everyone start calling her "T" because she's so cool she doesn't even need her full name -- decides to join Margie inmotherhood (it'll be fun!) and Annie is reluctantly dragged along into this baby mama pact.</p><p> The dialogue is so funny and precise -- with Margie consistently judging every potential male by the status of his cell phone -- that this sad spectacle is convincing, hilarious and sad all at once.</p><p> Milk Like Sugar is good, but FOLLOW US Going Back To Westrate and Brooksher front and center, it never feelslike work.</p><p> THE ATMOSPHERE OF MEMORY 1/2 * BANK STREET THEATERPlaywright David Bar Katz's new work is an exhausting story about a playwright who has written a family drama based on his own painful childhood.</p><p> He casts his actor girlfriend as his sister and then casts hismother (a retired actress played by Ellen Burstyn) as his mother and when his ne'er do well father (John Glover) steamrolls into town, yelling and arguing and hashing out of old memories ensues.</p><p> None of it is interesting.</p><p> The play is filled with so many inconsistencies and confusions that they're too numerous to mention.</p><p> To focus on one key moment, the playwright (Max Casella) spent his childhood tape recording every family conversation and transcribing them into notebooks.</p><p> They fill literally dozens of black school composition books with key moments from his parents endless bickering.</p><p> He takes one of those notebooks andconfronts his father with the moment when dad meanly told his son he was going to leave for good unless the kid could give him some reason to stay.</p><p> This is meant to force Glover to face up to his meanness and cruelty.</p><p> The only problem? Glover's dad relishes his meanness and cruelty.</p><p> He doesn't need to be forcedto admit it -- he loudly proclaims it every minute.</p><p> He insults and demeans everyone around him everyminute of every day and gleefully acknowledges it.</p><p> So what possible cathartic moment could the son be hoping for? His dad KNOWS he's a prick; it's in fact his defining characteristic.</p><p> He would be upset if you didn't think he was a prick.</p><p> So this hoped for powerful moment has zero impact since it doesn't revealanything the dad and we don't already know.</p><p> Glover leads with his crotch in this show (if you saw his performance, you'd know what I mean) and in some inexplicable way manages to create a genuine character.</p><p> It helps that he has the meanest, mostvivid bits of dialogue.</p><p> In the fuzzier role of the mother who is both less and more than she seems, Burstyn like everyone else struggles and fails to make sense of the show -- and the even worse show within a show that we catch glimpses of.</p><p> Melissa Ross somehow gins up some emotion as the sister,which is a small miracle.</p><p> But these are small passing moments of interest and not nearly enough to rescue the work.</p><p> All the tech elements are out of kilter because they are desperate to do something with a floundering work.</p><p> Adam Schlesinger of the marvelous Fountains Of Wayne devises desperate cues in his score that can't create drama where none exists.</p><p> The set feels
