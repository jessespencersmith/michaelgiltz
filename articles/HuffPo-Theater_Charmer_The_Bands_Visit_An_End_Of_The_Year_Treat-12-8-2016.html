HuffPost  EDITION US Michael Giltz , Contributor BookFilter creator Theater: Charmer "The Band's Visit" An End Of The Year Treat 12/08/2016 10:00 pm ET THE BAND’S VISIT *** 1/2 out of **** ATLANTIC THEATER COMPANY One of the best films of 2007 has become one of the best musicals of 2016.</p><p> You probably haven’t heard of the movie it’s based on: The Band’s Visit was an Israeli film that enjoyed a lot of critical acclaim ( including mine ) and worldwide success.</p><p> But because half of its dialogue was in English, it wasn’t accepted for Oscar consideration and lost the most useful platform in the US for bringing attention to movies from around the world.</p><p> Written and directed by Eran Colirin, it focuses on the modest but grandly named Alexandria Ceremonial Police Orchestra, which is traveling from Egypt to Israel to perform at the opening of a cultural center.</p><p> But a little booking mistake strands them in a tiny Israeli town in the middle of nowhere.</p><p> They’re forced to camp out in various homes overnight where tensions arise (it’s the Middle East, after all), friendships are formed and a little romance blossoms.</p><p> It’s a gentle and sweet story about the essential humanity of us all and who couldn’t enjoy hearing about that right about now? It’s also funny and just plain delightful, the sort of gem that brings a smile to your face.</p><p> Now it has been turned into a musical and the transition is so seamless and perfect you’d swear the film was a musical to begin with.</p><p> David Yazbek created the music and lyrics and it’s surely the best work from a man who recently did the hapless Women On The Verge Of A Nervous Breakdown, but previously worked on Dirty Rotten Scoundrels (a big hit) and The Full Monty (ditto, which also has in common with The Band’s Visit a generous attitude towards people).</p><p> The book is by Itamar Moses, who accomplished similar magic with the musical The Fortress Of Solitude .</p><p> And the director is David Cromer, the genius behind such terrific shows as Tribes , a brilliant Our Town, Adding Machine and many more.</p><p> It’s a triumph for all involved.</p><p> Indeed, this is one of those shows where everyone is so in tune with each other that the show simply unfolds in front of you.</p><p> You don’t think “What a great set!” (by Scott Pask, and who knew the Atlantic could accommodate a roundtable?) or “What perfect costumes that avoid mugging but are fun anyway?” (by Sarah Laux) or what great accents (Mouna R’miki was the language and dialect coach) or what great lighting and sound and orchestration (Tyler Micoleau and Clive Goodwin and Jamshied Sharifi).</p><p> You’re too busy enjoying Theater: Charmer "The Band's Visit" An End Of The Year Treatyourself.</p><p> It begins with a group of men in fancy, powder blue uniforms, ready to buy their tickets for an important trip.</p><p> Tony Shalhoub is Tewfiq, the officious leader who takes his role as conductor very seriously indeed.</p><p> An Egyptian cultural center that’s not even located in Jerusalem is probably not that big a deal in the scheme of things, but you know it’s a journey they’ve been anticipating for a long time.</p><p> Shalhoub sends the band’s Lothario (Ari’el Stachel as Haled) to buy the tickets.</p><p> But Haled is too busy flirting with the ticket seller and next thing you know they are lost in a poor excuse for a town with no hotel and no way out until the next day.</p><p> The no-nonsense but awfully attractive and self-confident owner of a cafe named Dina (Katrina Lenk) takes pity, finds them all lodgings and we’re off.</p><p> This may be an isolated backwater that even the locals put down with regularity, but it’s a notably lovelorn one.</p><p> John Cariani is Itzik, an unemployed schlub whose wife is tired of his endless adolescence. (He’s got the short pants to prove it, seeming more like a gangly kid than a new father.) Daniel David Stewart plays Papi, an awkward kid who freezes up whenever a girl gets near.</p><p> Dina is so appealing — and yet single — that you know there has to be some story there, too.</p><p> There’s even a guy (Erik Liberman) who simply stands by the one pay phone in town, night after night, waiting for his girl to call.</p><p> We don’t know why she left or why he still dreams she might ever reach out again, but we have the sad-sack feeling his vigil is pointless.</p><p> It’s that sort of town.</p><p> And music is flowing around all these people and all these stories.</p><p> The orchestra members naturally carry their instruments to whatever home they’re bedding down in, then people talk and the musicians begin to play.</p><p> Like many shows, The Band’s Visit uses a cast that in part can pull off playing those instruments with aplomb.</p><p> Other nominal members of the orchestra are always in the background somewhere, so singing out your troubles seems as natural as breathing.</p><p> The show casts an intimate spell akin to the musical Once .</p><p> Unlike that deserved Tony winner, The Band’s Visit doesn’t boast the intense and immediate rush of the stirring pop tunes featured in that movie turned musical.</p><p> The songs here are more shaggy, more human, though everything from “Welcome To Nowhere” to “Beat Of Your Heart” (about falling in love) and the hilarious “Papi Hears The Ocean” stand out musically.</p><p> Also unlike Once , the songs are not belted out by people who suddenly sound like major recording artists.</p><p> These are real songs sung by real people.</p><p> Even the actors who have better voices hold back and allow themselves to sound sweet and genuine, sometimes singing in awkward keys or given melodies that rise just a little out of their range when they’re most plaintive.</p><p> The result is a musical with a charm and vulnerability you never get in more typical shows when someone is belting out an anthem about feeling left out yet soaring to the heavens with a gorgeous voice.</p><p> It’s a truly original and refreshing approach on every level, the perfect marriage of sensibility with story.</p><p> It’s also a real ensemble, though Shalhoub and Lenk obviously hold our attention throughout.</p><p> Lenk gives a charismatic, knowing turn as Dina, a woman who is tough but not so hardened to life that she can’t see Tewfiq as a person worth reaching out to when she has the chance.</p><p> Shalhoub charms, of course, and the more his character — a widower — tries to hide in his shell, the more we yearn to see him break out.</p><p> I don’t know what his TV and movie commitments are, but he needs to clear his schedule for the next year, so The Band’s Visit can play right where it is or transfer to Broadway.</p><p> If it happens soon, Ben Platt of Dear Evan Hansen and Andy Karl of Groundhog Day just got some worthy competition.</p><p> But who cares about Tonys?
