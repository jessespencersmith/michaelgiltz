masterpiece, obviously.</p><p> It can withstand an unnecessary change in setting, director Ivo Van Hove's now familiar tics (spare set, anonymous costuming and near-constant scoring) and even an utterly bonkers decision to go full-on Exorcist and still remain a solid and compelling evening of theater.</p><p> If that's not playwriting magic, I don't know what is.</p><p> Don't worry.</p><p> If you're lucky enough to be in New York City and attend this production, you'll see a mostly excellent cast do justice by Miller's famed tale of the Salem Witch Trials as a pointed stand-in for thedemonization of McCarthyism.</p><p> The terrific Ben Whishaw is compelling and forthright without ever being self-righteous as John Proctor.</p><p> Proctor had an affair with the very determined and very young Abigail (Saoirse Ronan, as good onstage as she is on film).</p><p> Abigail is furious that John won't continue to dally with her and even angrier that his wife Elizabeth(Sophie Okonedo) has turned her out and bad-mouthed the girl to one and all.</p><p> Abigail's simmering resentment boils over when she and other girls are discovered by the priggish Rev.</p><p> Parris (a strong Jason Butler Harner).</p><p> He knows he saw the girls dancing (!), he thinks he saw one of the girls runnaked through the trees (!!) and once he's asked, why yes maybe there was some devilish potion (!!!) in thatbrew they were crafting with the help of the Caribbean servant Tituba (Jenny Jules).</p><p> The girls are clearly hiding dark deeds (even if today, we would see them as pointless if not harmless) but their protestations are to no avail.</p><p> The townsfolk are worried and since his own daughter is among the wicked, hecan't afford to show leniency. (Worse, Rev.</p><p> Parris is so tiresomely gloom and doom that his sermonizinghasn't yet won over folk and his employment by the town is in jeopardy; being pro-Satan would obviously bebad for business.) And so he makes the fateful choice to call in Rev.</p><p> Hale (Bill Camp), a man who haswondrous success in other colonies throughout America.</p><p> No one would accuse him of hypocrisy but when Theater: Ben Whishaw's 'Crucible' Boils Over, Steve Martin's 'Bright Star' Dims, Sweet 'She L Rev.</p><p> Hale comes looking for the works of the devil, well by God he finds them.</p><p> Miller's play is one for the ages.</p><p> How easily we see petty everyday grievances find their outlet in wild accusations.</p><p> A woman who has lost baby after baby points a finger at Rebecca Nurse (a wonderful BrendaWehle), who both midwifed those failed births AND had the temerity to produce a baker's dozen of children onher own.</p><p> A man who greedily gobbles up the land of his neighbors points the finger at someone who owns atract only he could afford.</p><p> And inevitably Abigail points the finger at John Proctor's wife.</p><p> Director Ivo Van Hove certainly should be credited for drawing such talent to his side for every project.</p><p> Almost every part is faultlessly played, from Ciarán Hinds as the unshakeable Deputy Governor Danforth to Camp asthe dangerously open to doubt Rev Hale right down to the minor role of Giles Corey.</p><p> That character - wholoves to sue one and all and thus has a good working grasp of the law - is played by the great Jim Norton.</p><p> It'snot a significant part but Norton is so memorable you'll be forgiven for thinking the play is about him.</p><p> Whishaw and Okonedo make the couple at the story's heart palpably real.</p><p> Elizabeth can often be seen as hatefully unforgiving but Okonedo finds the decency in her and allows us to see John as more self-flagellatingthan coldly done in.</p><p> Whishaw similarly avoids the heroic mode for John; his inability to see himself in the samevein as the almost saintly Rebecca Nurse is all too human and heartbreaking.</p><p> The lone exception to the casting is Tavi Gevinson as Mary Warren, the new servant of the Proctors now that Abigail is gone.</p><p> Young actresses always yearn for the role of Abigail, but Mary is the far more complex anddemanding part.</p><p> She must go from willful accomplice to somewhat reluctant truth-teller and then out of fearget pulled right back into the unspoken conspiracy of the accusing young women.</p><p> Gevinson debuted onBroadway in a revival of This Is Our Youth, playing a role pretty close to home.</p><p> Here she is quite out of her depth, unfortunately.</p><p> It's a pity Ronan didn't realize this meatier part was better.But The Crucible must overcome much more than one weak performance in an otherwise marvelous ensemble.</p><p> Typically, he has a score by Philip Glass that is compelling and beautiful but overdone.</p><p> It is present in the first act and omnipresent in the second, often overwhelming the performers. (It doesn't help that Rev.Hale is asked to speak so quietly at a key final moment that I had to lean forward and could barely hear him atall...and I was in the fifth row.</p><p> God help the people in the second balcony.) Glass's contribution is ominousand gorgeous; I just wish it had been deployed more judiciously.</p><p> Worse is the inexplicable decision to set the story in a different time period.</p><p> I suppose it doesn't really matter; the tale is timeless after all.</p><p> But the driving force of the entire conceit is the Salem Witch Trials as a stand-infor McCarthyism.</p><p> Instead of colonial times, the play is set in a vaguely modern schoolroom. (Given theloudspeakers we see, perhaps it's meant to be the 1950s?) Maybe, maybe one could make a case for settingit in a Congressional hearing a la the HUAC show trials.</p><p> Yet a schoolroom adds little oomph other thanreminding us of the youth of the young women at the heart of the story.</p><p> Playing off a line in the play, there's even a random if compelling stunt that lets nature loose upon the stage.But the really inexplicable choice comes towards the end.</p><p> If you're seeing the show, stop reading now, though since it's a classic any of the trappings conceived by a particular production can hardly be seen as spoilers.What Van Hove has done is to make manifest the witchcraft at issue.</p><p> In one brief passage the current lowersand then rises briefly while we spy a young woman flying through the air.</p><p> OK, that might just be a vision or aglimpse into what people fear or wonder or worry about.</p><p> No one else sees her and it's just a brief isolatedmoment that may or may not have actually happened.</p><p> But they make nonsense of the play towards the end when the apocalyptic fears come true.</p><p> As people argue and debate whether Satan is at play, the set literally crumbles around them, beams crashing to the groundand the winds of hell come whipping through an open window, wrecking havoc.</p><p> It's the end of the world.</p><p> Andeveryone is there.</p><p> Everyone sees it.</p><p> I mean, end of debate: Satan is in the house! But no one says a word!(How could they? It's not in the script.) What possible reason could there be for making the sorcery actuallyreal, other than the fact that no one has probably ever done that before?) This can only take away from the power of the story, not to mention undermining the very message Miller was offering: how hysteria and fear and jealousy can wreck lives even when the accused are blameless.</p><p> One couldtell a story that added in the complexity of recognizing that for the standards of the day, it's arguable someevil witchcraft circa Barbados was indeed taking place and thus the question of guilt and innocence is muddierthan it might seem to us today since we live in a world where Satanic rituals and magic are laughed at.</p><p> But that's not the play Miller wrote.</p><p> And these directorial flourishes ultimately amount to little more than that.</p><p> The essential drama remains, along with a compelling cast.</p><p> That's why this remains a satisfying evening oftheater despite the shenanigans on display.</p><p> Van Hove might have keyed off the sense of nature running amok that led him to have a wolf wander on stage when no one is around. (It's actually a rare breed of dog.) The set could have slowly disintegrated without theactors being aware.</p><p> The winds could have whipped through when people weren't present.</p><p> Maybe that wouldhave raised unsettling questions for the audience without interfering with the actual drama.</p><p> Instead TheCrucible boils over into some Exorcist-like extravaganza, distracting us from John Proctor's redemption, from the new-found love between him and Elizabeth, from the admirable bravery of the others being hanged andfrom the very point of The Crucible in the first place.</p><p> Why would Van Hove try all this? I guess the devil made him do it.</p><p> BRIGHT STAR ** out of **** CORT THEATRE It's almost a relief to realize Steve Martin won't immediately succeed at everything he attempts.</p><p> But this hugely successful stand-up comic, acclaimed actor and director, polished writer, Grammy-winning bluegrassmusician and even playwright hasn't hit it out of the park with that devilishly hard art-form the musical.</p><p> Still, the main problems with the modest and sweet Bright Star aren't Martin's, who handles the music, book and story.</p><p> They're the lyrics of Edie Brickell (who also contributed with the music and story), the direction of Walter Bobbie, the choreography of Josh Rhodes and a lumbering scenic design by Eugene Lee.</p><p> That's theproblem with musicals: it's a group effort and the group let Martin down.</p><p> The story is momentarily confusing but soon resolves itself.</p><p> The tale begins right after World War II with a soldier proudly returning home to North Carolina.</p><p> Billy (a very appealing A.J.</p><p> Shively) reunites with his dad(Stephen Bogardus) only to discover that there was no way to share the bad news: his momma is dead.</p><p> Ifanything that makes Billy more determined than ever to pursue his dream of a career writing. [Don't do it,Billy! You'll never pay the bills! But I digress.] His good friend Margo (Hannah Elless) works at the local bookstore and gosh she just loves and admires Billy's writing.</p><p> Of course, we also know she loves and admires Billy but it's going to take him all of act oneand most of act two before he realizes that.</p><p> Margo is Billy's biggest fan and his toughest critic, offering up lineby line edits and by gosh he's going to roll the dice and head to the big lights of Asheville, where a famedmagazine that's apparently the New Yorker of the south is ruthlessly overseen by the hard to please butcareer-making tastemaker Alice Murphy (Carmen Cusack in a winning Broadway debut).</p><p> Billy talks his way past the gatekeepers and charms Alice with his whopper of a lie.</p><p> She sees something in the kid, likes his writing enough to encourage him to write more and we're off to the races.</p><p> The show also flashesback some 20 years earlier where we see the buttoned up and all business Alice as a wild child of sorts.</p><p> She'sa poor gal and determined to make something of herself.</p><p> Alice wants to get away from her small town as badas Billy did.</p><p> But boy is Jimmy Ray (Paul Alexander Nolan) the son of the mayor a handsome sort.</p><p> In by far theshow's best number, Nolan and Cusack duet on a sexy, well-choreographed piece called "Whoa, Mama" thatshows them flirting and pushing the envelope for what is proper back then (and even now) at a picnic.</p><p> Quicker than you can say whack-a-doodle, Alice is at the doctor's finding out she's pregnant.</p><p> Jimmy Ray's father isn't about to let some upstart girl ruin his plans for the boy so he bundles her off to an isolated cabin,forces her daddy to give him custody of the child and then - in the show's most outlandish moment - insteadof giving it up for adoption simply tosses the infant off the back of a moving train.</p><p> So Alice is estranged fromJimmy Ray and her father, Jimmy Ray loses the woman he loves and won't speak to his dad and 20 yearslater we have to wonder how these two disparate stories are going to link up.</p><p> No points for figuring it out.The problems of Bright Star are many but the cast ain't one of 'em.</p><p> British actress Cusack really does wow in her Broadway debut. (I for one am sorry to have missed her in a UK show called Saucy Jack and the Space Vixens.) She pulls off the tricky gambit of playing a character both in her teens and two decades later withaplomb, sings beautifully and holds the show together with her charisma.</p><p> Nolan is every bit her equal in thesmaller part of Jimmy Ray.</p><p> Shively is the other main actor and his essentially decent, drama-free character isharder to keep interesting than one might think.</p><p> He has to be awfully dim about the girl of his dreams anddoesn't have to do much more than avoid the machinations of a big city gal and work hard at his writing.</p><p> ButShively keeps him real.</p><p> Everyone is good, really, from Dee Hoty as Alice's mom to Michael Mulheren as Jimmy Ray's foolishly ruthless dad.</p><p> Even small parts like that big city gal and her gay pal (both working for Alice) are put over strongly byEmily Padgett and Jeff Blumenkrantz.</p><p> Still, this is a musical and the songs are mostly not up to snuff.</p><p> I was worried the first time I heard the album of songs from the show recorded by Martin and Brickell.</p><p> The melodies were sprightly enough but the songs wereawfully repetitive lyrically and didn't seem to be the sort that would deepen character or push the storyforward.</p><p> Things are a little better onstage since the songs are broken up by having a lot more voices jumpingin rather than just Brickell's. (An artist I've liked, by the way.) But that can't compensate for thuddingly badlyrics in "A Man's Gotta Do" and so many others that really don't deepen character or push the story forward.</p><p> Worse of all is the presentation.</p><p> The costumes by Jane Greenwood are fine.</p><p> But the set is a boondoggle.</p><p> The main conceit is a sort of open-air log cabin on wheels that takes up a quarter of the stage.</p><p> Some of the liveband is in that cabin and it dominates the visual look of the show.</p><p> Shove it over here and it's the front door ofa cabin.</p><p> Shove it over there and it's supposed to be part of the bookstore.</p><p> Shove it back over here and it'ssomething else.</p><p> Actually, it never looks like anything except what it is: a big, lumbering, unappealingdistraction that actors are forever having to manhandle.</p><p> One worries the musicians will get seasick, beingspun this way and that throughout the night.</p><p> Actors leap through it and around it but there it is, precisely itselfand nothing more.</p><p> Tellingly, the few brief scenes where that cabin on wheels was not seen felt like a visualrelief.</p><p> It makes a hash of most everything.</p><p> The choreography has dancers up and over and around it, but you can't really grasp where anyone is supposed to be or why they're maneuvering around it.</p><p> One takes for granted thefluid direction that can tell a story with a minimum of props.</p><p> But not when you see a scene where Billy leaveshome and heads to Asheville.</p><p> Actors lift chairs and place them down, wave around blankets and Shivelytraipses this way and that to no good effect.</p><p> You recognize intellectually what they're trying to do but Bobbiefails to make it happen.</p><p> And that damn cabin looms in the background throughout.</p><p> And the apparent infanticide throws the story way out of whack, given its sensibility.</p><p> Oh, I know, folk songs and bluegrass music delve into dark corners.</p><p> But the warm demeanor on tap makes that unnecessary plottwist feel off-putting.</p><p> They could put a barrier between Alice and Jimmy Ray with far less drastic measures.Heck, even small details get overlooked.</p><p> That gay employee of Alice seems to be about to dally with acustomer at a bar they head to but this unspoken storyline is dropped as soon as it's raised.</p><p> And the bigdance number for Billy and Lucy peters out as he slips away, ending it on a deflating note.</p><p> It goes down harmlessly, thanks to talent like Nolan (charming in Daddy Long Legs), Shively and especially Cusack.</p><p> With weak songs, Bright Star was never going to shine that brightly.</p><p> But this very messy production does it no favors.</p><p> A simpler, more straightforward staging would do wonders for this gentle tale.</p><p> Tweak thestoryline and write a few new songs and Martin's old-fashioned piece could have a whole new life.</p><p> SHE LOVES ME *** out of **** ROUNDABOUT THEATRE COMPANY AT STUDIO 54 This revival of She Loves Me is a bright, sparkling bon-bon of a show without a care in the world.</p><p> That cheery disposition envelops everything from the pretty enough to eat set to the performances of the entire cast right down to star Laura Benanti's choice to sing in a whipped cream voice with classical stylings a la operetta.</p><p> The only problem with that is the fact that a truly great production of She Loves Me would include the dark veins that make this musical and the previous film and play The Shop Around The Corner classics.</p><p> Keep in mind that this confection charmingly mounted by The Roundabout includes attempted suicide, the shattering of a a decades long marriage, a lothario who callously uses women and deceives his innocentboss, heartbreak and even a central romance where the man has an unfair advantage of knowledge over thewoman that he pushes to the point where you almost (almost!) turn against him.</p><p> You wouldn't know itwatching this show.</p><p> What you do have is pleasurable fun delivered with aplomb by an excellent cast with all the care that the Roundabout can lavish upon it.</p><p> The story takes place in Budapest between the wars, 1934 to be exact, soyou know its dark approach is years away.</p><p> The setting is Maraczek's Parfumerie, a nicely run but not hugelyimportant store that specializes in lady's soaps and perfumes and jewel boxes and other knick knacks.</p><p> It's notMacy's but it's not rundown either and the employees are a family of sorts, the usual eccentric characterstossed together by employment.</p><p> Ilona (a delightful Jane Krakowski) is a looker who is having a secret romance with Steven (Gavin Creel), an oily sort who is happy to keep their affair hush-hush from the others.</p><p> Since she's a looker and so sweet, oneknows immediately he's not to be trusted.</p><p> Ladislav (the always dependable and winning Michael McGrath) simply keeps his head down and does his job.</p><p> In contrast, Georg (Zachary Levi of TV's Chuck and his great voice work on the animated gem Tangled ) is the rising star, a favorite of the owner and clearly with upper management in his sites.</p><p> That owner is the affable Mr.</p><p> Maraczek (played with ease by Byron Jennings).</p><p> Theyall dote on the messenger boy Arpad (the scene-stealing Nicholas Barasch), who yearns to become a clerksome day.</p><p> Their calm and familiar world is upended when Amalia (Laura Benanti) wrangles a job with an off-the-cuff sales pitch to a customer that wins Mr.</p><p> Maraczek a bet and places her at odds with Georg.</p><p> The two of themare constantly at each other's throats, with Amalia always just a little late to work and Georg always just a littletoo eager to criticize her.</p><p> They're made for each other obviously but what do they care? Each of them has asecret pen pal, a person they've never met but with whom they've been exchanging letter via a lovelorn ad.THAT'S the person they're met for, not this annoying fellow or gal right in front of them.</p><p> Of course, a long-awaited date to meet in person reveals to Georg in fact their secret pen pal is their rival at work.Complications ensue before we can find out whether they can find romance in real life or only on the page.One such problem: Georg is inexplicably forced to quit by a furious Mr.</p><p> Maraczek for no good reason.</p><p> Goodheavens, how will it all turn out? I've never seen the original play by Miklós Låszló, but the 1940 film starring Jimmy Stewart and Margaret Sullavan is one of the all-time greats.</p><p> What you remember is the delightful romantic comedy.</p><p> But every timeyou see watch it, you're struck anew by the mournful streak that runs through it.</p><p> Mr.</p><p> Maraczek thinks Georg isthe one cheating with his beloved wife, which is why Maraczek both pushes Georg to quit and attempts toshoot himself.</p><p> When Amalia thinks she's been stood up by her secret pen pal, she's genuinely heartbrokenand knowing that Georg can tease and toy with her makes us a little uncomfortable.</p><p> The danger to the singleIlona of being toyed with by a man in that era is always an unspoken concern.</p><p> It's a little like It's A WonderfulLife.</p><p> She Loves Me shouldn't be that dark, but all you remember about Stewart's holiday classic is the wave of joy at the end.</p><p> What makes that so effective is the depths of despair it plummets before then.Similarly, a really great She Loves Me would have more weight and reality.</p><p> This production seems to take place in a fairy land of all reward and no risk, happy ending guaranteed.</p><p> The set design of David Rockwell is delightful.</p><p> The streets of Budapest look adorable and the exterior of the Parfumerie opens up like a presentbeing unwrapped.</p><p> The store itself is a wonderland of bright colors and beautiful wares.</p><p> The employees aredressed to the nines, with not just Ilona but everyone looking positively smashing.</p><p> Director Scott Ellis fails to ground the show in reality the way it should be to really pierce our hearts.</p><p> This isn't Astaire and Rogers in a high gloss fantasy.</p><p> The yearning and heartbreak on display is much more powerful ifthese are regular folk, not glamor gals.</p><p> The store is just a store, not Willy Wonka's factory.</p><p> Mr.</p><p> Maraczek is asdesperate as a man can be, trying to shoot himself to death at the end of act one.</p><p> But at the beginning of acttwo, he seems positively jolly in his hospital room just hours later.</p><p> Jennings is rightly taking his cue from theentire production yet the show suffers for it.</p><p> Later, when he is alone in the world and desperate for company,Mr.</p><p> Maraczek settles for dining with the messenger boy turned clerk Arpad.</p><p> Except here it plays more like abachelor party in the making rather than a sad old man picking up the pieces of his life.</p><p> Benanti sings in that semi-classical style I mentioned, which she puts over with ease.</p><p> It's the vocal equivalent of the costumes and sets, divorcing us from the reality of what's going on.</p><p> Nothing could ever really go alongin a world where people can sing like that, certainly not genuine sadness and loneliness.</p><p> She seems to beauditioning for the role of Eliza in My Fair Lady, a role which by God she can and should be doing on Broadway soon.</p><p> But it heightens the fantasy and that lowers our emotional investment.</p><p> Levi in contrast has a modest voice and can't do more than offer a regular-guy spin on singing and dancing.</p><p> Ironically, that makes him better casting for Georg though we'll never know if he could get past the glibsurface and find the drama that resides underneath this take.</p><p> To be fair, the others downplay the hijinks andkeep their characters pretty realistic too.</p><p> If Ellis had done the show justice, he certainly had a cast that coulddeliver.</p><p> This is my first time seeing She Loves Me and one terrible choice resides with the book by Joe Masteroff. (The impeccable music is by Jerry Bock and the lyrics by Sheldon Harnick of Fiddler On The Roof of course.) Masteroff sets that long-awaited meeting of pen pals in a fancy restaurant that is an assignation for lovers, often illicit in nature.</p><p> The redoubtable Peter Bartlett does his best with the role of an officious waiter trying tomaintain 'A Romantic Atmosphere." But people on the make is a far cry from the romantic innocence of ourtwo pen pals.</p><p> This setting - played for drawn out, broad laughter - feels utterly at odds with the scene and theshow.</p><p> Happily, the cast rises above.</p><p> Krakowski is an utter pleasure as Ilona and Creel avoids the moustache-twirling that would have made Steven a panto villain.</p><p> Mcgrath is so immediately likable you always wish he'd get tocarry a show rather than do the heavy lifting for others.</p><p> Levi has charm and chemistry to spare with Benanti,who is a pro and anyone's idea of a winning leading lady, despite her misguided vocal choice here.</p><p> And in thesmall role of Arpad, Barasch follows the lead of the rest and avoids mugging.</p><p> He's a charming presence andsomehow manages to make the most of his show-stopper turn in "Try Me" - bouncing on the hospital bedand practically off the hospital walls too - without actually looking like he's trying to stop the show.</p><p> He makes the most of his opportunity.For all my caveats and misgivings, it was great to finally hear this score and see this show performed live for the first time.</p><p> Song after song glides by, from the opener "Good Morning, Good Day" to the amusingpenultimate "Twelve Days Of Christmas." Act Two almost floats by on one winner after another: theaforementioned "Try Me" followed by the comic "Where's My Shoe?" to the peak "Vanilla Ice Cream" and thetitle track and Krakowski's peak "A Trip To the Library."
