If you like Westerns and don't usually like musicals, an OK western will probably appeal to you more than a decent musical.</p><p> Of course a great musical should give you more enjoyment than a decent Western, assuming you don't break into hives the moment someone breaks into song.It's all arbitrary and critics rarely discuss how simply being in the right mood for a dumb college romp/action flick/sappy romance can make you enjoy a movie more than you otherwise might.</p><p> It gets even more confusing when talking about a family film.</p><p> Do my standards change when watching a movie geared towards kids? Nope.</p><p> But I might have fun at a movie because I went with my nephews more than I might if I'd gone alone.(Though that still won't make me like G-Force .) Which is a long way around to admitting that on Twitter ( follow me! ) I gave Hannah Montana: The Movie ($39.99 regular or $44.99 on BluRay; Disney) two stars out of four and Dragonball: Evolution ($29.98 regular or $39.98 on BluRay) just one star, much to the disgust of Adrian, a 12 year old friend of mine who watched all of Dragonball with me and got bored with Hannah about half way through. (His little sister, Laurel, however, loved it.) Dragonball: Evolution is the movie that came from a TV show that came from a manga about seven magical dragonballs that must be collected in order to defeat Piccolo before he can take over the world.</p><p> I kind of like the lead actor Justin Chatwin (he was in War Of The Worlds and the TV miniseries Traffic ), who plays the unfortunately named Goku.</p><p> But probably two things really kept me from enjoying this film even on a dumb level.</p><p> One, it co-stars Chow Yun-Fat as Master Roshi, and seeing such a great actor have to perform a secondary role in such a stupid film was upsetting.</p><p> It would be like watching Clint Eastwood having a secondary role in a bad cop film. (I was gonna say "in Reno: 911 " but that's actually something I'd want to watch.) Her dorkily cute brother Jackson (Jason Earles).</p><p> Totally different movies and neither one is actually good, butI'd rather watch Hannah Montana again if I had to, rather than DE.</p><p> So if you're in the mood for a really dumb action movie, watch something other than Dragonball: Evolution .</p><p> And if you're in the mood for a family friendly light drama with some tuneful music and you can't watch High School Musical again no matter how much your kids plead, there are worse things in the world than Hannah Montana: The Movie .</p><p> And if Hannah/Miley and Goku were locked into the Thunderdome in a fight to the finish, my money would be on Hannah.</p><p> Especially if the winner got some really nice shoes.</p><p> Also Out This Week: Kagemusha ($39.98 on BluRay; Criterion) -- I often wondered why some Hollywood directors didn't just lend their name to Orson Welles and allow him to make more movies. (Of course, the biggest reason was probably Welles himself, a man paralyzed by his brilliant early success and too unfocused to see countless projects through to fruition -- beginning as early as The MagnificentAmbersons .) But sometimes talented directors did behave like the fans they are: that's why George Lucas and Francis Ford Coppola are such a happy presence on this DVD.</p><p> Kurosawa was always more beloved in the West than in Japan.</p><p> And after cost over-runs and a tyrannical reputation made Japanese studios shy away from him, Kurosawa spent years trying to finance this samuraiepic.</p><p> He tried to work with Hollywood via Tora! Tora! Tora! but then backed out, adding to a difficult reputation that Welles might have envied.</p><p> He even attempted suicide.</p><p> But finally,Kurosawa won the Oscar for a Russian co-production...and still found himself waiting years to getthe money together for Kagemusha .</p><p> Until Lucas and Coppola stepped in, allowing Kurosawa to make this three hour acclaimed classic that led to his final masterpiece, Ran .</p><p> Criterion offers a bevy of extras including an informative essay by Peter Grilli, audio commentary by a scholar, amaking-of documentary, the Suntory whiskey ads shot on the set and a 19 minute short in which Lucas and Coppola take a well-deserved bow.</p><p> Also out on Criterion this week is Repulsion ($39.95 on regular or BluRay), Roman Polanski's fine followup to Knife In The Water.</p><p> It would be followed by two lesser films until Rosemary's Baby and Chinatown made him a legend forever.</p><p> It's wonderful and ironic that Criterion -- which always charged a well-deserved premium on laser disc -- sells its movies for the same price on regular or BluRay.</p><p> Both look smashing, though I must admit with a BluRay player that makes the regular DVD look as sharp as possible, the differencesare more modest than usual because Criterion takes such care to provide excellent prints.</p><p> Richard Pryor: Live & Smokin' ($14.95; Genius) -- Here's a fascinating glimpse at Richard Pryor just on the cusp of stardom back in 1971.</p><p> This 46 minute set filmed in New York finds Pryor a bit overawed by the cameras recording his routine and an audience perhaps overawed by his casual use of the word nigger and raw, sexually explicit take on life.</p><p> Frankly, the first half of the set isfairly routine despite Pryor's raw edge.</p><p> He seems a little awkward and the jokes are pretty familiar to audiences today. (Lenny Bruce's routines can seem pretty tame out of context of the times, too.) People aren't quite sure when to laugh, Pryor is nervous and the set doesn't catch fire.</p><p> Then out ofnowhere Pryor launches into an extended monologue portraying two people: a wino and a younger junkie the wino is counseling.</p><p> Suddenly Pryor is riveting.</p><p> He creates complete, marvelously detailed characters the way Lily Tomlin would in years to come -- a slurred word, an angry frown, alean to the right and Pryor has captured the essence of someone; a stock character becomes a genuine person; and it's hilariously heart-breakingly funny.</p><p> It's leagues beyond the set-up and punchline material Pryor offered earlier in the set.</p><p> You understand why he became a star and alsowonder why he didn't get more dramatic work in the movies.</p><p> Clearly, Pryor had the talent to do itall. 17 Again ($28.99 regular and $35.99 on BluRay; Disney) -- Even as an 11 year old kid (please don't do the math), I remember finding this live-action/animated flick pretty thin going.</p><p> I was being kind.</p><p> This attempt to replicate the magic of Mary Poppins a decade after Uncle Walt died proves how very difficult family movies can be.</p><p> Pete is an orphan (natch) who runs away with the help of a dragon who can make himself invisible so he won't scare people.</p><p> Half the movie is spent with Pete scolding thedragon to behave himself (which usually acts like an over-eager sheep dog) and the other half ofthe movie involves stars like Mickey Rooney, Red Buttons and Shelley Winters hamming it up. (Co-lead Helen Reddy keeps her head down and the mugging to a minimum.) You know a film is going to be unbearably sweet when the very first song involves a little boy and dragon singing "I love you" to each other.</p><p> Slightly less than brazzle dazzle-y (a would-be charmer of a tune), but there are lots of extras.</p><p> Only the smallest of tykes will sit through this one.</p><p> Dexter Season Three ($42.99 regular and $57.99 on BluRay; Nickelodeon) -- Books are a funny thing, especially picture books and children's books -- we identify with them so strongly and on such a basic level that any adaptation of them that doesn't hit exactly the right note can be off-putting.</p><p> I never came up with any firm idea of howI thought Olivia would look or sound in a TV show, but as soon as I saw this computer-animatedseries, I thought...nope.</p><p> That's not it.</p><p> The animation just doesn't feel like the right style for the very old school Olivia, who counts Eleanor Roosevelt as a hero, among others.</p><p> I find the delightful picture books by Ian Falconer wonderful and have written about them for years .</p><p> I do think these eight tales capture the spirit of Olivia quite well and they're certainly not bad.</p><p> But I can't helpthinking they failed to match the proper style of animation for this material.</p><p> The Beatles Rare and Unseen ($14.95; MVD) -- I enjoyed watching this 90 minute grab-bag of old Beatles footage and new interviews.</p><p> John Lennon chatting with French TV? On set footage from Help ?The only existing film from their 1964 tour of Scotland? Count me in! Of course, I'm also foaming at the mouth to purchase the Beatles boxed sets of their remastered albums -- both the stereo AND the mono versions.</p><p> None of it is revealing or revelatory in the least.</p><p> But if you care, you care.</p><p> The 5 Deadly Venoms ($19.97; Genius) -- I'm no martial arts movie expert, though I've seen my fair chunk of the masterpieces.</p><p> Funnily enough, martial arts fighting can date just as easily as special effects.</p><p> You'd think fighting is fighting but the way they're staged, edited and the sound effects that accompany them can age faster than a guy in a gorilla suit in a jungle epic.</p><p> So The 5 Deadly Venoms may be influential in one way or another.</p><p> But all I know is that it's a "classic" martial arts movie in that the subtitling is poor, the acting poorer and most of the fights are pretty tame by today's standards.</p><p> It perks up considerably at the finale, where vague hints of fantastical powers come to the fore (such as fighters perched on a wall high off the ground while fighting theirenemies) but it was too little too late for me.</p><p> Extras include commentary by Hong Kong cinema expert Bey Logan, who unquestionably knows much more than me but can't change how bored I was while watching this.</p><p> Also out is An Empress and the Warriors ($19.97; Genius) which was directed by the guy who did the action choreography for Hero and House Of Flying Daggers and therefore deserves to direct as much as he can.</p><p> Inglorious Basterds ($34.95 on BluRay; Severin) -- With Quentin Tarantino's tremendously fun Inglorious Basterds opening today, it's a good time to catch up with B movie maestro Enzo Castellari's film of the same name, which Tarantino loves.</p><p> His movie is not a remake of this one,MORE BIG NEWS PAGES » Books by this author On the ball: with his new play All That I Will EverBe, a post-Six Feet UnderAlan Ball proves there'slife afterdeath.(THEATER): Anarticle from: The ...
