It's even rarer when that experience is actually fun.</p><p> That's whathappened last fall when I attended Figaro 90210!, a multi-culti Los Angeles update to the classic Mozart opera set in the world of undocumented workers.</p><p> Suddenly The MarriageOf Figaro involved pot-smoking landscapers, hip-hop teens rapping to some classical beats and true love endangered by a lecherous boss who knows one phone call can sendyou across the border and away from your sweetheart.</p><p> Conceived and with a new libretto by Vid Guerrerio, it combines a modern setting and all- new English lyrics with the music of Mozart for a show that is much shorter than mostoperas and fresh enough to give a new spin to that classic tale.</p><p> Here's a video clip of "It'sLike, Yo, I Don't Know" from last fall's staged reading.</p><p> That brief run was a success and it's coming back June 11-16 via Morningside Opera at the NSD Theatre in NYC.</p><p> In the clip, the artists were only accompanied by a single pianist.</p><p> For this run, a new group of performers is joined by a pianist and a five string ensemble,making for a much more powerful musical punch.</p><p> Sometimes, happily, friends of is an artist still searching for her voice.</p><p> She evinces a playful love for language a la Caryl Churchill.</p><p> She embraces the arch absurdity of Will Eno.</p><p> But as of yet, what she lacks is humanity, the ability to turn intellectual gambits into emotional truths, odd conceits into characters we care about even if we don't quite know why.</p><p> Somewhere Fun becomes exhausting by the end but it's interesting enough to believe her next play or the one after that might be the breakthrough she needs.</p><p> The first act is the strongest.</p><p> Rosemary Rappaport (Kate Mulgrew) is striding down a New York City street when she spots Evelyn (Kathleen Chalfant), an old acquaintance now in a wheelchair.</p><p> They banter and Rosemary heads to lunch where she holds court, steamrolling over Cecelia (Mary Schultz) though Cecelia has news of her own thanks to an internet romance.</p><p> The dialogue here is artificial and amusing when delivered by a pro like Mulgrew.</p><p> She relishes the word play, the repetitions and -- for a while -- Schultz manages to convey the way people talk past each other.</p><p> The style on display reaches for a heightened reality.</p><p> It's a lot of work to listen to since it's all linguistic razzle-dazzle.</p><p> Yes, information is imparted but it's mostly the verbal juggling that interests the playwrightand a little bit of that goes a long way.</p><p> The show is only 135 minutes or so, but youunderstand why it has two intermissions -- the audience simply needs a break.</p><p> But the distinctive patter Schwartz sets up in that first act -- a patter Mulgrew makes themost of -- slowly dissipates.</p><p> The dialogue becomes more naturalistic, though no morenatural or convincing.</p><p> Soon we have flashbacks to characters as children (a very weakpoint of the show) and a surfeit of plot: cancer, a young woman with no face (it was tornoff by a dog), budding romance, estranged family members, a woman who has melted intothe sidewalk, an elderly romance.</p><p> Unfortunately, none of this feels convincing -- it's just aframework on which to hang some more verbal pyrotechnics and those have lost theirrazzle dazzle, as fireworks always do.</p><p> The result is a tiring work with diminishing returns, though you can only diminish so much when talents like Mulgrew and Chalfant are on stage.</p><p> They both shoulder theburden of this effort with their usual grace and are joined by a generally solid cast.</p><p> Schultzis quite good as an old friend with a new love, Richard Bekins and Brooke Bloom are solidin smaller turns and Maria Elena Ramirez manages to infuse some life and humor intothe omnipresent but little-noticed care-giver.</p><p> Greg Keller is a standout again, as he was inthe equally confused show Belleville by Amy Herzog.</p><p> He has a wonderful, gravelly voice and an ease on stage that's winning; I look forward to seeing him in something bettersoon.</p><p>
