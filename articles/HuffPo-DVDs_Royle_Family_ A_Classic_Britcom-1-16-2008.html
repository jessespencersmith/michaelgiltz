BBC Video).</p><p> But that simple idea is executed so brilliantly that this wonderfully acted,hilariously funny sitcom takes its place proudly alongside classics like Fawlty Towers, Blackadder,Absolutely Fabulous, Monty Python's Flying Circus and the original UK version of The Office.</p><p> I'm not exaggerating: for half an hour, the Royles just sit there and watch the TV.</p><p> It doesn't take long to sort out the cast.</p><p> Jim Royle (Ricky Tomlinson) is a crotch-scratching, farting, man of thehouse with a million opinions and none higher than his opinion of himself.</p><p> He rails against the high cost of living, though if he has any job it escapes notice.</p><p> His wife Barbara (Sue Johnston) takes a job at the local bakery and is forever lighting up and crushing out cigarettes into an overflowing ashtray.</p><p> She also has a gift for turning one word lines like "Oh" into multi-syllabic symphonies of concern, admiration, curiosity and whatever other emotion she can squeeze out of it.</p><p> Their daughter Denise (co-creator Caroline Aherne) becomes more and more sluggish as the season wears on because she's "overwhelmed" with the prospect of marrying the stolid, unflappable Dave Best (co-creator Craig Cash).</p><p> And underfoot of them all is teenager Antony (Ralf Little) who is abused as a lazy arse even though he is always the one who has to make the tea, empty the garbage, run down to the corner for ciggies, answer the phone and so on.</p><p> Like The Honeymooners, The Royle Family revels in repetition and grows richer on repeated viewing.</p><p> Whenever anyone enters the house, Barbara askswhat they had for tea and then parses the answer.("Liver and onions? Ooooh.") The first season is sopitch-perfect and strikes such a delicate balance between revealing the lives of these people and letting absolutely nothing happen that you fear itshould never have been repeated.
