Thursday, November 14, 2019  NON-FICTION The Root of All Evil ‘The Finance Curse: How Global Finance is Making Us All Poorer’  November 13, 2019 Michael Giltz We’re in the midst of a bull market when it comes to books on inequality, the ills of Wall Street, tax justice and how to fix the whole damn mess.</p><p> Though well-intentioned, many of these books have flaws.</p><p> Some are too geeky–Thomas Piketty, we’re looking at you.</p><p> Some are so overwhelmed by the sheer number of financial crimes to choose from their litany of abuses becomes a blur.</p><p> The Finance Curse gets it just right.</p><p> Journalist Nick Shaxson has written for The Guardian and works with the Tax Justice Network .</p><p> Here he provides a sharp history of finance and the many storied criminals of the past, among them robber barons like Rockefeller and his ilk.</p><p> Then Shaxson smoothly and lucidly dives into a chapter-by-chapter breakdown of global finance and how it’s swallowing up the world economy.At the heart of his story? Not Wall Street, but London.</p><p> The British Empire led to its second empire of inherently criminal tax havens.</p><p> And that led to the Bill Clinton/Tony Blair Third Way of neo-liberalism to Ireland’s paper Celtic Tiger right up to the rapacious hedge funds and private equity firms of today.</p><p> If none of that means much to you, it will after you read this book.</p><p> If it’s familiar to you, Shaxson is a treat to watch tie it all together.</p><p> Anyone raging against the system from the left or the right should find common groundright here.</p><p> Indeed, The Finance Curse is a righteous cri du coeur.</p><p> But it avoids the frat-boy trash talk of Matt Taibbi and the blinkered timidity of those on the right afeared of being called anti-capitalism.</p><p> Reading it, my inchoate understanding that the rules are rigged and the bad guys are breaking them anyway because they’re such bastards became more grounded.</p><p> Shaxson makes a meal of neo-liberalism.</p><p> He feasts on the flimsy idea that countries must “compete” by deregulating before the country next door deregulates and wins a race to the bottom.</p><p> He continues to gorge,showing how the horrific bloat of finance makes the global economy more dangerously unstable than ever.</p><p> For dessert, Shaxson savors in reversing the idea of takers and makers.</p><p> Again and again, he demonstrates that the biggest “takers” of all are not the working class and poor who struggle to survive on a minimum wage.</p><p> Theyare, in fact, the Wall Street fat cats who extract wealth everywhere they prowl.← The End of ‘The Affair’ The Five Best Irish Mob Movies →Two of his points loom large.</p><p> First, Wall Street is far from alone.</p><p> London’s financial district and its worldwide empire of illegitimate tax havens are equally if not more to blame for the dangerous ills of the world economy.</p><p> Second, he underlines the sheer criminality of it all.</p><p> We tend to think of the financial industry and the one percent as above the law, people who can bend it to their whims.</p><p> They rig the rules and yet the rules don’t quiteapply to them.</p><p> All true.</p><p> And yet they still break the law.</p><p> They don’t just arrange for loopholes and “legally” avoid taxes.</p><p> The wealthy and multi-national corporations commit crimes again and again, every day, all over the world.That’s good news.</p><p> Because we can catch criminals.</p><p> Finally, despite Shaxson’s meticulous reporting I must regretfully point out a substantial fact error.</p><p> It appears on the cover of the book, for Pete’s sake.</p><p> The subtitle of The Finance Curse is “How Global Finance Is Making UsAll Poorer.” Wrong.</p><p> Not ALL of us are becoming poorer because of global finance.</p><p> Just 99 percent of us.
