(By the way, when people say a film was greeted with a standing ovation at Cannes, they're referring to the evening performance for invited guests and the stars where a standing ovation is almost obligatory.</p><p> It's the morning screening for the critics where people will applaud and boo with gusto and that's the reception that matters.) It's a familiar story, but of course one could say thatabout virtually any film.</p><p> But when a film is specific and true, when its characters come alive, it doesn't matter if you're watching boy meets girl or a mother fighting to keep her family together or a triumph over adversity.</p><p> When it's done well, it becomes fresh all over again.</p><p> In this case, it's the story of a harmless young man sent to prison for some unknown crime.</p><p> Within minutes, it seems, he's entered a horror zone where men attack and beat him with impunity.</p><p> Soon,one of the strong men of the prison -- the Corsican leader Cesar (Niels Arestrup) -- is demandingour hero Malik (Tahir Rahim) kill another prisoner slated to testify in court.</p><p> Malik is a mutt, of sorts, and can pass between many groups but is despised by all.</p><p> The Muslims see him as Corsican, the Corsicans see him as a dirty Arab, the gypsies see him as weak, the guards don't see him at alland when he's beaten there is no one to help.</p><p> Will Malik commit the murder? Will he become hardened and cruel or get eaten alive or somehow survive but maintain his humanity? Co-writerand director Jacques Audiard has made some very good films this decade.</p><p> Read My Lips was a fun thriller about a deaf woman partnering with her lover to pull off a crime.</p><p> The Beat That My Heart Skipped was an unlikely remake of the cult film Fingers by James Toback that surpassed the original.</p><p> But this film is a major leap forward.</p><p> It's visually gripping, with shadowy passages interspersed throughout the film where all we see is a fuzzy glimpse of what is happening or mightbe happening soon. (Don't forget the title of the film is "A Prophet.") And the score is sparely usedand very effective: at key moments, the theme overwhelms the action and transports Malik from a man going about his furtive business to a person taking charge of his destiny.</p><p> Both lead actors are exceptional and I'm certain it will snag at least one major award.</p><p> Perhaps it is this year's Gomorrah , a crime film that was embraced by critics but didn't quite break out in the US.</p><p> But however this fares commercially, you don't want to miss it.</p><p> JAFFA -- ** (out of four) A melodramatic Israeli film with some nicely restrained acting by two lovers surrounded by scenery chewing and a cheap, intrusive score.</p><p> The set-up has potential.</p><p> An Israeli family owns a garage where the two main mechanics are Arabs (perhaps Palestinians?) good at their jobs.</p><p> Unfortunately, they're overseen by the tiresome owner's son who can't be bothered to do any work but makes everyone else's lives miserable. (His own mother calls him aplague.) What's immediately apparent is that the daughter is in love with the sweet-faced, youngerArab mechanic and they're planning to elope right away (she's pregnant).</p><p> A tragic accident/crime ensues and everyone is miserable from then on.</p><p> Frankly, it's hard to take seriously a film where a young woman ages almost a decade -- and suddenly has a 9 year old daughter to prove it -- butlooks exactly the same.</p><p> MOTHER -- ** 1/2 (out of four) Bong Joon Ho's The Host -- the best monster movie in years -- was so much fun I can't help being a bit let down by his new film, even though it has merit.</p><p> A mentally challenged young man is railroaded by the police into confessing to the murder of a high school girl that has shocked the community.</p><p> He was seen in the area where the body was found, drinking and flirting with another high school girl just hours earlier.</p><p> Finding a golf ball with hisname on it near the body didn't help his case either.</p><p> What's to investigate? But the young man's determined mother wants to clear his name.</p><p> She starts by hiring the most expensive attorney in town but he's too dismissive.</p><p> So Mother stars investigating on her own, discovering that themurdered girl was famously promiscuous and had a cell phone with pictures of all the men that slept with her.</p><p> Wouldn't any loving mother hide in the homes of thugs to get evidence, hire people to beat up potential witnesses and get information or do just about anything to free her son? Boththe mother and her son are well-acted, but after the fifth or so shocking revelation, the story becomes both more far fetched and more predictable.</p><p> That doesn't take away from the central appeal of the revenge/justice engine that drives the film.</p><p> But it's ultimately more of a melodramathan a gripping potrayal of what love can drive people to do.</p><p> SAMSON & DELILAH -- *** (out of four) One of my most anticipated films of the fest isn't a home run but it's unquestionably an accomplished debut and the start of a promising career for director/writer/editor/cinematographer/composer and for all I know caterer Warwick Thornton.</p><p> This Australian talent has crafted an almost silent film about two young people who fall in love -- one warily and one guilelessly.</p><p> Samson is a huffer (a kid addicted to breathing in toxic fumes frompaint or gasoline or anything else that can get you high); he's got a brother in a band who plays the same damn song with his mates over and over again.</p><p> Delilah is a capable girl taking care of her aged grandmother while they craft lovely art sold far from their aboriginal home for a lot moremoney than they are paid.</p><p> Scene after scene unfolds with virtually no dialogue, though no big deal is made about this and it's done so artfully you won't think much of it until you're halfway through the film and realized how little dialogue is actually spoken.</p><p> Samson brings his bedroll over toDelilah's home.</p><p> She tosses it over the fence.</p><p> He puts it back over the fence.</p><p> She tosses it out again.He wears her down and sleeps by the fire far from where she rests.</p><p> But in the morning his bedroll is right next to hers.</p><p> She tosses it onto the fire.</p><p> It doesn't stop him.</p><p> The sweet, almost comic tone of the film slips away when Delilah's grandmother dies and a burst of violence sends them far fromtheir aboriginal community into town where we realize how outcast they truly are.</p><p> If they walk intoa supermarket, the guards follow them around suspiciously.</p><p> Even walking down the road they are targets for disdain and violence.</p><p> Numerous tragic turns -- including one major "cheat" by the film;
