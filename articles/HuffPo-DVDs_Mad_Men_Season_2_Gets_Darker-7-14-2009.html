Film Movement) -- Essentially a road movie, this is the shaggy dog story of a burly middle-aged man Yvan who comes home to find a burglar named Elie ransacking his place.</p><p> You immediately know you're in offbeat territory when this tense scene devolves into a comic standoff, with Yvan insisting the burglar come out of his hiding place and the burglar refusing.</p><p> Naturally, of course, he ends up offering the burglar a ride home. I've never been a fan of the Tom and Jerry cartoons, to say the least.</p><p> Simplistic, dull, repetitive and tiresome -- I'd call them the poor man's Wile E Coyote and Roadrunner, but I'm not a big fan ofTHOSE cartoons either. (Mind you, they play like witty plays by Oscar Wilde compared to Tom and Jerry.) But Chuck Jones is one of the giants of animation.</p><p> Maybe his Tom and Jerry shorts have an elan and cleverness the others lack.</p><p> So I popped in the disc and...nope.</p><p> Apparently, you can haveOrson Welles direct the Ritz Brothers but it would still be the Ritz Brothers and not the Marx Brothers.</p><p> The saving grace of the Chuck Jones set -- which contains 34 shorts and no, I most assuredly did NOT watch them all -- is a delightful, poignant profile of Jones in which he talksabout his childhood, stories that combine on camera interviews and casual, charming animation.It's an Oscar-worthy 26 minute ode to one of the greats.</p><p> Greatest Chases Vol. 2 contains 14 more shorts, so clearly the Chuck Jones set is a better bargain for those who care.</p><p> SHERLOCK HOLMES ($24.95; Kino) -- As a Sherlock Holmes buff, I was intrigued to finally see this 1922 silent film version starring John Barrymore.</p><p> Not only do you get to see Barrymore in top form, it's the closest I'll get to seeing the exceptionally popular stage play it sprang from.</p><p> Theresult is intriguing, especially for those already fans of Holmes.</p><p> We see Holmes as a college student just developing his ideas about detection when the travails of a fellow Oxfordian bring him face to face with future arch nemesis Moriarty (played by the cadaverous Gustav von Seyffertitz, who is somorbid you assume his day job is as an undertaker).</p><p> Seyffertitz is the best thing here -- he plays Moriarty with a compelling stillness that rivets your attention.</p><p> But you also get William Powell's film debut, not to mention gossip queen Hedda Hopper in an early acting role and stalwart Britishfilm presence Reginald Denny.</p><p> But of course it's Holmes you pay the most attention to here.Barrymore has the angular, inquisitive features that are perfect for the role and his bounding youth grows quite nicely into the sober detective.</p><p> It's disconcerting to see Holmes moon over a girl but how were they to know in 1922 that Holmes always should be a confirmed bachelor?
