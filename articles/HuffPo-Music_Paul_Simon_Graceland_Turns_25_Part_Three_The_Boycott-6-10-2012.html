(I'm still not clear if this was a mixtape someone created on their own,an album of music solely by the Boyoyo Boys or a legitimate compilation including multiple artists.)Simon couldn't stop listening to it and ultimately started writing lyrics to go along with the track,something he'd never done before in his life.</p><p> He wasn't even sure who had created the music or whetherthey were still alive or could be tracked down.</p><p> But he had to find out more.</p><p> That led to him reaching outand establishing contact with various people familiar with the music scene in Soweto.</p><p> That led to a floodof more music, the best that South Africa had to offer.</p><p> The more he listened, the more excited Simonbecame.</p><p> Thank goodness, because after almost 20 year of uninterrupted artistic praise and commercial success, Simon was at a rare low point.</p><p> He rather disingenuously says in the film that the commercial failure ofhis 1983 album Hearts And Bones was fine by him -- it meant his record label suddenly wasn't standing over his shoulder watching everything he did.</p><p> It seems unlikely that Warner Bros. immediatelyconsidered one of their premiere acts to be washed up.</p><p> And Hearts And Bones wasn't an isolated incident.</p><p> In 1980, he wrote and starred in the film One Trick Pony , a flop on every level that produced a soundtrack that surprisingly was also poorly received.</p><p> Simon surely imagined that the soundtrack was one aspect of the film he didn't have to worry about. ("Late In The Evening" has proven the mostenduring tune from it.) It wasn't simply that the movie did not do well.</p><p> It was an almost embarrassingfailure, especially for an artist who had gone from strength to strength for so long. (Simon would repeatthe experience to a lesser degree on Broadway with The Capeman in 1997; that music would flourish in a concert setting years later.) He followed this failure with Hearts And Bones , which originally was going to reunite him with Art Garfunkel.</p><p> At the last minute, he erased Art's vocals and it became another soloalbum, the first release of Simon's career not to go gold.</p><p> For an artist used to Grammy awards and hit singles, his career in the past decade since the peak of Still Crazy After All These Years was surely disheartening.</p><p> Feeling a glimmer of artistic rebirth was exciting and -- let's be fair -- launching into such a crazy, unusual project that upended his entire creative process must have been scary and exciting inequal measures.MOST POPULAR ON HUFFPOST 1 of 2 Was The Whole Donald Trump Announcement A Gigantic, Pointless Bust? First he made contacts to figure out who the acts were and whether they would be available.</p><p> As you can imagine, when an act in Soweto that wasn't even that hot a commodity locally wasapproached by one of the most famous artists in the world, why yes, they were available.</p><p> What about the boycott? Simon went to his friend Harry Belafonte and the singer/actor/activist spelled out exactly what he needed to do.</p><p> Simon needed to approach the African National Congress (ANC) and get permission to do what he wanted.</p><p> Belafonte immediately sensed Simon resisted, though Simon didn't tell him so.</p><p> Go to a political group and get "permission" to make music? That didn't sound like something he wanted to do, his Sixties knee-jerk resistance to authority kicking in at the mention of needing "permission" to make music.</p><p> In one of the film's most amusing passages, Simon describes what he later did as like a teenager who is told he can't use the car, but he has a date and really wants to use the car and goes ahead and does it anyway.</p><p> So Simon approached the artists via the musicians' union in South Africa and asked them directly.</p><p> Simon says at one point in the film that he was invited to South Africa but the truth is that he asked to be invited.</p><p> The union met and took a vote and gave its approval for Simon to come and record.</p><p> That wasgood enough for him, though he knew it wouldn't be good enough for Belafonte and purposefully saidnothing about ignoring the ANC.</p><p> In effect, Simon did indeed break the cultural boycott intended toisolate the South African government and avoid anything that might legitimize the system of apartheid.</p><p> He went to South Africa and recorded for about 10 to 12 days.</p><p> The basis of a studio group was formed and those musicians would become the core of his touring group for decades to come.</p><p> In another veryrevealing passage, Simon says the initial recording session with the Boyoyo Boys was disheartening.</p><p> Whether due to nerves or whatever, they couldn't deliver musically.</p><p> One of the white studio engineers dismissively said in effect, see? They say they can do it but they're really not technically up to it.</p><p> Simon was dumbstruck by the comment, which seemed so condescending, but he also candidly admits he let it color his thinking.</p><p> In fact, when he moved on to another musician and tried to record guitarist Ray Phiri, Simon was so wary of Phiri's skills that he discouraged Phiri from doing anything fancy.</p><p> Let's just try and get the basic trackdown, he said whenever Phiri suggested an idea.</p><p> They did it straightforwardly but Phiri kept asking to dooverdubs.</p><p> Simon relented and Phiri dazzled him with his technical skills and artistic ideas; heimmediately recognized Phiri for a world class talent. (Phiri still tours and records with Simon to thisday.) Simon then understood anew how poisonous prejudice can be and how he'd been momentarilysucked into believing something negative about the musicians he'd traveled the globe to work with.</p><p> The core of those 12 days of jamming and recording were taken back to the US and transformed over time into the music of Graceland .</p><p> Despite knowing he'd broken the boycott and ignored Belafonte's advice, Simon was astonished by the controversy that ensued when the album was released.</p><p> He was justtrying to create some music and couldn't everyone see how terrific it had turned out? It wasn't as if Simon had played the segregated crowds of Sun City and cashed a big check, for god's sake.</p><p> He had asked permission of the musicians of South Africa, received it, paid them triple the studio ratesthat musicians received in New York City and shared more songwriting credits than he'd ever donebefore in his entire life. (Five of twelve songs featured co-credits.) He toured around the globe with thosevery same artists and introduced their talents to the world, especially Ladysmith Black Mambazo.</p><p> Heck,his tour even (wisely) included Hugh Masekala and Miriam Makeba, two exiles from South Africa whohad been shining a spotlight on the cruelty of apartheid for decades.</p><p> Their anti-apartheid credentialsplaced them on the same level as Nelson Mandela as far as international visibility was concerned.</p><p> If theysupported Simon, how could anyone object?
