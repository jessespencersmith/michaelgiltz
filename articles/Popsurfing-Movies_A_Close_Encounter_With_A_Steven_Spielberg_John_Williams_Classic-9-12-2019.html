(I'm counting his upcoming remake of West Side Story .) But one of them is unique. 1977's Close Encounters of The Third Kind is the only film solely written and directed by Spielberg and Spielberg alone.</p><p> And if he'd made it after he was married and had kids, Spielberg says he would never have ended it quite the way he did.</p><p> Thank God he wasn't a dad yet.</p><p> CET3K is disturbing, obsessive, mildly bewildering, adult and startlingly spare and abrupt.</p><p> It's also awe- inspiring and very convincing in delivering the close encounter with aliens the title promises.</p><p> This movie is far less of a thrill ride thanRaiders and not remotely the heart-tugger of E.T.</p><p> It's longer than you remember, stranger than you think, more upsetting than you expectand not nearly so joyous as you want.</p><p> In it, a man goes nearly insane, destroys his home life and then knowingly and intentionally abandons his family, probably forever.</p><p> Yet in 1977 it saved Columbia Picturesand became that studio's biggest hit of all time.</p><p> I have a friend who was traumatized by it when her parents took her to the film at a far-too-young age.</p><p> In many ways, Jaws is a lot less scary.</p><p> In Jaws , the danger is a monster you can understand -- a beast of the deep doing what it was made to do: swim and eat.</p><p> In CET3K , the monster is within: it's the madness in your brain, the once-loving father in your home.</p><p> It is Spielberg's most personal film.</p><p> And in the same year composer John Williams revived the film score with the all-time blockbuster, Wagnerian triumph that was the music of Star Wars (an even bigger hit at the box office), he also deliveredMICHAEL GILTZ AT WORK Michael Giltz is a freelance writer based in NYC and can be reached atmgiltz@pipeline.com FAVORITE LINKS Americablog Five O'Clock Lightning baseball blogDeep Pop -- Lori Lakin's Blog The Back Page -- Jason Page on ESPN Radio Cine-Blog -- George Robinson's Blog Documents On Art & Cinema - They unsettle you.</p><p> The choral work of Musica Sacra wasespecially striking since I barely realized there WAS any vocal work in the score the previous five or ten times I've seen the film.</p><p> Heck, I barely remembered anything about the score except for the climactic scene where first contact is initiated with a five note "hello" played by humans and repeated by alien craft.</p><p> That back-and-forth game of Simon Says was captured to perfection by the electronic toySimon (introduced in 1978).</p><p> In my mind, I remembered the entire finale as just a synth-fest of some sort.</p><p> Far from it.</p><p> The philharmonic had long passages throughout the first half of the film where they were at rest.</p><p> CET3K isn't the marathon of a performance that Star Wars proved in their triumphant rendition of that score.</p><p> When intermission ended and they stretched out with somefull-bodied themes before the film proper started up again, I thoughtit a welcome chance to let them strut their stuff since the score was more discreet than usual for Williams.</p><p> Well, it turns out the beautifully paced climactic scene features the full orchestra.</p><p> My memory of asynth-fest in fact begins with a woodwind (I think!) duetting with atuba.</p><p> The small voice of humanity and the booming sound of aliensare actually playful and warm and soon more and more instruments join in.</p><p> It's positively delightful and a release to hear the melody hinted at throughout the film receiving its due.</p><p> Earlier in the movie, a little boy is abducted by aliens. (It's the scene you see in the trailer where the child is opening a door while a fiery but enticing red light comes bursting in.</p><p> Spielberg says that may bethe defining image of his career and I'd agree.) It's scary but becausethe little boy isn't really frightened at any point, we instinctively knowthe aliens won't hurt him.</p><p> Little children and puppies are invariably good predictors of danger in movies: if they look nervous or growl, you should be nervous too.</p><p> In the same way, the score that unnerves us throughout, the five note melody that pops up here and there but never gets resolved in a musical fashion finally bursts out at the finale.</p><p> The orchestral interplayduring that triumphant meeting of humanity and alien life isoverwhelming and heart-stopping.</p><p> But because it's told through thevoice of the orchestra rather than synthesizers (as I always imagined) the audience knows this is a conversation, a dialogue.</p><p> The tuba makes you smile.</p><p> Yes, the interplay is frantic and a little confusing, but it'smessy and human (or should I say humane?), a meeting of mindsrather than a clashing of cultures.</p><p> And Williams told us all that with his score alone.</p><p> At the end, conductor Richard Kaufman pointed out individual musicians and sections of the philharmonic one by one, calling onthem to take a bow.</p><p> But of course it was John Williams who stoodtallest.</p><p> He wrote and experimented with hundreds of different fivenote themes, searching for the perfect combination that would satisfy Spielberg and allow Williams to undergird two hours of film with music that never called undue attention to itself.</p><p> He succeeded.</p><p> Again.The result is one of his most innovative and least appreciated scores,just as
