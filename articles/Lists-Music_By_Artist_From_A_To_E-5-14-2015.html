For the ﬁrst and only time, an MTV veejay made a recommendation and I listened and believed.</p><p> More cheerleader than anything else, I don't recall veejays expressing anything other than generic excitement about each and every video they played and each and every artist they interviewed.</p><p> But Triple J, one of the ﬁve original on-air personalities at that channel, slipped up just once.</p><p> He played the exceptionally catchy song (and striking video) "Sweet Dreams (Are Made Of This)." The relentless chorus, the stunning Annie Lennox dressed like a man and sporting that positively shocking red hair , the synths! It was awesome, it was catchy , it was...gimmicky? The lyrics were a bit repetitive; they worked but they didn't necessarily inspire conﬁdence in the songwriters.</p><p> Maybe they just got lucky .</p><p> The song was a great record, sure.</p><p> But I wondered -- were they a one-hit wonder , like A Flock Of Seagulls and a million other acts since pop music began? Back when you forked over a precious $8 just to buy ONE album on cassette, that was a serious question.</p><p> Should I buy it? As if he read my mind, Mr.</p><p> Jackson said something like, "Hey , that's a terriﬁc song.</p><p> And if you're wondering, the rest of their album is just as good!" I think management must have sent him a memo because I never heard J.J. or anyone else of fering anything like that comment ever again.</p><p> The closest they ever got to criticism after that -- good or bad -- was Beavis and Butt-Head.</p><p> Heh-heh.</p><p> But I heard him, I believed him, I bought the album and indeed, it WAS terriﬁc.</p><p> As was their quick follow-up "T ouch." They remained an excellent singles band after that remarkable, distinctive one-two punch.</p><p> And if you trim the too-generous, 18 track "Greatest Hits" set from 1991, why you'd have another four -star album to their credit. (For the record, I'd cut "The Miracle of Love" and both tracks from "W e Too Are One.")
