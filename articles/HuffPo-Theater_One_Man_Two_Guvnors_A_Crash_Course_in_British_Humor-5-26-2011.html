spot the royalnewlyweds returning gifts at Harrods, check out OneMan, Two Guvnors at the National.</p><p> It's a crash course in very British humor that will teach you as much about post-war Britain as a hundred walking tours.</p><p> You may not be convulsed with laughter at every double entendreand every single bad pun, but the people around you willbe having such a grand time you'll be certain to enjoyyourself and learn something about the Brits at the sametime.</p><p> Carlo Goldoni 's durable comedy The Servant Of Two Masters has been modernized and plopped down in Brighton in 1963.</p><p> Our hero -- James Corden of The History Boys -- is desperately hungry and decides to work for two guvnors in the hopes of being paid quicker and finally, finally getting to have a proper meal.</p><p> Little does he know, those two masters are both in disguise and in love.</p><p> One is a woman disguised as hergay, homicidal, criminal and deceased almost-twin brother.</p><p> The other is her one bones and you've got the makings of low-down farce.</p><p> Nicholas Hytner directs a show adapted by Richard Bean that gets all the little things right (except for theposter, which does nothing to convey this comedy's silly, breezy nature).</p><p> A skiffle band performs tunesbefore each act and during set changes, joined at times by actors from the cast who provide percussion byslapping on their bare chests, honk on car horns or whiz away on the xylophone.</p><p> Yes, it's that kind ofshow, a revival of the music hall spirit where everyone has a go, the actors joke with the audience andeven drag one or two people up onstage for a bit of business.</p><p> The set by Mark Thompson has just theright clever feel, giving the grand Lyttleton a more rinky-dink atmosphere.</p><p> The songs, by Grant Olding, fit in quite nicely, even as the band switches from skiffle to pure rock and roll and the cast joins in on the finale.</p><p> What kept this from being delirious fun for me was a cast split about evenly down the middle between those who are performing this silliness and those who feel it deep in their bones.</p><p> Most of the women farepoorly.</p><p> Suzie Toase is the lone exception as the saucy accountant who can deliver silliness about a worldwhere petrol stations have three times as many loos for women as for men and garner applause.</p><p> JemimaRooper was less vivid as the cross-dressing Rachel Crabbe and Claire Lams made no impression as Pauline, the girl in love with her hammy suitor.</p><p> Fred Ridgeway does no better as Pauline's father and Trevor Laird has so little to do other than move along the plot it wouldn't be right to say he succeeds orfails.</p><p> Don't despair: those actors don't drag the show down; they just don't lift it up to the comic heights reached by the rest.</p><p> Daniel Rigby is quite fun as Alan Dangle, the thespian who loves Pauline almost asmuch as he loves himself and wants everyone to know it.</p><p> Tom Edden is a priceless addition as adoddering waiter, added to the famous set piece where our hero Francis Henshall tries to deliver meals toboth of his guvnors in separate rooms while nibbling away himself.</p><p> Edden falls down the stairs so manytimes you're certain it will stop being funny.</p><p> And yet it never does.</p><p> But for me, the show's genuine find isOliver Chris as the silly blueblood Stanley Stubbers.</p><p> Chris captures the tone of this sort of comedy with ease, landing virtually every single line (whether it's a clever retort or just a "Hmm") with panache.</p><p> Front and center of it all is James Corden as Francis.</p><p> He's working a little harder than Chris (who makes it all seem so easy) but his warmth and easy rapport wins over the audience from the start.</p><p> Pratfalls, lies,beating himself up (literally) and desire for food battling with desire for sex -- Corden does it all.</p><p> Agrowing force in television -- where he's written numerous sketches and comedy bits, not to mention co-creating the sitcom Gavin & Stacey -- Corden has found the perfect role for his talents.</p><p> And I should point out that I'm almost entirely alone in finding even the slightest bit of fault with this show.</p><p> TheBritish press raved over it and the audience I saw it with was convulsed with laughter from start tofinish.</p><p> The show goes on tour after its limited engagement, but is certain to find a home in the West End after that.</p><p> If you can't make it to London, you can check it out anyway by catching the live broadcast of a
