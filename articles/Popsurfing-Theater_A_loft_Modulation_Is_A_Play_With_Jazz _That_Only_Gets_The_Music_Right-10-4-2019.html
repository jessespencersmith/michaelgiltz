FRIDAY, OCTOBER 04, 2019 THEATER: "A(loft) Modulation" -- A Play With Jazz Only Gets The Music Right (A)LOFT MODULATION * 1/2 out of **** THE A.R.T./ NEW YORK THEATRES When you hear about it, you can't help heading to Sixth Avenue between 28th and 29th Street in NYC.</p><p> In the mid-1950s, acclaimedphotographer W.</p><p> Eugene Smith left a sweet gig at Life magazine, moved into a loft at that address and got to work.</p><p> One neighbor was a teacher at Julliard whose own loft became a de facto jam session and rehearsal space for a rotating cast of jazz musicians including the great(Thelonius Monk, Charles Mingus, Bill Evans) and the never-was(drummer Ronnie Free).</p><p> Painters like Salvador Dali also swung by.</p><p> And Smith documented it all.</p><p> He relentlessly took photographs from 1957-1965.</p><p> Of course he captured the jazz greats.</p><p> But Smith alsopeeked out his window, meticulously (obsessively?) taking photos ofthe street life below.</p><p> As if that weren't enough, he wired the wholedamn building for sound, recording everything from the jazz musicians to snatches of conversation to late-night bull sessions about the nature of art.</p><p> The result? Some 40,000 images and more than 4,500 hours ofaudio tape.</p><p> When writer Sam Stephenson stumbled on this mother-load, he became just as obsessed as Smith, spending years archiving and organizing all this material.</p><p> The result? A book, a documentary film, aradio series on NPR (what the kids today would think of as a"podcast") and a traveling exhibition.</p><p> And now, unfortunately, a play.</p><p> Jaymes Jorsling saw this ferment of artistic creation and thwarted dreams, this cross-pollination of jazzand photography and art (and literature -- Norman Mailer dropped in,too) and wanted to capture it onstage.MICHAEL GILTZ to it.</p><p> Certainly, the sight of a man listening to old audiotapes is inherently undramatic, no matter how often his wife stops to ask exactly what the hell he's doing and why didhe quit his job.</p><p> But while none of the stories are worth hearing moreabout, it's certainly preferable to the wide-angle approach taken in (A)loft where everything simply happens all at once.</p><p> It's a credit to the rich vein of voyeurism and artistry of The Loft Project that it's proven a source for worthwhile efforts on so manyplatforms from radio to books to a documentary film.</p><p> Perhaps only theimpossibility of getting music clearances has stopped it from producing a boxed set of jazz music.</p><p> Sure, you can tell any story in any way you want.</p><p> But a play seems especially unlikely for this particulartale and that instinctive response is borne out here.</p><p> The jumble of aset, the murky lighting, the stabs at humanity from a cast burdened by clichés, the way the past
