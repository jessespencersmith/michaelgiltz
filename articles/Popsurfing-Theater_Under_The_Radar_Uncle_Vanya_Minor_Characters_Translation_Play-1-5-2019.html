The New Saloon theater company came up with a clever stunt of a show: find every English translation of the classic Chekhov play Uncle Vanya (including their own hilariously literal version via Google Translate), mash them up, have multiple actors tackle the same roles (often at the same time) and see what happens.</p><p> Quite a lot, as it turns out.</p><p> Any production of Chekhov that doesn'tbore is to be appreciated.</p><p> At just 90 minutes and with a strong dollopof humor, Minor Character does quite a bit more.</p><p> If you don't know the story, don't worry.</p><p> Despite the on-stage chaos --sometimes three versions of various characters like Vanya and Yelenaand Sonya jostle for space in a scene -- the basic story comes acrossquite nicely.</p><p> The cantankerous, grumpy, irascible Professor is visiting the country estate that supplies the funds to keep him living well.</p><p> At his side is the professor's notably younger and more beautiful wife,Yelena.</p><p> Servants are underfoot but the estate is overseen with tireless diligence by Sonya (the professor's daughter from his late wife) and the dead woman's brother, Vanya.</p><p> Also dropping in is the Doctor; he is drawn to Yelena while meek, mild, mouse-like Sonya has worshipped the manfrom afar.</p><p> Little happens and little changes (it is Chekhov, after all),but done well the play can be devastating and sad.</p><p> Like the actors, those multiple translations bump into one another throughout the show.</p><p> At times, a single character might run throughtwo or three variations of a
