Anyone tired of A Christmas Carol on stage or A Christmas Story marathon on TV has a new option.</p><p> Actor Ken Jennings is at the intimate black box space at The Sheen Center to share the Gospel of John.</p><p> He offers up the entire gospel in a casual, confiding tone and it doesn't get more Christmas-y than that.</p><p> Unless of course you want to hear the Nativity Story, in which case you probably want the Gospel of Luke (or Matthew).</p><p> For many years, actor Alec McCowen performed the Gospel of Luke as a one-man show.</p><p> He brought it thoroughly to life, painting various apostles as specific, unique people; drawing out the humor and compassion of Jesus and building to the awful and bloody climax.</p><p> Like John Gielgud with his Ages Of Man tribute to Shakespeare or Patrick Stewart with the Dickens' Carol, St.</p><p> Mark's Gospel was McCowen's annuity, a show he performed and returned to again and again.</p><p> When a member of the audience once asked McCowen where she could get a copy of the script, he said, "Any hotel room." It's a good joke but not an honest answer.</p><p> McCowen was faithful to the text but he shaped it into drama in ways large and small.</p><p> Ken Jennings does none of that.</p><p> This evening is described as "conceived and performed" by him, but I prefer to say he's sharing it.</p><p> It's not quite a recitation, but he certainly doesn't shape the gospel into drama.</p><p> Jennings most assuredly does not bring to life a host of characters.</p><p> He stands on a lovely wooden stage, whose edge has a wavy curve to it.</p><p> That platform stands a little off the ground.</p><p> Towards the back, stage right, is a small wooden bench he moves here and there.</p><p> A simple, rough-hewn cloth curtain hangs at the back, stretching down but never touching the stage. By deciding not to create a galaxy of characters, by not dramatizing the gospel, Jennings subtly places the emphasis not on the stories within but the message.</p><p> He memorized the Gospel of John during tough times and used it as a prayer.</p><p> That's precisely what his recitation feels like here, a straightforward, sincere act of communion both with the gospel and with the audience.</p><p> That's not to say Jennings is a mere reader.</p><p> His Gospel of John is surely theatrical, complete with lighting and sound effects.</p><p> I wish his performance had been just as committed to using every tool at his disposal.</p><p> Almost everyone but Jesus blurs into one another and usually they sound like angry, kvetchy characters.</p><p> Each outburst -- whether a pharisee or a woman at a well or an apostle that doubts Jesus has risen from the dead -- dims the impact of the one that follows.</p><p> That creates a wearying sameness to the many other voices found in the Gospel of John.</p><p> Worse, every once in a while Jennings will be speaking as Jesus when the sound design of M.</p><p> Florian Staab gives his voice an echo-y, God- like sound, just in case we don't quite
