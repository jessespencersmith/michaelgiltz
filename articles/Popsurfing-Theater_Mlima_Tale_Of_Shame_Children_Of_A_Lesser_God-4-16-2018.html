"My distance is my weapon," he says with pride and sadness mingled.</p><p> Soon we move from the elephant to the hunters tracking him and waiting for Mlima to die from the poison they've used.</p><p> And the hunters lead us to the corrupt chief of police who hired them and on to the public official in Kenya who wants to blame the death of Mlima onSomalis and on to the clerk at the port, the middleman, the artisthungry to carve those tusks and right on to the wealthy wife who wantsa showpiece for the entrance to her new home.</p><p> This La Ronde -style chain of provenance establishes the guilt of everyone involved in theslaughter of elephants -- however much they explain away theirpersonal choices.</p><p> Mlima lived a long life but he never stood a chance.</p><p> Ultimately, neither does the show.</p><p> It has style and presence and runs a nimble 80 minutes: unlike Nottage's last play -- the Pulitzer Prize-MICHAEL GILTZ AT WORK Michael Giltz is a freelance writer based in NYC and can be reached atmgiltz@pipeline.com FAVORITE LINKS Americablog Five O'Clock Lightning baseball blogDeep Pop -- Lori Lakin's Blog The Back Page -- Jason Page on ESPN Radio Cine-Blog -- George Robinson's Blog Documents On Art & Cinema - ended with Mlima's death, rather than begun with it.</p><p> CHILDREN OF A LESSER GOD ** 1/2 out of **** STUDIO 54/ROUNDABOUT THEATRE What a confusing, fascinating and frustrating play you'll find in the revival of Mark Medoff's one major success, the 1979 Tony winner Children of A Lesser God.</p><p> Somehow it manages to be both ahead of its time, sadly relevant and weirdly behind the times all at once.</p><p> Medoff and Hesper Anderson adapted and improved on the play for theOscar-winning feature film starring Marlee Matlin and William Hurt.</p><p> Both are about a young deaf woman who refuses on principle to talk vocally and the speech therapist she falls in love with.</p><p> Yet this is theoriginal play, replete with the debate in the Deaf community abouthow much to accommodate the hearing world, along with office andsexual politics that come across a lot more awkwardly than they must have in 1979.</p><p> It's cumbersome, generally well-acted, saddled with a dreadful set design and I doubt any of this matters.</p><p> Children Of ALesser God is more than a "problem play," but it still has problems and always will.</p><p> Happily, it also has two solid lead roles and anexcellent Lauren Ridloff and a solid Joshua Jackson do well by their characters.</p><p> James (Jackson) is a passionate, unconventional teacher who gets great results working with deaf kids who are learning to speak.</p><p> ButSarah (Ridloff) is older than his other students, a beautiful young woman.</p><p> And she has no interest in learning to speak.</p><p> She doesn't want to do anything she can't do well and since Sarah was born deaf,vocalizing will never be a strong suit for her.</p><p> Besides, why should shehave to learn to speak? Why can't hearing people learn to sign?James is struck by her beauty, challenged by her politics and engaged by her wit.</p><p> Instead of another dull classroom session, he invites her out to dinner and soon they are mixing business with pleasure. (Rather uncomfortably so, from our current perspective.) Soon Jamesis offering Sarah a life she never really imagined as a wife and perhapsmother, with James as her personal interpreter to the hearing world.</p><p> Her friend Orin (a passionate John McGinty) wants to agitate for more Deaf teachers and other changes at the school (or is it an institution?)where they reside.</p><p> Will Sarah forget her Deaf friends? Will James getin trouble with his boss Mr.</p><p> Franklin (Anthony Edwards) for fraternizing with a student, even if she is 21 years old? And deep down, is James always hoping and expecting he can convince Sarah to use her voice, to speak? And will that be a step forward for her...or astep back? (photo © by Matthew Murphy) It's very, very hard to "unpack" the issues of this play. (Dreadful word, that.) The debate in the Deaf community is sadly just as relevant today as it was almost 40 years ago.</p><p> Precious little progress has been madewith the Hearing community, though certain technological advances(the internet, subtitling) might count as practical if not cultural progress.</p><p> On the plus side, the question of assimilation, of what one gives up to be accepted will always be universal for marginalizedpeople.</p><p> Yet the minute you try and see the insight the show can offer,other pressing issues come to the fore, like the fact that James isquickly seducing a young woman he's supposed to be teaching andthus has authority over. (She lives and works at the school as a maid).</p><p> Worse, another student seems much younger, but James lets her drink beer and be sexually suggestive without truly addressing her needsappropriately.</p><p> His boss (Edwards) comes across vaguely when not being a jerk, though it's hard to know if he's just being blunt or intended as the nominal villain.</p><p> So you focus on the romance, but thenSarah is enraptured by a blender like any good little housewife andfeels reduced, along with women in general.</p><p> Her story of the sex shehad with numerous men when she was younger is hard to parse in this #MeToo era.</p><p> Is it more sexually liberated (which is how it plays here --Sarah owns her sexual pleasure) or is it abusive on the men's part? And the biggest problem of all is that the whole damn thing takes place in the mind of James.</p><p> He is reflecting back on the woman heloved and lost and hopes to get back.</p><p> That's all fine and a simple black box would have served us well.</p><p> Yet the dreadful scenic design by the deserved Tony winner Derek McLane is a disaster: for some reason,it's lit in moody blues and hot pink and looks like South Florida circaMiami Vice.</p><p> Instead of a vague no-man's land, it feels like a suburban mall and is impossible to put out of your mind.</p><p> Thus no one can just pop into James's office or home -- every character's entrance feels weighted with symbolism the way a naturalistic set or no set at allwould not have done.</p><p> The score by Branford Marsalis (a favorite artistof mine) is a modest plus but the pre-existing music that appears throughout -- from Stevie Wonder to Paul McCartney to classical pieces -- feels oddly random and lacking in impact.</p><p> Surely in a showabout sound and the lack thereof, any music that is played should havemajor import.</p><p> Not so here.</p><p> Worse, we're constantly adjusting our expectations about issues the play is raising versus the issues it had no clue would arise with futureaudiences.</p><p> What we want is to simply follow the romance but thatcan't happen.</p><p> Medoff keeps getting in his own way by piling on thebackstory.</p><p> Sarah is estranged from her mother, while her fatherabandoned the family over his daughter's deafness.</p><p> James has his own heavy tale, with a mother that committed suicide (!) and a father he hasn't spoken to in years.</p><p> Surely falling in love and navigating a newrelationship should be enough, without tossing in family woes, legalbattles and so much more.</p><p> On the bright side, sign language is simply a beautiful, expressive language and it's a pleasure to experience it.</p><p> I can't speak to Jackson'sfluidity in ASL, but he convinces as a thoroughly decent guy, if not thecompelling rebel suggested in the text.</p><p> Ridloff holds us from start tofinish with her expressive face, body and signing.</p><p> They are reason enough to see a play that makes their characters seem more confused than Medoff intended.</p><p> McGinty is also memorable as the rabble rouser Orin, though Treshelle Edmond is fuzzier as the student Lydia.</p><p> She comes across as so young (is she 12? 15? 18?) that it's hard to understand James's dealings with her as anything other than clueless and thoughtless.</p><p> Actually everyoneother than the leads and Orin are fuzzy, from the lawyer (a fine JuleeCerda) to Edwards in the thankless role of the administrator to KeciaLewis, who has little to do as Sarah's mother but be hurt and angry.</p><p> That is surely
