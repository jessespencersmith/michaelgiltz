buttons too readily that the show goes off the rails.</p><p> It's easy to disguise a person's prejudice with social niceties and then reveal with coded words and increasingly brutal language the prejudice that simmers underneath.</p><p> Disgraced doesn't do that.</p><p> Instead, our main character is set up quite believably as one sort of man shaped by his family and intellectual growth (not to mention the Western culture he lives in and fully embraces) only to have him do a 180 and prove to be the exact opposite of what he's been shown to be.Amir rejects his family's faith because of its ascetic who dons traditional garb and hangs out with guys in Starbucks who tell people the West got what it deserved.</p><p> And most confusingly and pointlessly of all, Emily's artistic breakthrough by drawing upon Persian imagery isrejected by Emily herself as naive.</p><p> Huh? This all explodes thanks to plot twists that pile onto each other in an especially awkward manner, each one less convincing than the last, or rather, less interesting.</p><p> There's nothing unconvincing about maritalinfidelity or having a co-worker leap you on the corporate ladder.</p><p> But none of it has any bearing on theweighty religious and cultural topics that Disgraced clumsily raises.</p><p> It certainly isn't the fault of the cast, which is fine throughout, with Karen Pittman especially good as that co-worker of Amir's, probablybecause she's the most internally consistent character on the stage and isn't asked to suddenly turn into someone entirely different.</p><p> Pittman also has an appealing warmth and intelligence.</p><p> It's only 90 minutes but Disgraced felt
