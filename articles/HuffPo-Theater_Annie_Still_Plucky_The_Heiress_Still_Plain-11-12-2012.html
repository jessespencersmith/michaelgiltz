Theater: Annie Still Plucky; The Heiress Still Plain ANNIE ** 1/2 out of **** THE HEIRESS ** out of **** ANNIE ** 1/2 out of **** PALACE THEATRE Annie has great timing.</p><p> I don't just mean the fact that this revival of the smash hit musical features a new president with a New Deal and a New York City on the skids but ready to bounce back with optimism and determination.</p><p> Sure, that makes this Annie feel very welcome.</p><p> But the even smarter timing is that it's opening on Broadway now and canestablish itself as a family-friendly hit before that UK import Matilda The Musical comes thundering in next March armed to the teeth with rave reviews, big box office and majorawards.</p><p> Both girls are scrappy fighters and both can survive on Broadway.</p><p> But having seenthis Annie , it's lucky to have some breathing room.</p><p> The story is familiar even though I've never seen the musical performed live or read thecomic strip.</p><p> Plucky little girl escapes depressing orphanage, befriends a dog named Sandy,wins the love of the richest man in the world and sings "Tomorrow." Perhaps the biggest shock to someone familiar with only the leaden feature film version is how many other terrific songs are packed into the show.</p><p> You've got the sweet "Maybe," the rousing "It's a Hard Knock Life," the show-stopper "Little Girls," "Easy Street," "You're Never Fully Dressed Without a Smile" AND "Tomorrow," plus a few others.</p><p> The songs by Charles Strouse and Martin Charnin have a Jerry Herman-esque by-god-you're-going-to- remember-me quality that is rare these days and they are sung well by most everyoneinvolved.</p><p> If you're headed to Annie , it's because of these songs, and no wonder.</p><p> An earlier revival was apparently rather woebegone but this show feels much more solid.The cast is generally strong and the two leads are very good.</p><p> It's in the varied techelements that this Annie fails to capitalize fully on its rock-solid virtues.</p><p> But don't blame the actors.</p><p> Lilla Crawford is a trooper as Annie, belting out her numbers with precisionwhile hitting her mark and keeping Sandy in his place. (The performance I saw, Sandyyawned during "Tomorrow" and thought he might wander off but Crawford kept him closewithout ever breaking character.) I've no idea if she'll make the transition to a full careerbut she certainly has the pipes and poise, sounding in this interview like a veteran.</p><p> April 29, 2014 This is the print preview: Back to normal view Â» Posted: 11/12/2012 6:30 pmEven better is Anthony Warlow as Daddy Warbucks.</p><p> He brings a warmth and emotional depth to both his character and his singing that gives Annie a genuine heart.</p><p> AnAustralian star, I'm sure we'll be seeing him again in other roles.</p><p> Katie Finneran has lessluck as Miss Hannigan.</p><p> She scored a triumph and a Tony for Promises, Promises but here she avoids the cartoonish over-the-top side of the villainess head of the orphanage andgoes for something more real and vulnerable in its way.</p><p> It's a reasonable choice and shecertainly nails many of her lines but without a real bad person to hiss at (we never thinkfor a moment that she'd actually harm Annie) the show has no danger.</p><p> That's certainly noton display via her brother Rooster (a fine but not scary Clarke Thorell).</p><p> The casting is not the problem.</p><p> Brynn O'Malley is a prim assistant to Warbucks but the potential romance between her and her employer barely registers.</p><p> And the gang oforphans is solid as well.</p><p> The little girls all dance and sing with aplomb; the Bowery Boysaccents are a little thick in the first scene but they soon settle down into reasonable roles.</p><p> No, the reason this Annie is merely pleasant instead of joyful is the creative team led by generally dependable director James Lapine.</p><p> To their credit, the show does not have a sickly sweet aura about it.</p><p> That is probably an eternal issue with this show; you can't haveFDR telling a little girl her attitude is exactly what the country needs right now withoutexpecting a little schmaltz.</p><p> But in general, the show has no momentum.</p><p> Without a MissHannigan to fear (or at least chew up the scenery) and with Daddy Warbucks warming upto Annie about two seconds after she arrives, there's essentially no story to tell.</p><p> Worse, the choreography by Andy Blankenbuehler has an anonymous quality about it.</p><p> In the big number "N.Y.C." the stage just feels crowded, not bustling with life as it should.</p><p> The costumes by Susan Hilferty are more spot-on, and she certainly doesn't mess with the iconic red dress towards the end.</p><p> But the scenic design by David Korins is another missed opportunity.</p><p> The orphanage "folds" out from one side of the stage like the page in a picture book.</p><p> That idea is carried over to the Warbucks mansion where a tour of the house involves "turning" part of the setlike turning pages in a book to go from room to room.</p><p> It might seem churlish to point out that Annie is a comic book and not a picture book so this conceit is out of place.</p><p> Okay, I'll accept the story has a fairy-tale aspect but this is about the only idea of the show.</p><p> And that mansion is an ugly, crowded busy set with stairs moving this way and that and fatpillars getting in the way.</p><p> When Annie first sees it she says it seems more like the lobby ofan hotel than a place people live.</p><p> She's meant to sound overwhelmed but in fact shesounds discerning.</p><p> It does look like the lobby of an hotel, albeit an ugly and unimpressiveone.</p><p> I hoped the mansion might transform itself and become warmer and prettier asAnnie's influence is felt but no such luck.</p><p> The Christmas tree of lights? Yep, that looksexactly like something a hotel lobby might feature when what a little girl wants is a realtree.</p><p> Happily, when the cast is belting out songs and Crawford and Warlow have center stage, you can ignore all that and focus on what is indeed a very impressive clutch of songs andtwo winning performers.</p><p> It may be a hard knock life next March for this show, but for themoment when they're singing their songs the faults of this production seem far lessimportant.</p><p> THE HEIRESS ** out of **** WALTER KERR THEATREWhen I heard about this revival of The Heiress starring actress Jessica Chastain, I immediately thought it was horrible casting.</p><p> That striking film actress, so good in movies like Tree of Life and Take Shelter, trying to embody the plain Jane known as Catherine Sloper? Absurd.</p><p> It turns out I was right but for the wrong reason.</p><p> This isn't MichellePfeiffer in Frankie & Johnny : Chastain can indeed frump herself down and at least "feel" unattractive (a very frizzy wig helps quite a bit).</p><p> Unfortunately, Chastain simply doesn'thave the vocal chops for Broadway yet, over-emphasizing her lines or delivering them intoo broad a manner that hammers home the laughs or misses the mark entirely at times.She has some confident moments, especially when simply reacting to what is said (not asurprise for someone who has flourished on film) but it will take more experience on herpart to determine whether her talents are suited to the stage.</p><p> And without that burningcentral performance, The Heiress has almost no reason for being.</p><p> Inspired by the Henry James novel, this play involves the timid, shy Catherine, thedaughter of a widowed doctor (David Strathairn) who constantly compares his child to thebeloved wife that died while giving birth.</p><p> Needless to say, the child is found lacking.</p><p> Shelongs for her father's approval and isn't surprised that no one else ever pays attention toher.</p><p> That makes the arrival of the handsome and seemingly impulsive Morris Townsend (apleasant Dan Stevens) all the more shocking.</p><p> He is quite the most eligible bachelor around(if you don't mind penniless young men with pretty faces), so his whirlwind wooing ofCatherine fools no one but the poor creature herself.</p><p> Her Aunt Lavinia (a very good Judith Ivey) suspects Morris is enraptured with Catherine's wealth as much as anything else, but like the other practical women in the play, so what?He might enjoy her money and make Catherine happy at the same time, so where's the harm? But her father is too pitiless in his attitude towards her: He doesn't love her, so no one could love her, and this fortune hunter isn't going to make a fool of him.</p><p> I love the 1949 feature film version starring Olivia de Havilland, Ralph Richardson and Montgomery Clift.</p><p> It's one of the all-time greats and improves in subtle ways even on the stage play.</p><p> Comparing it to this production is instructive.</p><p> De Havilland is a rather plain-looking movie star at the best of times so it's very easy for her to inhabit the mousyCatherine.</p><p> Chastain can't be that plain but this adds a complexity they might have minedfurther.</p><p> If Catherine isn't that plain, it makes the idea a young man might be attracted toher just for herself more plausible. (Catherine does exhibit smarts and an appealingbluntness at times.) Richardson is the definitive father, bitingly sarcastic and almost cruelin his treatment of his daughter; it's like watching a man kick a puppy.</p><p> Strathairn is verydifferent, choosing to shade his take on the doctor.</p><p> He's rather old and infirm from thestart of the show and his clumsy comments are wounding but you can almost think he'strying to help.</p><p> And I pity any actor who has to be compared to Clift.</p><p> When you're matchedup against one of the most beautiful actors of all time, you're going to come up short.(Leonardo Di Caprio in Titanic or Alain Delon in Purple Noon might not suffer; few else.) Stevens needless to say is not the Byronic beauty that was Clift.</p><p> But again, this offeredopportunity.</p><p> Might Morris just be a hapless, well-intentioned fellow? Sure he likes hermoney but his lack of work might just be because he's ill-suited to it and perhaps what he's suited to most is enjoying Catherine's wealth and keeping her company.</p><p> Better that than she live alone, no? Unfortunately, no new insight into Morris is offered; he seems like a straightforward cad looking to dupe a girl and get her money.</p><p> All of these shadings and possibilities fall by the wayside, I'm afraid, with Chastain emphasizing her lines and losing all subtlety time and again.</p><p> She's done stage work before and might blossom with time (or even by the end of the run, perhaps) but at this moment seems to be taking the broadest choices possible.</p><p> She's certainly not bad or embarrassing, and it's no reflection on her skills in the movies.</p><p> Ivey shows her and everyone else how it's done as the silly but somehow sensible Aunt Lavinia.</p><p> The costumes by Albert Wolsky are strong, the hair and wig designs by Paul Huntley work wonders especially with Chastain and the set by Derek McLane is handsome.</p><p> The staircase rises up inevitably to the second floor landing for that dramatic finale, but unfortunately the drama doesn't rise with it.</p><p> THE THEATER SEASON 2012-2013 (on a four star scale) As You Like it (Shakespeare in the Park withLily Rabe) **** Chimichangas And Zoloft * Closer Than Ever *** Cock ** 1/2 Harvey with Jim Parsons * My Children!
