First Run) -- It took me a few weeks to catch up with this documentary about five dancers in the Russian Kirov (aka Mariinsky).</p><p> The five women range in age and disciplines, but their talent and extreme hard work link them completely.</p><p> You see rehearsals and exercise and pain and talk with them while preparing for performances of crowd-pleasers like Swan Lake and Romeo & Juliet .</p><p> But even those warhorses are incredibly demanding.</p><p> Obviously, dance lovers will love this, but it's a testament to the sweat that goes into any artistic endeavor worth the name and anyone can appreciate it.</p><p> Also out is the charming Carmen & Geoffrey ($24.95; First Run), which captures the remarkable love story and artistic accomplishments of dancers Carmen de Lavallade and Geoffrey Holder.</p><p> You'll appreciate my ignorance of the world of dance when I tell you I only knew Holder as the guy from those 7-Up ads and the James Bond film Live and Let Die.</p><p> Race To Witch Mountain ($39.99 Special Edition and $44.99 BluRay; Disney) -- Okay, before I bemoan the changes made to Escape To Witch Mountain (what, kids think the word "escape" is too dull so they changed it to "race") not to mention the inclusion of The Rock, let's be clear.</p><p> I had fond thoughts for the original Seventies kid flick but it was pretty tame, even at the time.</p><p> So Race To Witch Mountain doesn't soil a beloved classic, it just takes a high concept idea and revamps it for today.</p><p> No harm, no foul.</p><p> But no good movie, either.</p><p> The BluRay costs $5 more than the SpecialEdition but only $3 more on sale at Amazon ($25.99 versus $22.99).</p><p> But the single disc edition costs only $15.99 on sale, which is a very substantial difference.</p><p> It's nice to see BluRay and SpecialEdition DVDs coming closer in price, but until they're regularly under $20, fans won't bother witheither.</p><p> The BluRay does contain a regular DVD along with the digital copy that also comes with the Special Edition.</p><p> Is all that confusing? For $26 instead of $16, you can get a lot of flexibility (a copy for the BluRay player, a regular DVD for the kid's room and road trips and a digital copy to carryon a laptop or other device).</p><p> But that's a lot of money for a little convenience.</p><p> Kudos to them forreleasing all three versions at the same time, but cut out all the bonus features or keep them and lower the price and drop the stripped down one-disc version and send those only to video stores and Netflix.</p><p> The array of choices is confusing and wasteful and tiresome and BluRay is about to go under for good if it hasn't already as an option.</p><p> Flight of the Conchords Second Season ($29.98; HBO) -- During the first season of this show about a New Zealand duo looking for pop success in the US, I often fast-forwarded to the always delightful musical numbers.</p><p> But slowly the show is growing on me or the guys are justfinding their rhythm.</p><p> In either case, it's become a quiet charmer...even when they're not singing.</p><p> The Soloist ($29.98; DreamWorks) -- Oscar bait, but no one was nibbling.</p><p> The TV movie-ready story of a down on his luck newspaper reporter (Robert Downey Jr) helping out a talentedhomeless man (Jamie Foxx) with a gift for classical music) is pure mawkishness that two fine actors can't overcome.</p><p> The Shape of the World ($59.99; Athena) -- This six-part PBS miniseries turns the science of cartography -- map-making -- into an exploration of the globe.</p><p> Narrated by Patrick Stewart, it does have the air of a lecture, but a very good lecture indeed.</p><p> Teens might think they won't be interested,but they will be.</p><p> It's often damning with faint praise to say a series can inform and educate you, but that surely was the goal here and they achieved it.</p><p> Project Runway Season 5 ($27.95; Genius) -- The most star-studded PR yet, with guests like Sandra Bernhard, LL Cool J, Brooke Shields and Natalie Portman popping in alongside Tim Gunn and Heidi Klum.</p><p> The men were completely shut out of the female-dominated final three and rightlyso.</p><p> And while a reality series might be the wrong place to make this argument, why do they wait till right before the beginning of the new season to release a show on DVD? In the UK, they release shows on DVD as soon as the series ends (sometimes, even while it's still airing) and thatpiggybacking on all the reviews and publicity seems smart to me.</p><p> Anyone who wants to catch up before the new season only has a blink of an eye to do it here. (That's really a pain when it comes to 24, for example.)
