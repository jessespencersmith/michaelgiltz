(Yes.) We see the family and friends in their orbit, all punctuated by the seasons and their work at the local cooperative vegetable garden.</p><p> It begins brilliantly with Gerri, a psychiatrist at the local clinic, talking with a tightly wound Imelda Staunton.</p><p> Staunton simply wants some sleeping pills because she hasn't slept well in weeks or even months.</p><p> Gerri gently probes to find out what the real trouble might be. ("On a scale of 1 to 10, how would you rate your happiness?" asks Gerri. "One," says Staunton brusquely. "Well, there's a bit of room for improvement there," observes Gerri.) Staunton's character is so vivid, so wounded and yet defensive that we spend the rest of the movie anticipating her return -- if Judi Dench can win an Oscar for a brief appearance as the Queen in Shakespeare in Love, Staunton can win for this turn.</p><p> As my friend Stephen pointed out, in a brilliant bit of writing, we see Gerri go home.</p><p> When her husband asks about her day and she launches into an anecdote, we immediately expect her to talk aboutStaunton.</p><p> When she doesn't (Gerri talks about another patient, an alcoholic teacher who showed up drunk), it lets us know that desperately unhappy people aren't so scarce on the ground.</p><p> Indeed, the film is filled with them: their lonely but stable son, then there's the brother of Tom who has lost his wife andbarely knows what to do with himself, an old college pal who is single and grossly overweight and stuffing himself with potato chips and beer plus many others as well.</p><p> But above all there's Leslie Manville -- a Leigh regular -- who plays a co-worker of Gerri's and slowly becomes the heart of the film.</p><p> She's amess, kindly indulged by Gerri and Tom.</p><p> Divorced, her big plan is to buy a car, which becomes a cock- up: it breaks down, she gets lost, piles up ticket after ticket and so on.</p><p> She's painfully eager in conversation, awkwardly attracted to their son (who is 10 years her junior and thinks of her as an auntand certainly not a love interest) and just desperate, really.</p><p> Quietly, masterfully, the film reveals itself:everyone's life is messy and difficult at times, but some people look around and see their friends with homes and careers and children and just know, just know that life is passing them by.</p><p> They're slightly dazed by the thought and wondering exactly what they did wrong.</p><p> All these characters are so vivid, it's a while before you realize who is taking center stage, since any one of them could.</p><p> Leigh's improvisational approach to building the script means scene after scene flows naturally, with hilariously awkward moments following delicately sad ones.</p><p> And it ends just beautifully, with the sound dropping out and theaudience looking compassionately at the tense and confused face of Manville's character.</p><p> If you're not looking at yourself at that point and wondering where your life went wrong, you should just thank your lucky stars.</p><p> YOU WILL MEET A TALL DARK STRANGER ** out of **** -- Is it worth seeing? No.</p><p> At this stage, as mediocre film follows mediocre film and the bad outnumber the good and threaten his reputation (which deserves to remain high, no matter how many bad films he makes), I think most fans just want toknow if seeing the new Woody Allen movie will diminish him in their eyes just a tad more or keep his integrity undimmed.</p><p> This comedy is too innocuous to tip the scales either way, but if it isn't a net positive like Match Point , why bother? Why cloud your memory of Annie Hall and Manhattan and Husbands & Wives and Hannah And Her Sisters and The Purple Rose Of Cairo and his other great works from the 70s and 80s with more of the same, only less? Tall Dark Stranger certainly won't surprise you in the least.</p><p> Josh Brolin and Naomi Watts are an unhappily married couple; he's a frustrated novelist (onesuccess, multiple failures) who has a wandering eye; she's an assistant in an art gallery whose sexy new boss (Antonio Banderas) isn't being nearly forward enough.</p><p> Anthony Hopkins is her dad, saddling himself with a dim-witted prostitute turned girlfriend. Well, it's "the Mexican cannibal movie" as everyone describes it, a serious-looking film about a family whose father has suddenly died.</p><p> Unfortunately, dad's the one who always procures the people for them to eat and without him around, the two teenage sons need to step it up.</p><p> Everyone has a slightly tired look about them (apparently flesh isn't the most well-rounded of diets) and is edgy and unexpectedly violent.</p><p> The mother resented thefather for always dabbling with whores, the sister and younger brother seem to have some sexual tensiongoing on and older brother Alfredo (a memorable Francisco Barreiro) may be more bothered about possibly being gay than being a cannibal.</p><p> The obvious comparison is Let The Right One In and it's a good one: Somos Lo Que Hay isn't that accomplished but it takes its subject very seriously, even as the film escalates into some blackly humorous scenes when the family members must awkwardly subdue one of their victims. Their home is wonderfully oppressive(with all the windows blocked and the light a sickly brown) as is every detail overseen by director JorgeMichel Grau.</p><p> It's probably not violent or "out there" enough for midnight movies nor is it quite successful enough as a drama for arthouse audiences.</p><p> But Somos Lo Que Hay is certainly an impressive, distinctive debut.</p><p> BELLE EPINE * 1/2 out of **** -- Hmm, a French coming of age tale crossed with a Roger Corman exploitation flick? Count me in.</p><p> Belle Epine doesn't live up to the promise of that build-up unfortunately as it details a teenage girl mourning the death of her mother just days ago.</p><p> She takes to shoplifting and then starts hanging out with the out of control youth who risk their lives racing motorcycles on an abandoned strip of asphalt every weekend.</p><p> Our heroine is quite adrift, since her dad is in Canada dealing with his wife's effects and seemingly paralyzed by grief while her older sister can't bear to even be in thehome.</p><p> So she's all alone, which means wild parties with the bikers, one of whom happily takes her up on the offer of sex.</p><p> It becomes quite silly, eventually, with the biking and "dangerous" scenes played at a music video level that undercuts the solid performances all around.</p><p> The two genres don't play off eachother or give each other a fresh spin; they awkwardly coincide and never truly cross-fertilize.</p><p> Thatintriguing description at the beginning proved too good to be true, just like the idea that foolishly risking your life or offering yourself up to strangers could make you feel better.</p><p> LES AMOURS IMAGINAIRES/HEARTBEATS ** out of **** -- One of my most highly anticipated films of the fest is both a mild disappointment and a step forward for its director, Xavier Dolan.</p><p> He had a smashing debut last year at Cannes with the blackly humorous drama I Killed My Mother.</p><p> It won three top prizes at the Directors Fortnight and was one of the major hits of the fest. (It went on to acclaim in Canada and festivals around the world.) This year, his second film played Un Certain Regard, which is where young filmmakers go on their way up and elder statesmen go on their way down.</p><p> At this rate,Dolan will be in Competition with a film in the next few years.</p><p> Let's certainly hope so.</p><p> His new movie,which has the godawful English title Heartbeats when The Imaginary Lovers is far better, is a bisexual Jules & Jim with a dash of Wong Kar Wai thrown in for good measure.</p><p> The movie is interspersed with people talking about their doomed romances in faux documentary style, with an Almodovar-esque beauty(the actress has a wonderfully angular face) especially memorable.</p><p> Then we meet best friends Francis (Dolan) and Marie (Monia Chokri).</p><p> He's gay and makes little slashes on his bathroom wall dappled deer, in wanders Nicolas (Niels Schneider), a tall curly-haired Adonis who loves ingenuously flirting with both of them.</p><p> He shares a bed with the two friends -- innocently of course -- and is then surprised when they both developcrushes.</p><p> The film is a tug of war between the two, with Nicolas either bemused or unaware of their desire.</p><p> Mostly it's an excuse for Dolan to develop his skills as a filmmaker.</p><p> I Killed My Mother was accomplished and had some nice framing of its characters but was by and large dialogue driven.</p><p> Les Amours Imaginaires is a huge leap forward stylistically with Dolan telling much of the story through visuals.</p><p> He goes a bit overboard, with some scenes playing like music videos, and his choice of classical music cues are far more cliched than his taste in pop.</p><p> But if he's to become a great filmmaker, these are exactly the sort of talents he needs to develop.</p><p> Dolan and Chokri are inherently appealing but I try to read as many books as possible.</p><p> Huh? the novels of Madame De Lafayette.</p><p> Rather than reading this one, I purchased her masterpiece The Princess Of Cleves.</p><p> Considered the first modern French novel (it dates from 1678), the novel demonstrates LaFayette's subtlety and focus on ethics and mores rather than heaving bosoms.</p><p> But this film of her other great novel, by director Bertrand Tavernier, has no subtlety or charm or wit.</p><p> It's flatly obvious as we follow a princess forced to marry one (rather jealous) man but in love with a dashingcompetitor and inspiring love in her spouse's older mentor.</p><p> Swords flash, with Christian wars as a backdrop for her passion.</p><p> We're told the Princess has rejected her lover and come to appreciate her husband (quite a winning fellow, actually) but then it's back to the lover and the film is too simple for usto know whether she genuinely changed her mind again and again or was dissimulating.</p><p> The same is truefor all the characters so instead of the devious, thrilling intrigue of Dangerous Liaisons, we just follow the surface action without ever really knowing or caring about who really loves whom.</p><p> ARMADILLO ** 1/2 out of **** -- A fine Danish documentary this follows a group of soldiers on a six month tour of Afghanistan.</p><p> The director's statement said the film was about the addiction of war.</p><p> Clearly, he came in wanting to understand why anyone would choose to go to a war zone, especially people asnominally involved in the politics of the US occupation of Afghanistan as young Danish men.</p><p> Happily, the film evinces no such agenda.</p><p> We follow the guys as they're tearfully hugged goodbye and then endure months of boredom.</p><p> Many scenes are depressingly familiar, with local Afghanis caught between soldierswho show up during the day and the Taliban who show up at night.</p><p> The men reinforce the idea ofsoldiering as frat boys with guns: they party, call each other gay, wrestle around, watch a lot of porn and do public service -- in this case serve their country with diligence.</p><p> It's only late in the film when we witness two firefights and the filmmakers are so clearly in danger that we become frightened for them.The rush of actually facing the enemy (the soldiers are worried they'll go home without being tested in battle) is clearly the key as far as the filmmaker is concerned.</p><p> Their actions on the field, which may be callous by civilian standards (they boast about their actions, joke about the enemy dead) seem perfectlyreasonable.</p><p> But in the film launched into whether anyone behaved inappropriately.</p><p> What's no surprise is that about half the men re-up.</p><p> I think it's not that momentary rush a la The Hurt Locker but more the camaraderie and sense of genuine purpose they have.</p><p> Perhaps most striking is the film's strong visuals under very difficult conditions.</p><p> The material is too similar to other documentaries From a banal examination of a compelling international issue we go to a wonderfully illuminating documentary about an especially complex topic.</p><p> Inside Job is unquestionably the most important film that will play at Cannes and hopefully can arouse people into forcing legal action to be taken against those who have so clearly brokenthe law and swindled the taxpayers and citizens out of literally hundreds of millions of dollars.</p><p> Inside Job is a relentless, accurate and highly entertaining look at the financial meltdown.</p><p> Director Charles Ferguson clearly maps out the origins of the crisis, how it could have been prevented, how it could have been lessened, where the bodies are buried and who buried them.</p><p> From Elliot Spitzer to a spokesman for thefinancial industry on Wall Street, from France's head of finance to guys living in tents in Florida,Ferguson went everywhere and spoke to everyone willing to talk.
