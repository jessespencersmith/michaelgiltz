Shepard's tale is bristling with tragic details -- Aron isalways crying, it seems -- but it's a joy to read.</p><p> One of the WarsawGhetto's tragic figures -- the child advocate Janusz Korczak -- figures prominently and beautifully, but like everyone else he is fully complex and contradictory and alive.</p><p> The tale builds and builds but this isn't a storyabout what happens to Aron.</p><p> It's richer, more satisfying than that.</p><p> You'llread it quickly and then want to read it again.</p><p> Unforgettable.</p><p> No serious reader I know cares about genre: is it mystery, sci-fi, romance, literary fiction, graphic novel, young adult, a chapter book, a biography, a history, poetry, play? Who cares? Is it good? That's all that matters.</p><p> The foolishness of labels has never been more apparent than in theastonishing career of M.T.</p><p> Anderson, who has produced a string ofsophisticated, witty works under the label of "young adult." That term has covered his dystopian sci-fi, horror, contemporary teen, historical fiction and now this work of biography.</p><p> Since "young adult" also encompassesthe masterpiece "Adventures Of Huckleberry Finn," as complex andinvolving a work as there is, he should happily embrace it.</p><p> Essentially, "young adult" means there is (probably) a young adult at the heart of the story.</p><p> And yet, "young adult" indicates one initial audience for this workand that may explain why it's so particularly good.</p><p> Anderson has wowed with "Feed" and his landmark two volume work "The Astonishing Life Of Octavio Nothing," fiction that captures the Revolutionary Era, shines afresh light on the horrors of slavery and presents our nation's founding from a novel perspective.</p><p> It's intensely detailed and utterly convincing.</p><p> So now we have a work of nonfiction about composer Dimitri Shostakovichand how he created his Seventh Symphony during the Siege ofLeningrad.</p><p> It's a delight; no surprise since Anderson has evinced an enthusiasm for music almost as joyful as Shostakovich's love of soccer.</p><p> What makes this book so good is that Anderson doesn't simplify the talefor its presumed audience.</p><p> But he does clarify the story in an elegant manner that any historian or biographer should envy.</p><p> With seeming ease, Anderson tells of Shostakovich's life, the unceasing terror of Stalin's reign, the invasion by Germany, the horrors of the siege, cannibalism,despair and the role of music and why it mattered so much.</p><p> He also deftly captures the moral complexity of Shostakovich's life, surrounded as he was by friends and family who were murdered by the state or turnedagainst him to protect themselves or depended on him for their survival.</p><p> A children's book? Hardly.</p><p> And definitely.</p><p> This isn't dumbed down history.Indeed, more traditional doorstops about history might now seem clogged up, overstuffed as they are with unnecessary dates and detail when Anderson -- with a vivid image and pointed sentence -- can makehis case with directness and verve.</p><p> It leaves you with one excitingquestion: what will Anderson do next?
