Tuesday, June 18, 2019  FICTION A Porpoise-Driven Life Mark Haddon’s New Novel Plays With Narrative, to Narrative’s Detriment  June 17, 2019 Michael Giltz In author Mark Haddon’s The Curious Incident of the Dog In The Night-Time, he found inspiration from an unreliable narrator.</p><p> In that case, it was a teenager living with Asperger’s Syndrome.</p><p> Through that character’s eyes, an unremarkable event became a mystery and the people around him became equally puzzling.</p><p> The novelproved a blockbuster, albeit overly praised.</p><p> Once you figured out the perspective of our narrator, once you “gothim,” the story lost interest.</p><p> I found the hit stage adaptation more satisfying.</p><p> Before that breakout, Haddon was a modestly successful writer of children’s books who dipped a toe into television.</p><p> After, he branched out into all sorts of areas.</p><p> He tackled Down’s Syndrome in a radio play and TVmovie, he wrote an adult novel revolving around mental health, he delivered short stories (some highlyacclaimed), and even wrote poetry.</p><p> Despite some good reviews here and there, it’s fair to say Curious Incidentwas looking unrepeatable.</p><p> Porpoisefully Retelling the Myth Not anymore.</p><p> The Porpoise plays with an ancient myth that inspired the play Pericles, Prince Of Tyre by William Shakespeare (and George Wilkins, or so the scholars say).</p><p> It’s one of those legends that proves more horrifying the more you actually pay attention.</p><p> In it, a King’s wife dies in childbirth, and he becomes obsessed with the daughter that survived.</p><p> The King wants her exclusively for himself but can’t say so aloud.</p><p> So he welcomes suitors once she comes of age.</p><p> But if they can’t solve the King’s riddle, he immediately executes them.</p><p> One suitor realizes the King’sunnatural desires, but of course can’t voice the truth, so he delays and then flees for his life.</p><p> Most retellingsfollow this bold adventurer as he dodges King-sent assassins, falls in love with another woman, and so on.</p><p> Haddon read this story and thought, hey, what about the women? His retelling doesn’t see the King’s daughter as a plot device but as a full-blooded person trapped in an almost unimaginable horror.</p><p> Haddon does the samefor the woman our “hero” falls for, finding her journey just as exciting as the man’s.</p><p> So The Porpoise is ready for the #MeToo era.</p><p> But is it good? Not quite.</p><p> Haddon needed something else to complicate the telling.</p><p> Instead of a uniquely unreliable protagonist, he devised a narrative structure that dartsback and forth through time.Author Mark HaddonThe Porpoise begins with a strand set in the modern era.</p><p> The King is now a wealthy billionaire, and his Queen a world-famous movie star.</p><p> When she dies in a plane crash but their unborn child survives, the billionaire keeps the girl cloistered away from the tabloid media and prying eyes.</p><p> Haddon is quite good at showing the father’slove slippery-sloping into obsession and then rape.</p><p> Come Sail Away Just as a “suitor” appears and bodyguards chase him off,the story jumps to ancient times, where the suitor is on aship fleeing a mad King, desperate for safe harbor.</p><p> Thisstrand is clean and uncomplicated in the telling as thesuitor’s next love interest takes center stage.</p><p> Heradventures include an accidental burial at sea, and she latersurvives and flourishes in a small town as a priestess.Meanwhile, their daughter grows up in a royal householdand finds herself targeted by a mad Queen jealous of thegirl being more popular than her own child.</p><p> And then the narrative jumps again, this time to Shakespeare’s era.</p><p> The Bard’s likely collaborator onPericles, Prince of Tyre was a vile innkeeper and pimpwho once beat a pregnant woman.</p><p> Here, that man, George Wilkins, is dying, surrounded by servants andhangers-on who clearly don’t give a toss for the bastard.</p><p> The ghost of Shakespeare appears and leads Wilkinstowards his destiny in the afterlife, an uneasy prospect at best for such a man.</p><p> Haddon is essentially retelling the tale two (or three) times and that allows him to offer up multiple endings that are variously righteous, sad, and happy.</p><p> While he handles the narrative structure capably, it gets in the way of asimpler, better book.</p><p> The modern setting, which focuses on the King and his imprisoned, abused daughter is fine but never quite catches the imagination.</p><p> Every time we left it, I was disappointed, not because it was so good but because I’dbeen holding out hope it would soon come alive.</p><p> The relatively brief passages set in Shakespeare’s time are both the best-written and the least necessary.</p><p> Standing on its own, this section would make a fine short story but it serves no purpose here.</p><p> But Haddon sets the bulk of The Porpoise in the classical era.</p><p> These sections boast a clean, uncomplicated voice.</p><p> He delivers the story from a modern perspective, giving the women their due, without lapsing into unduemoralizing.</p><p> It doesn’t rise to the heights of Madeline Miller’s Circe or The Song Of Achilles, but, on its own, itwouldn’t have been too far off either.</p><p> Haddon will probably enjoy his biggest hit in years with The Porpoise.</p><p> But if he gets out of his own way and trusts his writing to stand on
