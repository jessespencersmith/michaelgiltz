Bull riding.</p><p> The PBR (Professional Bull Riders) is the group that oversees some 300 events in 5 countries, including the US, Canada, Mexico, Brazil and Australia. (London looks like a possibility for next season, but those five countries are stalwarts that produce top riders on the tour.) Last week, I went back stage at the final PBR event of the season before the finals in Las Vegas taking place over two weekends from October 31 through November 9.</p><p> Getting up close to the action lets youknow how crazily dangerous bull riding can be.</p><p> Talking with 19 year old Rookie of the Yearcontender Reese Cates -- who has blogged about his time on the tour for ESPN -- makes clear thatthe PBR has a whole crop of rising stars who are media savvy and ready to spread the fun of bullriding to a wider audience the way Jeff Gordon and others did for NASCAR.</p><p> Not convinced? "This is the most dangerous sport in the world," says Cates, whose dad and granddad rode bulls and whose mom competed in barrel riding (an event at rodeos). "You're competing against something that's ten, 15 times your size and he doesn't care about anything but getting you on the ground and doing his job." 2.</p><p> The bulls are stars, too -- Unlike the cruel sport of bullfighting in Spain -- recently featured on 60 Minutes -- the bulls are not harmed in the PBR.</p><p> In fact, watch it for five minutes and you'll realize that the bull is just about the only person not in danger.</p><p> Cowboys wear spurs, but they'redull-edged and don't pierce the skin of the bull (which is six times thicker than human skin).</p><p> Bullsget a score just like the rider.</p><p> In fact, the better and more aggressive the bull, the better score arider who stays on can achieve.</p><p> And even if the rider is bucked off, the bull still gets a score -they're competing for Bull of the Year, just like the riders are competing for their own top title.Bulls win huge prizes at the end of the season and have fans who root for them.</p><p> They're treated asthe valuable steers they are and put out to pasture after their career is over.</p><p> Now hardcore PETAfans won't ever be satisfied, but most animal lovers will find the PBR celebrates the bulls, capturestheir personalities and treats them humanely. 3.</p><p> Bull riding is perhaps the only professional sport where no one is guaranteed a payday; not the rookies and not even the superstars -- "This is the only professional sport where we don't get paid just to show up," says Cates, an Arkansas native who is going to college in Texas and studying ag business while riding on the tour. "You have to ride and you have to win towalk away with money.</p><p> That's why you see guys riding with broke ribs and broke legs and stuff likethat - because they have families to feed.</p><p> You're not going to get paid unless you're out here puttingyour life on the line riding bulls." 4.</p><p> It's all-American -- They're cowboys, for gosh sakes.</p><p> And just like the story of the West isn't lily white but filled with immigrants and people of color, the PBR is just as diverse.</p><p> Competitors come from all over the world.</p><p> Some are like the hardscrabble boxers of the 1950s, people who see asport like this as their only way out of poverty.</p><p> Others are stars in their own country drawn by thelure of the US, the way Japanese baseball players come to the majors.</p><p> Currently, some of thebiggest names in the sport come from Brazil, including the most-decorated rider in PBR historyAdriano Moraes (who is retiring) and Guilherme Marchi, the most consistent rider of the last fouryears and the man poised to win the World Title in Vegas.</p><p> You'll see ads for trailer hitches andJack Daniels and smokeless tobacco if you go to a PBR event in person.</p><p> But before you make anyassumptions about the audience, listen to them cheer for Marchi or some of the other Brazilians (afew of whom speak as little English as other cowboys speak Portuguese). "The only word inPortuguese I know is 'oi,'" admits Cates. "And I don't even know what that means."
