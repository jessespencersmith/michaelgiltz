Saturday, February 16, 2019  FILM ‘Beale Street’: One Of The Best Films of 1959 Guess Who’s Not Coming To Dinner?  December 18, 2018 Michael Giltz Everyone deserves to see films about themselves, films starring beautiful people that tell their stories with dignity and respect.</p><p> Such movies play an important role.</p><p> We all want to be seen.</p><p> Yet this noble, self-consciousand pious movie feels about 60 years too late.</p><p> If this were 1959, Stanley Kramer were the director and the saintly Sidney Poitier was the star, If Beale Street Could Talk would be applauded as a landmark.</p><p> Today? It will still be applauded, but probably with polite indifference.</p><p> Of course, it couldn’t be made in 1959 since director Barry Jenkins based this new film on the fine 1974 novel by James Baldwin.</p><p> In it, we witness a story of pure devotion between childhood sweethearts. (It’s a pity Jenkinsdidn’t tackle the far more vital, complicated and brilliant Baldwin novel, Go Tell It On The Mountain.) In Beale Street, Tish (KiKi Layne ) is a 19-year-old girl and the narrator.</p><p> She is definitely a girl and not yet a woman, as innocent and naïve as a young black woman from modest means can be in America.</p><p> Fonny (Stephan James ) is her man.</p><p> The movie begins with a storybook glow as they declare their love.I’ll get you a nomination.</p><p> I promise.That moment takes place in a New York City positively alight with their wholesome and beautiful nature.</p><p> They glide through streets in tasteful clothes, dine in restaurants where Fonny chats casually in another language with his waiter friend Pedrocito (Diego Luna) and discover a loft space that a genial landlord (Dave Franco) willactually rent to a young black couple.</p><p> For a moment they can see the American Dream coming true right beforetheir eyes.</p><p> IF BEALE STREET COULD TALK (2/5 stars) Directed by: Barry Jenkins Written by: Barry Jenkins, based on the novel by James Baldwin Starring: KiKi Layne, Stephan James, Regina King, Brian Tyree Henry Running time: 117 min.</p><p> That dream derails when a Puerto Rican woman wrongly accuses Fonny of rape.</p><p> A racist cop is to blame for the set-up.</p><p> You immediately know he’s racist because the film adores beauty and this sneering cop has pock-markedskin.</p><p> Fonny goes to jail, Tish goes to work, her family rallies to hire a lawyer and the baby is born in one of thesimplest (and cleanest) deliveries in recent cinema history.</p><p> Jenkins has followed up his welcome, Oscar-winning triumph in Moonlight with this dream project.</p><p> It includes many of the same features: a production design that would make Douglas Sirk proud and a visual strategy where actors speaking to one another directly address the camera and thus speak directly to us.</p><p> And it’s all a little…polite.</p><p> The lovers are so wholesome it’s a surprise to realize they’ve made a baby.</p><p> Indeed, the scene where they finally consummate their lifelong romance is helplessly timid.</p><p> Layne and James aregorgeous people, so when James takes off his shirt one might feel a rush of pleasure.</p><p> Except they’re so shyyou’re worried they won’t know what to do.</p><p> He looks down bashfully, she demurely removes her own shirt andthey’re so decorous, so tentative, so Adam-and-Eve innocent about it all that you keep waiting for the scene tofade to black.</p><p> It’s a sanctifying strategy.</p><p> But we don’t need saints any more; we don’t need Sidney Poitier to be the perfectNegro.</p><p> That Sunday-best demeanor drains the story ofvitality.</p><p> The novel has two set pieces I anticipated: a meeting of the lovers’ two families when Tish announces she’spregnant and a dark monologue by Fonny’s best off.</p><p> The second works a little better, but while Henry does his best, it’s far too discreet to really convey the menace of the book at that moment.</p><p> In a desire to inject some cold reality, Jenkins breaks up the action with historical photos of infamous discrimination against people of color.</p><p> It’s like a splash of cold water.</p><p> And it seems to come from a different
