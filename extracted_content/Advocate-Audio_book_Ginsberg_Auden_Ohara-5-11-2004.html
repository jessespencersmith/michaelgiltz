No wonder, then, that his style is rather flat and affect­ less, fighting against the humor and vital drama of pieces like "Poem (Lana Turner Has Collapsed!)" and "The Day Lady Died." Auden is as patrician as any British poet should be. He may have written about cornflakes or, in "The Common Life," talked about "our roll-call of persons we would least like to go to bed with," but his is the voice of the old man of letters, not the young rebel. (And, no, the poem from Four Weddings and a Funeral is not heard in this series.) Ginsberg is the best reader of the three. He loved to entertain, loved large crowds, and loved the sound of his own voice.
