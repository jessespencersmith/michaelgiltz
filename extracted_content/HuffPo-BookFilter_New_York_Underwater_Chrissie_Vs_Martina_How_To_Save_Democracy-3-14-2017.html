Explore The Seven New Books We Tracked Down!*************** NEW YORK 2140 by Kim Stanley RobinsonKim Stanley Robinson is one of our boldest and most engaging writers of science fiction. Here he's headed to New York City after two catastrophically historic rises in sea levels have unintentionally turned Manhattan into a new and dangerous Venice. As with any good writer, Robinson creates vivid characters and stories that draw us in, from the police chief looking into the disappearance of two coders to the superintendent of a building constantly fighting off the threat of flooding to a Wall Street guy who might just get a little altruistic to woo a woman he likes. But as with the best of sci-fi writers dealing in hard science, Robinson has thoughtfully extrapolated what conditions science predicts in our near future with the ramifications of what that might mean for how people live and struggle and thrive. Human nature doesn't change and good writers like Robinson know that.They also know sci-fi can open our eyes to the possibilities (and threats) ahead while proving that people are always lusty and kind and greedy and scared and fascinating, no matter what world they live in..... MARTINA & CHRISSIE by Phil Bildner, illus by Brett Helquist How often do we get to share a picture book about someone we know, people we've met? Not very often, of course! But this picture book about the tennis rivalry and personal friendship between Chrissie Evert and Martina Navratilova is our big chance. We grew up playing tennis at the courts managed by Chrissie's dad, played Chrissie's little sister (and lost virtually every time) and as a ballboy watched these two legends first meet, play often and raise each other's games. It may be a stretch to call if "The Greatest Rivalry In The History Of Sports," but it's undoubtedly ONE of the greatest rivalries. The team behind it show their contrasting styles, how they made each other better and the respect they maintained even as their competition grew fiercer with each passing year.... Read More. We Predict 16 New Titles Ready For Reading In Your Future! *************** THE CRISIS OF THE MIDDLE-CLASS CONSTIUTION by Ganesh SitaramanThis work by a protege of Senator Elizabeth Warren has three intriguing sections which don't quite mesh but provide a wealth of information. One, author Ganesh Sitaraman shows how philosophers and political theorists have always worried about inequality. Two, Sitaraman shows how the US has a system of government that ONLY works with a strong and indeed dominant middle class. For many reasons, the rich weren't too rich for a hundred years and -- thanks to the Great Depression and WW II -- we had another three or four decades with everyone doing better. But that was a fluke, not the natural state of things in the world. Now we face inequality and the richer some get, the more political power they get and thus the richer they get and the more powerful...you get the idea. In the final section, Sitaraman runs through the many examples of how inequality is perverting our government and politics and the culture in general and points to some prescriptive solutions.... YVAIN: THE KNIGHT OF THE LION by M.T. Anderson, illus by Andrea Offermann M.T. Anderson is a shape-shifter of a novelist, going from sci-fi to historical fiction to biography with ease. Now he leaps to comic books and the result is as satisfying as usual. Yvain is a reimagining of a tale of the Knights of the Round Table, as popularized in the epic poem by Chrétien de Troyes. In it, our questionably young hero goes on impulsive quests, behaves like a buffoon, berates himself for it and vows to do better, over and over again. On one level, it's a straightforward story about a bold knight who wins and woos his love after much trial and tribulation; on another level it very much calls into question all the chivalric nonsense surrounding brave men and the helpless women they rescue. The illustrations are by Andrea Offermann and this is a true collaboration. The artwork and the words are in constant tension, with the images often illuminating certain ideas or flatly contradicting the "official" action with a haunted look here and resigned misery there. Yvain is, frankly, a bit of an ass, especially when falling in love at first sight with the lady whose husband he has just slain in battle. The result is a notably nuanced tale where villains are vanquished, but so are any quaint ideas about right and wrong, pure knights of good intention and fragile women. It's deft, beautifully drawn and wholly satisfying..... Read More. Love Comic Books?
