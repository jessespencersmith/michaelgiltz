Poet Dylan Thomas recorded his tour de force reading of that remarkable short story "A Child's Christmas In Wales" on February 22, 1952 at New York City's Chelsea Hotel. Thank two pioneering women! Barbara Holdridge and Marianne Mantell were convinced there was a market in the US for audio recordings of poetry. They couldn't find any takers for Shakespearean recitals or medieval music so they focused on poets reading from their own work. They dug into their own pockets and launched the famed label Caedmon Audio with Thomas's vibrant tale of his charming childhood memories. With this bold gamble, Holdridge and Mantell planted the seeds for audio recordings of books, short stories, poems and radio plays ever since, a genre that is bigger than ever and one of the few areas of growth in the book industry. However, it wasn't the first time Thomas was recorded. Back in the UK, the BBC lied to the notoriously unreliable man and said he's have to pre-record a radio show that would normally be performed live. And no wonder: In 1952, Thomas showed up at the Chelsea Hotel drunk, refused to do the poems he'd agreed upon and insisted he do "A Child's Christmas In Wales" instead and no he didn't have a copy of it so someone would damn well have to find one! The result? A masterpiece of performance. ***************

CONVERGENCE by Peter Watson Centuries ago, a person could be conversant in all the sciences by having a cabinet of curiosities, building a telescope and dabbling in this or experimenting in that. No more. What did chemistry have to do with astronomy anyway? Science seemed to have splintered. Now it's coming together once again. In this fascinating work, Peter Watson demonstrates how so many disparate areas of enquiry have become interlinked in the past 150 years, with the Theory Of Evolution, the insight of the conservation of energy and more. Each new wave of breakthroughs seems to turn all those disciplines into one web of knowledge. Watson dubs it the Convergence and with so many examples to draw upon, it's hard to argue with as a defining principle. It's a sweeping tale filled with mini-dramas, concise portraits of many scientists and a way of making complex ideas graspable by a layperson like myself.... Read More. Love Science & Nature? DIRECT ACTION by L.A. Kauffman "What happened to the American left after the Sixties?" That is the question that has bedeviled activist and journalist L.A. Kaufmann, who has been on the ground at protest movements her entire life, ranging from anti-apartheid to ACT UP/NY to Earth First! and beyond. She's wrestled with that query for years and the result is this scholarly work. Instead of a splintering of the left from its anti-Vietnam focus to a hundred different issues, Kaufmann paints a history of direct action. She sees its roots stretching far back but marks the beginning in the modern era to the MayDay protest in DC on May 3, 1971 right up to #BlackLivesMatter. It's thoughtful and interesting and ultimately hopeful for those who imagined the days of radical activism were stuck in the distant past.... Read More. Love Current Events? Take Action & Check Out Our 15 Picks For The Best New Releases Of The Week! *************** THE WORLD TO COME: STORIES by Jim Shepard

Author Jim Shepard's most recent novel was the gripping heart-breaker "The Book Of Aron," easily one of our favorites of 2015. Now he's returned with a new collection of short stories. They're built around real-lifeevents of overwhelming urgency, from French balloonists pushing the envelope on manned flight to pioneer housewives looking to forge a connection amidst harsh isolation to explorers testing their limits at the Arctic Circle. I've only read the first story, a corker of dreadful tension about the true story of a military disaster in peacetime that was wholly preventable. Delivered in a tone of reserve that's almost maddening as the tension rises, it captures the idiotic bureaucracy that stranded good men on a rickety rig in the Atlantic, the painful frustration of their loved ones back home and the nightmare of a storm of the century that dooms them all. Like all of Jim Shepard's work, it feels wholly original and yet of a piece with everything else he's done before.... Read More. Love Fiction? We've Chosen 24 New Releases!
