But there the audience was, faithful fans who weren't just there to hear "If You Leave," which was placed seven songs in on the 21 song set list -- not so early that they wanted to get it out of the way, but early enough to show they were confident in their songbook to keep the energy going. That they did, with leaders Andy McCluskey and Paul Humphreys repeatedly saying how much they appreciated the turnout and promising to come back again very, very soon. If anything, OMD proves that credibility is elastic. They came into prominence riding a wave of UK synth-pop acts, with OMD leaningtowards the darker, Kraftwerk and Brian Eno side rather than the more Motown-ish feel of, say, HumanLeague. With songs name-checking the plane that dropped the bomb on Hiroshima and Tesla, theybacked up their hooks with smarts. They were... cool. Then along came John Hughes. He was applauded for having excellent taste and actually breaking new acts with the soundtracks to his hit films about teenagers And yet, when Hughes helped give acts a hit song, their coolness plummeted immediately: Simple Minds went from a critic's darling to a stolid popmachine with pretensions of U2, Psychedelic Furs seemed defanged and OMD was seen as just anotherpop act from the UK. A few Top 20 hits followed in the US, but everyone soon went their own ways. A pop hit is a wonderful thing. I told one friend about the concert and he revealed that "If You Leave" wasthe song for he and his girlfriend in high school... until they got into a fight with another couple who wasfriends with them but insisted that the OMD tune was their song. My friend Jamie, who cajoled me into going to the show, said one of his first concerts was seeing OMD open for Thompson Twins at MadisonSquare Garden at the peak of fame for both of them. And another friend from Minnesota who saw mytweet about attending immediately texted the lyrics and wondered if James Spader was wanderingaround wearing a polo shirt with an up-turned collar. He too was amazed when I sent a photo showing the several thousand people filling the venue. And the best acts can survive a hit. McCluskey danced with all the dorky abandon of David Byrne at his art-rockiest and then made fun of it. But he also knew how to strike a pose at the end of a tune and drawout more applause. After a patch of slow tunes, he said everyone needed to start moving. "It iscompulsory; you will be ejected if you don't dance." Like old pros, they took pleasure in starting off theset with a new song, "New Babies, New Toys." The title track received some of the warmest applause ofthe night and they introduced another new tune -- "Sister Mary Says" -- by saying, don't worry, it'sactually quite good. My favorite moment was the moody, quiet tune "New Holy Ground," with McClusky half in shadows and the music sounding like it was rising up from the bottom of the ocean. But the entire set was more fun than expected by this casual fan who had nothing more than the new album and a greatest hits set on his shelf. In a way, OMD is more fun live. Their sound on earlier albums might be a bit dry and poppy, but in a show with a live drummer (in this case original bandmember Malcolm Holmes, not to mention Martin Cooper on keyboards) and the warmth of imperfections to give their tunes a humanness, songs like"Souvenir," "Sailing On The Seven Seas" and "Joan Of Arc" (all Top 5 hits in the UK) sounded better thanever. It helps when a few thousand fans are cheering you along. Here's a link to their upcoming shows inthe US and Europe. Thanks for reading.
