Orson Welles and Joan Fontaine in the Charlotte Bronte classic that you never wanted to read in high school. Fox Video, $19.98; laserdisc, $49.98. * The Best Intentions. Ing­ mar Bergman "retired" after making Fanny and Alexander, de­ claring it his last movie as a direc­ tor. Well, sort of. Since then he's done work for TV and theater, pub­ lished his memoirs, and plumbed the relationship of his mother and father in a novel and screenplay, both titled The Best Intentions. As directed by Bille August, the movie's a clear-eyed but moving story of the courtship and early years of marriage between Henrik and Anna Bergman. From the shocking opener-in which Henrik coldly refuses to see his dying grandmother-to the tentative, ambiguous ending, there's not a trace of sentimentality or roman­ ticism in it. Obviously, Bergman's only intention was to tell his par­ ents' story truthfully. He served them well. Academy Elite, $89.95; subtitled. MICHAEL GILTZ discretion, Bergman-style.
