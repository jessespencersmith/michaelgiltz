DINNER WITH DIMAGGIO by Dr. Rock Positano and John Positano My favorite story about the enigmatic Yankee Clipper involves the Simon & Garfunkel song "Mrs. Robinson." It famously name-checked Joe DiMaggio, who met Paul Simon and awkwardly asked him what the hell if meant and was it criticizing him? Simon assured him it wasn't and DiMaggio was mollified. A little. Always insisting on being introduced last at Yankee Stadium -- and always as "the greatest living Yankee" -- DiMaggio came from a very different era, when celebrities weren't expected to pour out their hearts. The Italian was especially private, which made his marriage to Marilyn Monroe all the more of a train wreck. He kept the world at arm's length. But this memoir of a long-time friend promising to offer a more intimate, albeit warm and hardly dishy look at what the man was really like...at least later in life...when his guard was down...sort of.... ALIENS: ON THE SEARCH FOR E.T. edited by Jim Al-Khalili This wide-ranging collection of essays by top scientists has multiple personalities, but I enjoyed them all. Some pieces are amusingly silly, like a run-down of how aliens are depicted in movies and a drolly straightforward recitation of the basics about some of the most famous claims of close encounters. Others are vaguely speculative about science. And most are rooted in the latest facts and offer intelligent summaries of what we know, why it's silly (or smart) to listen for radio signals, why studying the consciousness of the octopus is good practice for understanding aliens and so on. I'm not sure who the audience might be for this, but as a sci-fi reading buff who enjoys hearing the latest discoveries in science, I was right at home.... Read More. Plauen This acclaimed German comic strip flourished for three or so years in the mid-1930s, a simple and sweet (and mostly wordless) celebration of a father and son's many antics. Its author was despised by the Nazis for satirizing Hitler and others. Blackballed, he continued to work under pseudonyms, but in 1940 (after WW II had begun) sealed his fate by diving back into political satire. Plauen's legacy remains this influential and

thoroughly unpolitical work, but the tragic nature of his death by suicide in 1944 (just as he was about to be condemned to death) adds a plaintive note to what should be a strip much better known in the West....
